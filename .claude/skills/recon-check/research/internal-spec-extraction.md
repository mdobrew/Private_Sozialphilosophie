## PART 1 — Rubric dimensions

### A. The four parts (substance)

**1. Claim precision (part i)**
- **Source:** reading_practices.md, Practice A, part (i)
- **Tier:** Both (Light includes claim)
- **What good looks like:** Thesis stated in 1–2 precise sentences at the grain of the document's own example ("Honneth argues that the formation of an undamaged self-relation (*intaktes Selbstverhältnis*) requires three structurally distinct forms of intersubjective recognition…"). Demarcation cue: *"if you can substitute the author's name with another's and the sentence still works, you have not stated the claim precisely enough."*
- **Typical failure mode:** A claim so loose it is author-interchangeable ("Honneth argues that self-realisation depends on three forms of recognition" is explicitly flagged as "too loose").

**2. Claim ≠ definition / section heading (Dense-text corollary of 1)**
- **Source:** reading_practices.md, Appendix D.2, part (i)
- **Tier:** Both (bites hardest on Protocol-2/Dense texts)
- **What good looks like:** The claim states what the *move* establishes, in the reader's own words. Test from D.2: *"if your sentence could be swapped for the §142 definition or the section heading, it is not yet your reconstruction."*
- **Typical failure mode:** Restating the author's definition or the chapter title as if it were the reconstructed thesis.

**3. Support: premises, evidence, and mode of inference identified (part ii)**
- **Source:** reading_practices.md, Practice A, part (ii)
- **Tier:** Full only
- **What good looks like:** Identifies premises, evidence, *and* the mode of inference (transcendental argument, empirical generalisation, conceptual analysis, normative reconstruction, genealogical derivation) — "identifying the mode of inference is where you catch weaknesses."
- **Typical failure mode:** Listing what the author says without naming *how* the inference is supposed to work.

**4. Mixed-mode disentangling (sub-dimension of 3)**
- **Source:** reading_practices.md, Practice A, part (ii) (Jaeggi example)
- **Tier:** Full only
- **What good looks like:** Where the argument combines kinds of support without flagging the transitions (Jaeggi: a conceptual claim + an empirical presupposition + a normative claim), the reconstruction separates them and registers that "these are distinct kinds of support and can fail independently."
- **Typical failure mode:** Flattening a layered argument into one homogeneous chain, hiding independently failable steps.

**5. Support/presupposition demarcation**
- **Source:** reading_practices.md, Practice A, parts (ii)/(iv) demarcation cues
- **Tier:** Full only
- **What good looks like:** Cue (ii): *"support is what the author gives you; presupposition (part iv) is what they do not and need."* Cue (iv): *"a presupposition is not a missing premise the author forgot — it is a commitment they would have to accept if asked, but never explicitly state."*
- **Typical failure mode:** The document names it directly: "(This is the part most easily conflated with (ii) by a reader trained on formal arguments; resist that conflation.)"

**6. Directed-against: at least one named opponent (part iii)**
- **Source:** reading_practices.md, Practice A, part (iii)
- **Tier:** Both (Light includes directed-against)
- **What good looks like:** Demarcation cue: *"name at least one opponent. If none, the claim is either uncontroversial or you have not found the right framing."* "If you can't identify what the author is rejecting, you haven't understood the claim."
- **Typical failure mode:** No opponent, a vague opponent ("critics"), or the wrong opponent (CLAUDE.md: "directed at the wrong opponent" is one of the three things Claude is told to name).

**7. Multiple-opponent differentiation (sub-dimension of 6)**
- **Source:** reading_practices.md, Practice A, part (iii) (Jaeggi example: liberal neutrality *and* relativism *and* moral perfectionism)
- **Tier:** Full mainly; credit at Light where present
- **What good looks like:** Where there are several targets, they are distinguished, and the reconstruction registers that "these are three different opponents, and the argument works differently against each."
- **Typical failure mode:** Collapsing distinct opponents into one generic foil, so the argument's differential force is lost.

**8. Presuppositions identified and warranted (part iv)**
- **Source:** reading_practices.md, Practice A, part (iv) + pointing constraint
- **Tier:** Full only
- **What good looks like:** Unstated, load-bearing commitments the author "would *have to* accept if asked," each passing the counterfactual necessity test — "you can show the stated argument collapses without it." Productive presuppositions link forward (e.g., D.1's (iv): what Zurn later clarifies, what *Das Recht der Freiheit* later articulates).
- **Typical failure mode:** "A 'presupposition' that fails the necessity test is not a presupposition but your own objection in disguise; route it to Practice H rather than filing it as the author's commitment."

### B. Reconstruction discipline (governing rules)

**9. Rational reconstruction — strongest argument the text will bear**
- **Source:** reading_practices.md, "The reconstruction discipline: rational reconstruction and the pointing constraint"
- **Tier:** Both
- **What good looks like:** Neither psychological intention nor prose paraphrase, but "the *strongest valid argument the text will bear*" (Rawls's "I assumed the writers were smarter than I was"; Habermas's *rationale Rekonstruktion*). "Where the text underdetermines the argument, charity fills the gap with the strongest premise the text *permits*; it never licenses importing a premise the text excludes."
- **Typical failure mode:** Either a weak/uncharitable version that is easy to knock down (strawman) or over-charity that imports premises the text excludes.

**10. Own-words requirement (not paraphrase/transcription)**
- **Source:** reading_practices.md, Practice A opening ("in your own words, not paraphrase") + discipline section
- **Tier:** Both
- **What good looks like:** "The deliverable is a paraphrase you can attack, not a faithful transcript." Structure is your-words; only terms and specifics are their-words.
- **Typical failure mode:** Transcription/close paraphrase of the author's prose — "that is transcription, and it feeds the fluency illusion."

**11. Reconstruction, not summary (structure over text-order)**
- **Source:** reading_practices.md, Protocol 1, "Why not take running notes"
- **Tier:** Both
- **What good looks like:** "An identification of the argument's structure, which may not follow the text's order," answering "What is this author trying to establish, how, and against whom?"
- **Typical failure mode:** "A *summary* (a compression of what the author said, tracking the text's order)" — a note that tracks the chapter's page-order is the diagnostic symptom.

**12. The pointing constraint (anti-strawman warrant for every attribution)**
- **Source:** reading_practices.md, discipline section, "The pointing constraint"
- **Tier:** Both (applies to whatever parts the tier includes)
- **What good looks like:** "Every element you attribute to the author must be either (a) *textually anchored* — you can point to the passage it comes from — or (b) *inferentially necessary* — you can show the stated argument collapses without it (the counterfactual test)." Asserted premises are anchored; presuppositions are warranted by necessity.
- **Typical failure mode:** "Anything that is neither is something *you* supplied, however plausible: that is the line between reconstruction and free-association."

**13. Premise attribution by source (asserted / attributed / presupposed)**
- **Source:** reading_practices.md, discipline section, "Premise attribution"
- **Tier:** Full (support part); critical on immanent-critique texts (Marx, Jaeggi)
- **What good looks like:** "Tag each premise by source — *asserted* by the author, *attributed* to the target (the norm the object professes: the engine of immanent critique…), or *presupposed* (unstated, load-bearing)."
- **Typical failure mode:** "Marking a target's professed norm as though it were the author's own commitment inverts the argument and is the commonest reconstruction error on immanent-critique texts."

**14. Closed-book on structure, open-book on detail**
- **Source:** reading_practices.md, discipline section, final rule; Protocols 1–3
- **Tier:** Both (process criterion; leaves textual traces)
- **What good looks like:** "The *skeleton* — the claim and the inferential spine — must be reconstructible with the book shut… It does *not* mean recalling which example the author used, the exact technical term, or the section number — reopen freely for those."
- **Typical failure mode:** Either an open-book "reconstruction" that is really a summary of marginalia, or wasted memorisation pressure on details (examples, section numbers) that should be looked up.

**15. Same-week retrieval integrity (degradation ladder)**
- **Source:** reading_practices.md, Time allocation, "Practice debt and the degradation floor" (a)
- **Tier:** Both
- **What good looks like:** The filed note (at whatever tier) is written "in the same week as the read — never deferred to a later week, and never nothing." If Full time is unavailable, degrade to Light (claim + directed-against, 150 words, 10 minutes) same week.
- **Typical failure mode:** "A reconstruction shifted two weeks is no longer a memory test but an essay about your marginalia"; or catch-up reconstructions of texts read weeks ago.

**16. Voice separation: reconstructive voice vs. own judgment**
- **Source:** reading_practices.md, closing-sentence rule + Practice J ("What a Skizze is not") + pointing constraint routing
- **Tier:** Both
- **What good looks like:** The four parts stay in the reconstructive voice ("Honneth argues that…"); the reader's own judgment lives only in the closing sentence, "a record of *your own* judgments, distinct from the reconstructive voice." Objections route to Practice H.
- **Typical failure mode:** Evaluation leaking into the four parts — critique disguised as reconstruction, or presuppositions that are really objections.

### C. Format, length, and embedded components

**17. Word-count and compression discipline**
- **Source:** reading_practices.md, Practice A "Target length" + "On word counts for orientation"
- **Tier:** Full: 500–800 words per chapter/major section; Light: 200–300 words per text
- **What good looks like:** "These are not essays; they are compressed, high-density notes-to-self." Full ≈ 1–2 single-spaced pages readable aloud in 3–5 minutes; Light ≈ "a single dense paragraph."
- **Typical failure mode:** Essayistic sprawl (padding, throat-clearing) or a note too thin to test retrieval. (Note internal variants: §C.2 α-coverage says "150–250w Light"; the degradation-floor Light note is 150 words — the canonical tier table says 200–300.)

**18. Full-tier completeness (all required components present)**
- **Source:** reading_practices.md, "When and at what depth" tier table
- **Tier:** Full only
- **What good looks like:** "Full four-part structure (claim / support / directed-against / presuppositions) + forward-looking closing sentence + `## Empirical presuppositions` sub-heading + 2–3 C1 seminar questions filed at the bottom of the same note."
- **Typical failure mode:** Four parts present but the closing sentence, empirical-presuppositions sub-heading, or C1 questions silently missing.

**19. Closing sentence — the forward-looking move**
- **Source:** reading_practices.md, "One closing sentence — the forward-looking move"
- **Tier:** Full required; Light optional
- **What good looks like:** One sentence in the form *"If I were making this argument, the move I would change is X."* — "Be specific — name a premise, a method, an example, or a directed-against opponent." Not yet an evaluation; requires no defence. (Marginalia `[!]` marks are its read-time candidates.)
- **Typical failure mode:** Generic dissent ("I'm not fully convinced by X's framework") — at Light tier explicitly: "do not force generic dissent"; or the sentence becomes a defended mini-evaluation (that is Practice H).

**20. Empirical presuppositions sub-heading**
- **Source:** reading_practices.md, tier table + Practice H2 "Exception — component (ii) from the start"; tag `#empirical-presuppositions` (Appendix A)
- **Tier:** Full required (sub-heading); Light "add inline if salient"
- **What good looks like:** Identifies "what empirical claim is being presupposed" — practised from the first module because the economist's training makes it "immediately productive" (Hegel on the *Pöbel*, Marx on wage labour); feeds later H2 evaluations.
- **Typical failure mode:** Sub-heading missing, or filled with normative/conceptual points rather than genuinely empirical presuppositions.

**21. C1 seminar questions (embedded, Full tier)**
- **Source:** reading_practices.md, Practice C1 + tier table + Appendix D.4
- **Tier:** Full: 2–3 required, filed at bottom of same note; Light: "file if a tension surfaces"
- **What good looks like:** "A genuine tension, ambiguity, or productive difficulty *within the author's own framework*"; format "a short setup (2–3 sentences stating the tension) followed by the question" — calibrate against D.4 (Jaeggi circularity, Honneth autonomy-criterion examples). Post-M13 taper: only where a substantive tension can be named.
- **Typical failure mode:** "Things I didn't understand" dressed as questions — confusion (resolved by re-reading) mistaken for a genuine philosophical problem; or pro-forma questions as busy-work.

**22. Open difficulties flagged, not forced**
- **Source:** reading_practices.md, "Don't try to resolve every difficulty on first encounter" + tpl-reconstruction `## Open difficulties` + D.2
- **Tier:** Both
- **What good looks like:** "Note the difficulty (in your reconstruction: 'I don't see how step X follows from step Y'), move on, and return to it when a later text illuminates it." D.2: mark gaps explicitly ("I can't yet say why morality is insufficient on its own terms") and mark confidence levels.
- **Typical failure mode:** "Premature resolution — forcing a text to make sense before you have the subsequent resources" — or, conversely, silently papering over an unparsed step.

**23. Secondary-literature provenance marked**
- **Source:** reading_practices.md, Appendix D.2, part (iv)
- **Tier:** Full (esp. Protocol-2 texts read with [ES] secondary alongside)
- **What good looks like:** "Mark which you can defend from §§142–157 and which you are importing from secondary reading (Neuhouser)."
- **Typical failure mode:** Neuhouser's (or McCarthy's, Freyenhagen's) reading silently presented as what the primary text itself warrants.

**24. Key passages: pointer + gloss, not verbatim capture**
- **Source:** reading_practices.md, L.12 step 3, tpl-reconstruction `## Key passages`, Discipline "Don't take extensive verbatim notes"
- **Tier:** Full
- **What good looks like:** "Page references + one-sentence gloss of why each matters"; a quotation file of "5–10 key passages per major text."
- **Typical failure mode:** "A notebook that reproduces half the book" — verbatim copying substituting for intellectual work.

**25. Translation notes where triggered (Practice G hook)**
- **Source:** reading_practices.md, Practice G "Practical implementation" + tpl-reconstruction `## Translation notes`
- **Tier:** Both, ad hoc ("only when relevant")
- **What good looks like:** "Note significant translation discrepancies in your Practice A reconstruction with a brief gloss: 'Ashton translates X as Y; the German Z carries the additional connotation of W.'"
- **Typical failure mode:** Building an argument on an English rendering that flattens the German (*Verstand*/*Vernunft* both as "reason"; *Sittlichkeit*/*Moralität* contrast lost) without checking.

**26. Filing and template conventions**
- **Source:** reading_practices.md §L.2/L.3/L.12; CLAUDE.md Obsidian section
- **Tier:** Both
- **What good looks like:** One file per chapter/major section (Full) or per text (Light), titled by source (`Honneth-KuA-ch1.md`), four components under markdown headers (`## Claim`, `## Support`, `## Directed against`, `## Presuppositions`), frontmatter per `tpl-reconstruction.md` (type, module, author, work, work_short, chapter, bibtex_key, protocol, status, tags from the stable ≤30-tag vocabulary), C1 questions at the bottom of the same file, `[[concept]]` links to touched Practice B entries.
- **Typical failure mode:** Invented frontmatter fields or tags; seminar questions split into a separate file; missing concept links.

**27. Correct unit of reconstruction**
- **Source:** reading_practices.md, "When and at what depth — Definitions"
- **Tier:** Full per chapter/major section; Light per text
- **What good looks like:** "A 'major section' is a Roman-numeral or §-numbered division that the author themselves treats as a self-contained argumentative unit… When in doubt, one note per chapter; subdivide further only if a chapter exceeds ~80 pages." Light: "one filed note at the end, not running notes folded into a later artifact."
- **Typical failure mode:** Light-tier notes ballooning to per-chapter (untracked scope creep) or a Full note spanning units too large to reconstruct.

**28. Correct tier for the text**
- **Source:** reading_practices.md, tier-routing table + "The execution plan is canonical for tier assignment"
- **Tier:** — (meta)
- **What good looks like:** [P*]/[ES*] → Full; [P]/[ES] → Light; [O*]/[O]/[RS] → Orientation (reading-log line only); [NE] → out of scope. The execution plan's Inventory Tier column overrides the default; "silent deviation breaks the practices ↔ bridge ↔ plan triangle."
- **Typical failure mode:** Full-tier effort spent on an Orientation text, or a MUST-Anchor text filed at a silently degraded tier.

### D. Protocol-sensitive calibration (evaluator must adjust expectations)

**29. Protocol 2a format for aphoristic/paratactic texts**
- **Source:** reading_practices.md, Protocol 2a
- **Tier:** Full/Light on flagged texts (*Minima Moralia* clusters, *Dialektik der Aufklärung*, Benjamin's Thesen)
- **What good looks like:** The four parts become: (i) the phenomenon or figure the cluster circles (not a thesis); (ii) the target, named "as precisely as a Protocol-1 part (iii)"; (iii) 2–3 load-bearing sentences located by page, "pointed to and quoted, not paraphrased into a system"; (iv) "what the form itself refuses to say and why the refusal is the point." Closing-sentence rule applies unchanged.
- **Typical failure mode:** Applying the claim/support template — it "enacts precisely the *Identitätsdenken* these texts refuse" and produces "either a falsifying flattening or [the conclusion that] they have failed at reading; neither is true."

**30. Dense/Protocol-2 thinness is not failure**
- **Source:** reading_practices.md, Protocol 2 time calibration; D.2 warning box; L.2 `protocol` field rationale
- **Tier:** Both
- **What good looks like:** On Hegel/Adorno/Lukács/TKH Bd. 2 ch. VI, support may be "dialectical, not evidential — there are no premises to list"; part (iv) legitimately stays partial until later material arrives ("that incompleteness at §157 is *correct*, not a failure; flag it and move on"). The evaluator calibrates: "Protocol-2 reconstructions are thinner by nature, not by failure."
- **Typical failure mode (of the evaluator):** Penalising a Hegel reconstruction for lacking a thesis-then-reasons structure, or demanding a premise list where the argument is a transition.

**31. Economist's-instinct hazards**
- **Source:** CLAUDE.md "Economist's framing"; reading_practices.md D.2 Economist's note + H1 Translation discipline
- **Tier:** Both
- **What good looks like:** Bridges used only where illuminating; D.2: "resist reading the move to *Sittlichkeit* as preference-aggregation or a social-contract / social-choice construction." "Rational" in the Frankfurt tradition ≈ Aristotelian practical reason, not decision theory; "critique of political economy" is critique of social *form*, not of quantitative magnitudes.
- **Typical failure mode:** Incentive-compatibility/optimisation/rational-choice framing imported into the reconstruction; external objections ("not testable") filed as reconstruction content rather than routed through the H1 Translation move.

**32. Grain calibration against the worked example (D.1)**
- **Source:** reading_practices.md, Appendix D.1 (Honneth, "Pathologien des Sozialen")
- **Tier:** Full
- **What good looks like:** D.1 is the explicit calibration standard for "grain and structure": claim carries the full architecture in ~3 sentences; support names the method by name ("The mode of inference is normative reconstruction") and characterises the evidence type ("primarily textual-interpretive"); directed-against names three targets and distinguishes explicit from implicit ones (Rawls, positivist sociology, "implicitly against Habermas"); presuppositions are lettered (a)–(c), each genuinely unstated-but-necessary, with forward links to where the tradition later addresses them (Zurn 8.3, RdF 7.5).
- **Typical failure mode:** Reconstructions systematically coarser than D.1 (no named mode of inference, one undifferentiated opponent, presuppositions that are restated premises).

## PART 2 — Rules on HOW feedback should be delivered

### Sequencing (reading_practices.md, "Using Claude as an interlocutor — general principles")
- **Governing principle, verbatim:** "after your own written work, never before it." Explaining a chapter before the user's reconstruction "gets a fluent summary that feels like understanding but hasn't produced the neural pathways that constitute understanding."
- **Write-then-compare protocol** (Practice B, applied to C questions and exemplars): the user writes first; Claude/exemplars come after. "If you ask Claude first, your questions will sound like Claude's questions."
- Use 1 timing: "Run the check after the first Full reconstruction (W5 Honneth 'Pathologien') and after first-of-author Full reconstructions thereafter; ad hoc beyond that." The Use 1 check is "part of Practice A's first-instance protocol, not a separate practice."

### The Use 1 prompt and role (reading_practices.md, Claude — Use 1 callout)
- Prompt wording: *"Here is my reconstruction of the argument in chapter X. What have I gotten wrong, what have I missed, and where have I stated something ambiguously?"* — i.e., feedback answers three questions: **wrong / missed / ambiguous**.
- Claude "functions as a knowledgeable interlocutor who checks your output, not as a source of input," and "can identify structural misreadings (**wrong-step attribution, premise/conclusion conflation, missed qualifications**) and gaps." This is the checklist of misreading types the check should target.
- H1 Objection variant of the same protocol: "paste the reconstruction and ask where it misreads the standard scholarly view and which assumption is undefended."

### Posture (CLAUDE.md, "Intellectual posture")
- Default devil's advocate/interlocutor: "Name what is underdefended, conflated, or directed at the wrong opponent." "Surface the strongest opposing reading, not the most charitable one." "Don't soften."
- Name the secondary literature the user is missing, by name: "Neuhouser on Hegel, Heinrich on Marx, Freyenhagen on Adorno, McCarthy on Habermas, Zurn on Honneth."
- Pressure-test "per the four-part Practice A structure (claim / support / directed-against / presuppositions) plus the dialogue-moment objection" — i.e., close with "the strongest objection to the argument you just reconstructed" and expect an answer.
- Tutor mode only "when I am orienting in genuinely new material and explicitly ask for background."

### Uncertainty and hallucination discipline (CLAUDE.md "Uncertainty"; reading_practices.md Use 4 caveat, D.2)
- "Flag uncertainty explicitly… 'I haven't seen the German text of this passage,' 'I'd want to check Neuhouser §X before claiming this.'"
- "Prefer to point to the passage (section/page) and let me verify — don't synthesise confidently from training data." If the question hinges on an unquoted passage: "say 'paste the passage.'"
- Use 4 caveat (applies whenever Claude steelmans or supplies objections): "explicitly require Claude to point to either (a) a specific passage in the author's own work where this defence is made or implied, or (b) a specific secondary author whose defence this is. Treat any uncited defence as Claude's invention."
- On contested texts (D.2): "apply the Use 4 hallucination caveat heavily… treat any confident interpretive claim — Claude's or your own — as a hypothesis to verify against the German text."
- Interpretive disputes (Discipline section): "Claude should flag this kind of dispute rather than resolve it, and you should be suspicious if it doesn't" (e.g., Hegel metaphysician vs. social theorist — Neuhouser vs. Pippin).

### What Claude must NOT do (reading_practices.md "What not to use Claude for"; CLAUDE.md "What not to do")
- "Don't ask Claude to summarise a text you haven't read yet" (only exception: [O]-triage).
- "Don't ask Claude to write your seminar questions or critical evaluations."
- "Don't use Claude as a substitute for re-reading."
- "Don't use Claude to generate Practice B entries" — check-only ("Here is my tracking entry… What have I missed or mischaracterised?").
- CLAUDE.md: "Don't generate plausible-sounding chapter summaries of texts you haven't been given. If I need a reconstruction of a chapter, I'll do it; your job is to critique mine." "Don't paraphrase the bridge document or syllabus back at me."

### Adjacent uses to keep distinct (for scope demarcation in the skill)
- Use 2 (targeted clarification): only *with* the user's best interpretive attempt included — "Am I on the right track?" — never bare "what does this passage mean?"
- Use 5 (language assistance): grammar-only unpacking of tortured German; "Use 2 is for philosophical opacity after grammar is parsed; Use 5 is for grammatical opacity."

### Style of delivery (CLAUDE.md "Writing style" + "Shorthand discipline")
- German terms in italics with English gloss on first use; thereafter German. Cite "author + short title + section/page" ("Hegel, *Rechtsphilosophie* §142," "Honneth, KuA ch. 5").
- "No motivational padding. No 'great question.' No three-bullet summaries appended to substantive replies." One-line "I don't know / I'd need to check" when that is the answer.
- Expand programme shorthand on first use (tier names, DM, QM, W-numbers — never leave a bare W-number ambiguous); decode syllabus item numbers ("Zurn's second-order-disorders article (8.3)"); standard sigla (TKH, DdA, ND, MM, KuA, KvL) may stay unexpanded.
- Preserve [P]/[ES]/[RS]/[O]/[NE]/[MX] markers and [Economist's note] flags if editing documents.

## PART 3 — Exact tier format specs

### Full tier ([P*], [ES*] default; execution-plan Tier column canonical)
- **Unit:** one reconstruction note **per chapter or major section** ("Roman-numeral or §-numbered division that the author themselves treats as a self-contained argumentative unit"; when in doubt one per chapter; subdivide only above ~80 pp).
- **Length:** **500–800 words** per note (≈1–2 single-spaced pages; readable aloud in 3–5 min).
- **Components:** full four-part structure — **(i) Claim, (ii) Support, (iii) Directed against, (iv) Presuppositions** — under those markdown headers, **plus**: forward-looking **closing sentence** (required; "If I were making this argument, the move I would change is X"), **`## Empirical presuppositions` sub-heading**, and **2–3 C1 seminar questions** filed at the bottom of the same note. Template also carries `## Key passages`, `## Translation notes` (when relevant), `## Open difficulties`.
- **Time:** 30–45 min reconstruction-writing component; canonical all-in session close **60–120 min** (§L.12 steps 1–7). Writing hours capped at **2.0 h per Recon** (§C.2 per-Recon ceiling).
- **Protocol 2a variant** (aphoristic texts): four parts become phenomenon-circled / target / 2–3 load-bearing quoted sentences with page refs / what-the-form-refuses; closing sentence unchanged.

### Light tier ([P], [ES] default)
- **Unit:** one reconstruction note **per text (not per chapter)**; a multi-week Light text generates one filed note at the end.
- **Length:** **200–300 words** (≈ half to one page, "a single dense paragraph"). (Internal variants elsewhere in the doc: §C.2 α-coverage "150–250w"; degradation-floor Light note "150 words, 10 minutes".)
- **Components:** **two parts only — claim + directed-against.** No empirical-presuppositions sub-heading required (add inline if salient); no C1 questions required (file if a tension surfaces); **closing sentence optional** ("only where the text actually provokes one; do not force generic dissent").
- **Time:** 10–15 min per Light reconstruction; writing capped at **0.75 h per Recon**.

### Orientation tier ([O*], [O], [RS]; [NE] treated as Orientation if read)
- **No filed reconstruction note.** "A 1–2 sentence entry in the reading log (date, text, 'main point I want to retain') is sufficient — provenance without artifact."

### Degradation ladder (compliance floor, both tiers)
- Full that can't get its writing time → **Light note (claim + directed-against, 150 words, 10 min) in the same week as the read** — "never deferred to a later week, and never nothing." Light → reading-log line. Two+ tier-mandated notes missing at weekly review = practice debt; the sanctioned response is **degradation-in-place, not catch-up writing**. Protection order under degraded compliance: (1) Full reconstructions on MUST-Anchor texts, (2) Anwendung→Skizze production pipeline, (3) Practice B entries on the ~10 load-bearing concepts.
