# recon-check rubric

Synthesized 2026-07-14 from (a) the programme's own documents — `reading_practices.md` (Practice A, reconstruction discipline, tier table, protocols, Appendix D) and project `CLAUDE.md` — and (b) an external research pass over philosophy pedagogy, argumentation theory, and formative-assessment literature. Full provenance: [research/internal-spec-extraction.md](research/internal-spec-extraction.md) and [research/external-rubric-research.md](research/external-rubric-research.md). Internal-document criteria are **binding** (they are the programme's spec); external criteria are design choices and are marked *(ext)*.

Tier tags: **[F]** Full only · **[L]** Light only · **[FL]** both. Apply nothing untagged for the tier at hand.

## Layer 0 — Routing and calibration (check BEFORE evaluating)

| # | Check | Rule |
|---|---|---|
| 0.1 | **Tier** | Execution plan Inventory Tier column is canonical (overrides syllabus-tag default [P*]/[ES*]→Full, [P]/[ES]→Light, [O]/[RS]→Orientation). Orientation texts get no filed note — if one was filed, that's over-investment, note it. |
| 0.2 | **Unit** | Full = one note per chapter / major section (author's own self-contained division; subdivide only >~80 pp). Light = one note per text, filed once at the end. |
| 0.3 | **Format spec** | Full: 500–800 words; four parts under `## Claim / ## Support / ## Directed against / ## Presuppositions`; required closing sentence; `## Empirical presuppositions` sub-heading; 2–3 C1 seminar questions at bottom. Light: 200–300 words; **claim + directed-against only**; closing sentence optional ("do not force generic dissent"); empirical presuppositions inline only if salient. |
| 0.4 | **Protocol calibration** | Protocol 1 (systematic): full expectations. Protocol 2 (dense/dialectical — Hegel, Adorno, Lukács): support may be "dialectical, not evidential — there are no premises to list"; partial part (iv) is *correct*, not deficient; **do not penalize thinness**. Protocol 2a (aphoristic — MM, DdA, Benjamin): the four parts are DIFFERENT — (i) phenomenon/figure circled, (ii) target, (iii) 2–3 load-bearing sentences quoted + located, (iv) what the form refuses to say; applying the claim/support template to these texts is itself the error. Protocol 3 (short programmatic): standard four parts. |
| 0.5 | **Retrieval integrity** | Reconstruction written same week as the read? If it's a catch-up artifact weeks later, note that it tests marginalia-prose, not retrieval — flag, don't punish. |

## Layer 1 — Fidelity (did they read it right?) — highest severity

| # | Dimension | Good looks like | Canonical failure |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1.1 | **Claim accuracy & precision** [FL] | Author's actual main conclusion, at the author's level of ambition, 1–2 sentences at Appendix D.1 grain. Demarcation cue: *if another author's name can be substituted and the sentence still works, it isn't precise enough.* Not a definition, not a section heading, not the topic. | Author-interchangeable claim; sub-conclusion promoted to main claim; nearby-familiar thesis substituted. |
| 1.2 | **Scope / quantifier / modality preservation** [FL] *(ext)* | Hedges, domain restrictions, modal strength survive ("under capitalist conditions", "tends to", necessity vs. typicality). | Over-generalized premises — the top documented reconstruction error; qualified claim universalized; genealogical claim rendered analytic. |
| 1.3 | **Completeness** [F] *(ext + pointing constraint)* | Every premise the conclusion needs is present, including silently carried normative/methodological premises. | Stated premises don't reach the conclusion; gap papered over with "therefore". |
| 1.4 | **Parsimony / pointing constraint** [FL] | Every attributed element is (a) textually anchored or (b) inferentially necessary (counterfactual test). Nothing appears that the author doesn't rely on; examples and rhetoric excluded unless load-bearing. | Free-association: plausible material the *user* supplied; every emphatic sentence treated as a premise. |
| 1.5 | **Premise attribution / voice** [F; spot-check L] | Each premise tagged asserted (author's) / **attributed** (the target's professed norm — engine of immanent critique) / presupposed. Author's elucidation of an opponent not mistaken for the author's view; "contradictions" checked for a change of voice. | **The commonest error on immanent-critique texts (Marx, Jaeggi, Adorno): marking the target's norm as the author's own commitment — this inverts the argument.** |
| 1.6 | **Directed-against: named and correct** [FL] | At least one *named* opponent (cue: "if none, the claim is either uncontroversial or you have not found the right framing"). Multiple opponents differentiated — "the argument works differently against each." Charity is relational *(ext, Lewiński)*: strengthening the author by flattening her opponent is a fidelity failure, not charity. | No opponent; "critics" as opponent; wrong opponent; distinct opponents collapsed into one foil. |
| 1.7 | **Textual grounding** [F; L where claims are contentious] | Load-bearing attributions carry section/page anchors so disputes are adjudicated against the text. | Free-floating attribution; quote-stitching (quotes prove the words occurred, not that they play the assigned inferential role). |

## Layer 2 — Structure (did they render it as an argument?)

| # | Dimension | Good looks like | Canonical failure |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2.1 | **Structure, not summary** [FL] | Exhibits the argument's architecture — which may not follow the text's order. Answers "what is the author trying to establish, how, and against whom?" | Section-by-section compression in text order; a table of contents in prose. The single most likely first-reconstruction failure. |
| 2.2 | **Mode of inference named** [F] | Transcendental / empirical generalisation / conceptual analysis / normative reconstruction / genealogical derivation — named, because "identifying the mode of inference is where you catch weaknesses." | Listing what the author says without naming how the inference works. |
| 2.3 | **Mixed modes disentangled** [F] | Where support combines kinds (conceptual + empirical + normative, unflagged transitions), the reconstruction separates them: "distinct kinds of support that can fail independently." | Layered argument flattened into one homogeneous chain. |
| 2.4 | **Enthymeme discipline** [F] *(ext, Lambert & Ulrich)* | Supplied premises are (a) flagged as supplied and (b) warranted by what the author plausibly presupposes — not by what would make the argument valid. | Silent gap-filling; supplying the premise *you* need; or refusing to fill and declaring invalidity (uncharitable dual). |
| 2.5 | **Transformation audit** [FL] *(ext, pragma-dialectics)* | Diagnostic vocabulary for §2 findings: every reconstruction error is an illegitimate **deletion / addition / substitution / permutation**. Substitution is the highest-risk operation for this user: replacing the author's terms with "clearer" home-idiom terms silently replaces the concept. | Economist-idiom substitution (*Herrschaft* → "market power"; *Rationalität* → decision theory); permutation manufacturing a linear deduction from a dialectical text. |
| 2.6 | **No fallacy-fixing** [FL] *(ext)* | Rational reconstruction = strongest argument **the text will bear**; where the text has a real gap, the reconstruction preserves and flags it. Charity fills underdetermination with the strongest premise the text *permits* — never a premise the text *excludes*. | The reconstruction is a better argument than the text (evaluation smuggled into exposition); or a lazily invalid rendering the author would disown. |
| 2.7 | **Presupposition necessity test** [F] | Part (iv) items are unstated commitments the author "would have to accept if asked", each passing the counterfactual test (argument collapses without it). Distinct from support ("support is what the author gives you; presupposition is what they do not and need"). | "A presupposition that fails the necessity test is your own objection in disguise — route it to Practice H." Also: trivia ("assumes language exists"); suppressed premises misfiled as presuppositions. |

## Layer 3 — Interpretive calibration

| # | Dimension | Good looks like | Canonical failure |
|---|---|---|---|
| 3.1 | **Charity point declared** [FL] *(ext, Shields/Govier)* | The reconstruction sits at the right point between accuracy and strength and shows where: the **recognizability test** — would the author (or her best current interpreter: Neuhouser, Heinrich, Freyenhagen, McCarthy, Zurn) accept this as *her* argument? | Under-charity (dumbest permitted reading); over-charity (steelman drifting into strawman — rebuilt into what the *reader* finds convincing). |
| 3.2 | **Declared forks and open difficulties** [FL] | Where the text underdetermines the reading, the fork is named ("transcendental on reading A, genealogical on B; I take A because §X"). Difficulties flagged, not forced: "I don't see how step X follows from step Y" is a *correct* entry, especially on Protocol-2 texts. | False determinacy (one confident reading of a passage the secondary literature reads three ways); or premature resolution — forcing the text to make sense before later resources arrive. |
| 3.3 | **Declared vs. silent modernization** [FL] *(ext, Rorty)* | Deliberate rational-reconstruction anachronism (importing an apparatus, modernizing a debate) is fine **when declared**. Economist bridges (Polanyi, mechanism design, identification) used only where illuminating and marked as bridges. | Silent genre-mixing: decision-theoretic apparatus in Hegel presented as exegesis; incentive-compatibility framing on a normative-reconstruction question. |
| 3.4 | **Secondary-literature provenance** [F] | Marks which readings are defensible from the primary text and which are imported from the [ES] secondary read alongside (Neuhouser, McCarthy…). | Neuhouser's reading silently presented as what §§142–157 itself warrants. |
| 3.5 | **Translation vigilance** [FL] | Where a load-bearing passage was read in translation, the German checked or the check flagged as pending; discrepancies noted with a gloss ("Ashton renders X as Y; the German Z also connotes W"). | Argument built on a rendering that flattens the German (*Verstand*/*Vernunft* both "reason"); trusting a rough translation's negations. |

## Layer 4 — Artifact craft

| # | Dimension | Good looks like | Canonical failure |
|---|---|---|---|
| 4.1 | **Tier-format completeness** [FL] | All components required at the tier present (see 0.3); word count in range — "compressed, high-density notes-to-self", not essays. | Missing directed-against at Light; missing closing sentence / empirical-presuppositions / C1s at Full; essayistic sprawl or a note too thin to test retrieval. |
| 4.2 | **Own words, terms of art retained** [FL] | Load-bearing steps in the user's own words (the comprehension test); the author's language-bound terms kept in German (*Sittlichkeit*, *Verdinglichung*); quotes only where exact wording is contested. | Transcription/close paraphrase (fluency illusion); or full translation into home idiom that loses the concept (→ 2.5). |
| 4.3 | **Exposition/evaluation separation** [FL] | The four parts stay in the reconstructive voice; the user's own judgment lives **only** in the closing sentence. Objections route to Practice H; external-discipline objections route through the H1 translation move. | Evaluative adjectives doing silent work ("Adorno's *exaggerated* claim…"); Practice C/H content bleeding into the Practice A artifact. |
| 4.4 | **Closing sentence form** [F required; L optional] | Exactly one sentence, "If I were making this argument, the move I would change is X" — X names a premise, method, example, or opponent. Forward-looking; no defence yet. | Generic dissent; a compressed position-statement illegible without the conversation behind it; a defended mini-evaluation. |
| 4.5 | **C1 seminar questions** [F: 2–3; L: only if a tension surfaced] | Genuine tension *within the author's own framework*, 2–3 sentence setup + question (calibrate on Appendix D.4). | "Things I didn't understand" dressed as questions; pro-forma busy-work questions. |
| 4.6 | **Filing conventions** [FL] | Named by source (`Author-WorkShort-chN.md`); frontmatter per `tpl-reconstruction.md`; tags from the stable ≤30 vocabulary; `[[concept]]` links to touched Practice B entries; key passages as pointer + one-sentence gloss (5–10 per major text, not verbatim capture). | Invented frontmatter fields or tags; questions filed separately; verbatim passage-dumps. |

## Severity ladder (ordering for §2 findings)

1. **Fidelity** (Layer 1) — comprehension failed: misread claim, wrong opponent, scope inflation, inverted premise attribution.
2. **Structure** (Layer 2) — comprehension probable, rendering failed: summary-not-structure, unwarranted gap-fill, unnamed inference mode.
3. **Calibration** (Layer 3) — over/under-charity, undeclared anachronism, false determinacy.
4. **Craft** (Layer 4) — format, voice bleed, filing.

Severity labels within a finding: **misreading / weakens / polish**. Always attach the **comprehension vs. artifact** tag — the record shows these get conflated, and the conflation produces miscalibrated self-assessment in both directions.

## Feedback-design rules (from the formative-assessment literature; sources in research/external-rubric-research.md)

- **Feed-up / feed-back / feed-forward** (Hattie & Timperley): instantiate the standard for *this text*, locate the reconstruction against it, close with transfer rules. Task-level fixes repair the artifact; process-level rules transfer. Person-level praise does nothing or harms — omit it.
- **2–4 developed findings max** (Shute: manageable units); a residual list may follow undeveloped.
- **No sandwiching** (Hyland & Hyland: cushioning praise gets discounted and hedged criticism gets missed). The user has contractually requested unsoftened feedback. The supported alternative is *wise feedback*: state the high standard explicitly, state plainly that it is reachable, then be direct.
- **Rapoport opening**: one sentence showing the feedback understood what the reconstruction got right / was trying to do — a self-check against strawmanning the user, not a courtesy.
- **Self-assessment prompt (occasional)**: once the user knows the rubric, sometimes ask them to run one named dimension on their own draft before revealing the verdict (Sadler/Nicol: the goal is that the *user* comes to hold the standard). Use sparingly; never as a quiz ritual.
