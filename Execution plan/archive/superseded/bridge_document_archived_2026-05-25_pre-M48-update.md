**Purpose.** This document encodes the results of a structured diagnostic evaluation of the syllabus `sozialphilosophie_syllabus_v6.md` and the design decisions made by the reader. It serves as the specification for a companion execution plan — a week-by-week study schedule with assignments, comprehension checks, and pacing infrastructure. ## 1. Reader Profile

- Computational macroeconomist at the European Central Bank, Frankfurt.
- PhD-level quantitative social science (economics). Strong formal/mathematical skills.
- Fluent in German, English, Bulgarian. Can read philosophical German at pace.
- Available study time: **~7–8 hours/week**, realistic productive capacity **~300 hours/year** (accounting for holidays, travel, work surges, low-motivation periods). Possible additional ~50 hours/year in high-motivation periods.

The reader wants to be in the circle of people doing this work — attending workshops, presenting, publishing, potentially collaborating — while remaining a practising economist. This goal shapes the programme's output orientation: the endpoint is not "having read enough" but "having produced and presented a distinctive contribution."

## 2. Programme Parameters

### 2.1 Capacity envelope (the binding constraint) + motivational target

The programme is defined by the **capacity available to the reader**, not by a total-hours commitment. Timeline is a *derived* output of capacity × scope. The binding parameters are the capacity numbers below.

**Motivational target — soft anchor, not rigid commitment.** A ~42-month target (give-or-take) is psychologically load-bearing: an open-ended self-study programme without an explicit endpoint is harder to sustain than one with a visible horizon. The current operational target is **M44 (Jan 2030)** baseline + **M46 (Mar 2030)** W13-trigger fallback per Path D'. Trimming strategies + falling-behind protocols (execution_plan §A) should keep this horizon in mind — *but* the target is not a constraint to be defended at the cost of capacity-sustainable pacing. If scope × capacity arithmetic implies extending the timeline (e.g., to M48), extend the timeline rather than overcommit weekly hours. The 42-month frame keeps motivation; the capacity envelope keeps the programme sustainable; if they conflict, sustainability wins.

**Weekly rhythm**
- ~1h/day, ~7.5h/week sustainable
- ~40 productive weeks/year (the other ~12 absorb annual leave, illness, family, ECB peaks — NOT additional capacity)
- → ~300h/year baseline study time

**Project-allocated leave**
- 7–8 leave days/year project-allocated (out of ECB total 30 days/year)
- One dedicated leave-week per year of concentrated writing/retreat work
- → ~140–160h across the programme from transition retreats R1–R5 + writing blocks W1–W2

**Other capacity**
- GovC afternoons (when EU regulations release the reader from board-prep duties): ~16h/year
- Community engagement track (Phases II–III only): ~25–30h/year, drawn from the same project-allocated leave + evenings/weekends

**Aggregate capacity**: ~340–360h/year baseline + ~30h/year leave-week + ~25–30h/year community = ~400h/year sustainable.

**Voluntary overage band**: ~8–9 h/week is sustainable for short stretches (e.g., Module 7 peak; Phase III writing surges). Anything beyond ~10 h/week for more than 2–3 weeks is a falling-behind signal (see §3.4).

**The capacity ceiling is a structural commitment** (Class A per §8) — sustainability is the binding constraint. If a calculation says the programme needs more hours than this envelope provides, the response is to cut scope or extend timeline, not to overcommit weekly hours.

### 2.4 Community engagement and long-game

**The goal — what M44 actually does.** Become a participant in the Frankfurt-tradition conversation as an interdisciplinary outsider-insider — signal a non-standard profile (an ECB economist with strong quantitative training, who has done a serious self-study programme in Sozialphilosophie, willing, interested, and capable of engaging substantively, open to future collaboration). The aim is *not* to become indistinguishable from a Frankfurt-tradition PhD academic.

**M44 is the first visible move, not the destination.** A submitted journal piece + presentations + initiated scholar contacts represents the *opening* of community participation. The long game — published piece, second project, recurring conference presence, sustained scholar relations, possibly associated-researcher arrangements — runs through Phase IV and beyond. M44 produces a *credible signal*; Phase IV is where the signal compounds into participation.

**Activity rebalance (per audit 2).** The current track is calibrated for academic-insider entry: Vorlesung attendance + event listening + late cold emails — almost all audience-side, low-signal, high-orientation. For an outsider-insider profile this is upside-down. The user's *scarce, asymmetric signal* (ECB economist who can credibly engage Frankfurt-tradition material) is what should be made visible. The rebalance: downgrade audience-side activities; foreground activities where the user's profile is the value-add.

## 3. Structural Design Requirements for the Execution Plan

### 3.1 Weekly Assignment Structure

Each week should specify:
- **Core assignment** (~7–8 hours): the primary reading task for that week, drawn from the core programme. Specify exact pages/chapters, not just "continue Module X." Where a text spans multiple weeks, provide sub-assignments at the chapter or section level.
- **Bonus reading** (~2–4 hours, optional): a reading that enriches the current core assignment but is not required. Sources: (a) Context Layer items timed to the relevant core module; (b) [RS] items from the current module that didn't make the core cut; (c) secondary literature that illuminates the current primary text; (d) Phase IV connection points marked in the syllabus. The bonus reading should be genuinely optional — skipping it should never create a gap in the core trajectory. Label these clearly as "if you have extra time this week."
- **Active-practice task** (where applicable per the tiered regime): what to write, how long it should be, what question it should answer.
- **Session structure hint**: a brief annotation indicating what kind of sessions the current text rewards. Not a rigid schedule — a suggestion the reader can adjust. See §3.5 for the text-type → session mapping. The default rhythm is ~1h/day on 5–6 days plus a longer block (~2h) on one day for writing tasks. Deviations from the default are flagged only where the text genuinely requires it.

### 3.2 Comprehension Infrastructure

**Text-level checks (new — not in the syllabus).** For each primary text that takes >10 hours, provide 2–3 specific comprehension questions the reader should be able to answer after completing it. These should test argumentative understanding, not recall. Format: "After reading X, you should be able to: (a) reconstruct the argument that...; (b) explain why the author claims...; (c) identify where the argument is vulnerable to the objection that..."

**Module-level checks (from the syllabus).** The syllabus already provides these ("Comprehension indicator: after this module..."). Reproduce them at the appropriate week in the execution plan.

**Deviation protocols.** Include guidance for when actual time deviates significantly from estimated time:
- "If you've spent >X hours and can't answer the text-level comprehension questions, switch to secondary text Y for 5–8 hours, then return."
- "If you finished in <X hours and the comprehension checks feel easy, you may be reading too fast — test yourself by attempting a written reconstruction before moving on."
- "If a text is defeating you and the secondary doesn't help, skip to the next assignment and flag it for return. Do not spend more than 150% of the estimated time on any single text."

### 3.3 Checkpoint and Milestone Architecture

The execution plan distinguishes two related but distinct kinds of "comprehension checks" that were originally conflated:

**(a) Module-close comprehension checks** (canonical definition — referenced from `reading_practices_v4.md` Part III and `execution_plan_v1.md` §E.2). At each module close, brief written answers (~2–3h) to the capability targets listed at module start ("after this module you should be able to..."). These test argumentative understanding, not recall, and serve as a fast self-test of "do I have the apparatus?" — complementing (not duplicating) Dialogue Moments (§3.7), H1 light-discrimination notes, C1/C2 seminar questions, and 🗺 Practice D debate maps. Module-close cadence: every module (W17, W25, W28, W32, W41, W46, W54 in Phase I; M19, M22, M25, M27, M29 in Phase II; corresponding Phase III boundaries).

**(b) Quarterly milestones (QM, ~5 across the programme).** A brief field-test self-assessment anchored to module boundaries, NOT a strict 13-week cadence (the original 13-week framing was empirically replaced by module-boundary anchoring during execution-plan construction). Format: attempt to read 1 recent article from *WestEnd*, *DZfPh*, or *Constellations* and note (a) what you can follow, (b) what you can't yet, (c) what specific concepts or thinkers you're missing. ~2–3h; replaces one week's bonus reading. Scheduled at QM1 (W17), QM2 (W30), QM3 (M19), QM4 (M22), QM5 (M27), QM6 (M32–34 Comparison Exercise close), QM7 (M37–39).

**The two are different.** Module-close comprehension checks are *every-module* self-tests of capability targets; QMs are *every-quarter-ish* field-tests of growth measured against external journal articles. The first asks "did I absorb the apparatus?"; the second asks "can I follow current debates I couldn't before?". Both feed the year-end review.

**The existing Motivational Checkpoint** (at ~months 14–15, after Part I) is retained. It replaces two weeks of core reading, not adds to it. It functions as a milestone-grade field test (per (b)) but at higher resolution: Journal Test (~8–10h) + Field Report (~8–10h, the W3.2 seed).

**Year-end reviews.** At the end of each programme-Phase boundary, a longer self-assessment (~4–6h): re-read syllabus Part 0 orientation texts (especially Honneth's "Pathologien des Sozialen" and the Celikates & Jaeggi *Einführung*) and note how your understanding has changed. Tests for genuine depth growth vs. superficial coverage.

### 3.6 Dialogue Moments (canonical definition; cadence: every module close)

**Canonical definition.** `reading_practices_v4.md` Part III cross-references this section as the canonical definition site for Dialogue Moments (DM). The substantive content lives here.

**Cadence (post-Pass-7 reading-practices coverage audit 2026-05-19).** Originally specified as "3–4 points during Phases I–II"; this proved too thin once the execution plan was constructed. The operative cadence is **every module close**: ~7 DMs in Phase I (one per Phase I module close: W17, W25, W28, W32, W41, W46, W54) + ~5 DMs in Phase II (M19, M22, M25, M27, M29) + Phase III DMs where module-close points exist (Module 8A close M29). Total ~12+ DMs across the programme. The execution plan's §E.2 module-close checklist lists DM as a default item.

**Format.** After completing a module's comprehension check, formulate the single strongest objection to the argument you've just reconstructed and try to answer it. ~1h. Three execution channels: (a) **with Claude** — present the reconstruction, ask for identification of misreadings and undefended assumptions per `reading_practices_v4.md` Claude Use 1 (inline callout at Practice A); (b) **with a philosophically inclined colleague** when feasible; (c) **in writing** as a 200–300 word note filed with the reconstruction.

**Distinct from other module-close practices.** DM is a *peer-defense simulation* — it tests whether the apparatus survives challenge. It complements (does not duplicate) H1 (external→internal critique translation), C1 (in-author tensions), C2 (cross-thinker positioning), Practice D (whole-debate landscape), and comprehension check (capability self-test). See execution plan §E.2 for the full module-close checklist.

**Limitations of this approach (stated honestly).** Claude can identify whether a reconstruction is internally consistent and matches the standard scholarly reading, but cannot say "this is an original and interesting misreading worth developing" — which is what a good advisor does. DMs are pressure tests, not seminars. They catch errors; they don't generate insights. The community engagement track (Phase II onward) is the real compensatory mechanism.

**Hallucination caveat (per `reading_practices_v4.md` Claude Use 4 (inline callout at Practice H)).** When using Claude for DMs, treat its objections and steelmans as hypotheses to verify against the text, not as ground truth. Require citation of specific passages; verify each one.

### 3.7 Concentrated time blocks

The weekly rhythm (§2.1) is sustainable but inadequate for two kinds of work: (i) phase-spanning synthetic overviews that require holding many texts in mind at once; (ii) coherent long-form prose written without losing the argumentative arc between sessions. Concentrated blocks compensate. Two pools (per §8 Class A):

- **Pool 1**: project-allocated ~7–8 leave days/year → **5 transition retreats R1–R5** anchored to module/phase boundaries
- **Pool 2**: dedicated leave-week ~5 days × ~6h = ~30h/year → **2 writing blocks W1/W2** (M36 W3.2 first draft + M43 final revision)

**Transition retreats** (~16–20h each, ~85h total). R1 (W8): post-Part-0 field overview. R2 (W33–34): Hegel–Marx synthesis. R3 (W63–64): Phase I synthesis + Honneth "Pathologien" re-read. R4 (M16 W1): Phase II opener + Vorlesungsverzeichnis check. R5 (M30): Phase III opener + W3.2 candidate-topic outline + conditional-MUST promotion decisions (per [y3_critical_path.md](y3_critical_path.md) §4).

**Writing blocks** (~30h each, ~60h total). W1 at M36 (W3.2 first-draft consolidation; 5 days × 6h; pre-book to avoid GovC). W2 at M43 (final-revision consolidation; same constraints; avoid Christmas).

**Why concentrated blocks matter.** Daily 1-hour fragments cannot generate a 7,000-word philosophical argument with a single argumentative arc (W1/W2) or a phase-spanning synthetic overview (R1–R5). Retreats also function as enhanced checkpoints — the synthetic overview reveals misunderstandings that module-level comprehension checks might miss.

Operational details (per-retreat tasks, leave-booking lead times, GovC conflict avoidance) live in [execution_plan_v1.md](execution_plan_v1.md) Appendix E (retreats) + Appendix F (writing blocks).

## 5. Key Tensions the Execution Plan Must Navigate

**A. Early production vs. premature production.** Written output is deferred to the Motivational Checkpoint (M14, W57 Field Report) beyond Light-tier per-chapter Recon work. Craft Block production begins in Phase III. The Comparison Exercise (M32–34) is inserted between W3.1 and W3.2 as a bridge: harder than reconstruction, easier than original critique, and necessary for the positioning skill that the publishable piece requires.

**B. Comprehensiveness vs. feasibility.** y3's MUST-Anchor/MUST-Engage/SHOULD/COULD/DISCRETIONARY classification preserves intellectual breadth (via SHOULD + Context-Layer-style Orientation reads) without inflating the core budget. The execution plan keeps tier annotations visible in the Inventory so the reader always knows whether a given text is anchor-apparatus (Full), reviewer-coverage (Light), or breadth-context (Orientation).

**C. German vs. English.** The reader is fluent in German. Where both versions exist, German is generally preferred for Frankfurt-tradition primary texts (Hegel, Marx, Habermas, Honneth, Jaeggi, Adorno) because the conceptual vocabulary is canonical in German. English is fine for secondary literature, analytic philosophy texts, and non-German-tradition authors (Dewey, Fricker, Taylor, List & Pettit). Pacing in the Style × Tier matrix accounts for this: DE pp/h is slightly slower than EN within the same prose class.

**D. Community engagement vs. reading time.** The ~25–30 hours/year allocated to engagement in Phases II–III is a structural commitment per §8 Class A, not buffer-drawdown. Acceptable because (a) the comprehension benefit of live philosophical engagement likely saves more than 25 hours of self-correction per year, and (b) the community engagement directly serves the programme's ultimate goal (becoming a recognised interlocutor), which pure reading does not. Cut order under falling-behind: bonus readings → SHOULDs → tier downgrades → engagement reduction. **Never cut Vorlesung mid-semester unless behind 8+ weeks.**

**E. Slippage vs. re-engagement.** The dominant failure mode for multi-year self-study is not intellectual difficulty but schedule slippage leading to motivational collapse. The plan's falling-behind protocols ([execution_plan_v1.md](execution_plan_v1.md) §A + §A.1) are calibrated against this: graduated 2/4/8-week triggers, and at the 8-week threshold the rule is re-entry at the next phase boundary rather than catch-up. The goal is re-engagement, not checklist completion.
