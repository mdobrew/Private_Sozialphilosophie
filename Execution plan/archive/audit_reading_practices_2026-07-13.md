**Provenance.** Produced 2026-07-13 by a dedicated audit agent (Claude, Fable 5) as part of the three-part joint evaluation of the programme documents (this report; `audit_execution_plan_2026-07-13.md`; `audit_literature_coverage_2026-07-13.md`). **Question asked:** does the reading-practices regime survive contact with a full-time job at ~7.5 hours/week, and does it build the skills the Phase III publishable piece requires?

## 1. Fit to time budget — Score: 3/5

The arithmetic is unusually honest for a document of this genre: the α writing-overhead multipliers, the per-reconstruction time ceilings, and the explicit "40–50% on top of raw reading" admission mean the writing load is *budgeted*, not wished away. That is rare and worth preserving. The problems are in what the arithmetic doesn't capture.

**Honest per-week overhead estimates:**

| Week type | Practice overhead | Share of 7.5h |
|---|---|---|
| Full-tier Dense-class week (Hegel, Adorno) | ~2.75–3.75h (Full reconstruction amortised 1.5–2h + one C1 seminar question ~0.5h + concept-tracking updates / vault-linking / post-session workflow steps 5–7 ~0.75–1h + reading-log and marginalia normalisation ~0.25h) | 37–50% |
| Full-tier Systematic/Layered-class week | ~1.5–2.5h | 20–33% |
| Light-tier week | ~1–1.5h | 13–20% |
| Standard module close | 5–7h *stated* — but see finding (b) below | ~100% |
| Phase II steady-state standing load | Practice H2 (full structured evaluation, ~0.8h/week averaged) + Practice J Skizze (short argumentative essay, ~0.7h/week averaged) + Practice I Anwendung (concept-application, ~0.45h/week averaged) + community-engagement track (~0.5h/week) ≈ **~2.5h/week before any reading** | 33% |

Three specific findings:

**(a) The document gives three different numbers for the same event.** Practice A (the four-part argument reconstruction) says a Full reconstruction costs 30–45 minutes (reading_practices.md ~line 127). The per-reconstruction ceiling elsewhere in the time-allocation apparatus is 2.0 hours. The post-session workflow section (§L.12) says steps 1–7 — Obsidian template, metadata, the reconstruction itself, targeted re-consultation of the text, seminar questions, concept-entry updates, vault-linking, index update — cost **60–120 minutes per session-close** (reading_practices.md ~line 956). The §L.12 number is the true one, because it includes the Obsidian friction the other two exclude. If the α multipliers were calibrated on the 30–45-minute figure, the whole weekly envelope is ~30–60 minutes light. **Fix: the programme-week-13 calibration hook should measure workflow-inclusive time (the §L.12 number), and the document should say so explicitly.**

**(b) The module-close budget contradicts itself.** The time-allocation table caps a standard module close at 5–7 hours (reading_practices.md ~line 546). But summing the named items: comprehension check (the module-close self-test against the module's stated capability targets; canonical definition bridge document §3.3) 2–3h + Dialogue Moment (state the strongest objection to the argument just reconstructed and answer it; canonical definition bridge document §3.6/3.7) 1h + Practice H1 (light discrimination note: translate an external objection into an internal one, or show why translation fails) ~0.75h + Practice C2 (cross-thinker positioning questions) ~0.5h + Practice D (debate map) with the mandatory negative-space note 1.5–2h + weekly review note + master-index update + (from mid-Phase II) the Practice E quota of "3–5 synthesis notes per module-close pair" at 0.5–1h each. That is **7–11 hours** — every module close becomes a zero-reading week that overflows into the next. Practice E's quota is not in the module-close table at all. This is a concrete internal inconsistency; fix it by cutting, not by raising the budget (see §7 and the top-3 changes).

**(c) What gets silently skipped, and what that costs.** Under time pressure, the drop order will be: vault-linking (post-session workflow step 6) → concept-tracking updates (Practice B, step 5) → synthesis notes (Practice E) → review notes → negative-space notes → C2 cross-thinker questions. Note what is on that list: Practice B (concept tracking) is the document's own declared instrument against "conflating positions that are actually in tension," and synthesis notes are a declared *prerequisite* for the publishable piece (the document itself says a candidate for writing exercise W3.2 — the Phase III publishable piece — "that doesn't lean on 3–4 prior synthesis notes is probably under-integrated; revise"). The two most load-bearing retention/integration artifacts live in the lowest-salience, highest-friction slot — the post-session Obsidian chores — while low-yield apparatus (the 19-tag marginalia ontology) occupies protected reading time. The failure is not that practices will be skipped; it's that the *wrong* ones will be, and nothing in the document detects it (see §7).

**Replacements:** (i) Amend the week-13 calibration hook to log workflow-inclusive overhead, not reconstruction-writing time alone. (ii) Reconcile the module-close table with the actual item list by cutting to three artifacts (see top-3 changes). (iii) Delete Practice E's "3–5 per module-close pair" quota; replace with trigger-based synthesis plus a monthly 30-minute vault walk (see §4).

## 2. Fit to text types — Score: 4/5

The three reading protocols are genuinely distinct and operational — Protocol 1's no-running-notes single pass with closed-book reconstruction, Protocol 2's two-pass approach with pointer-skeleton and essential-secondary-before-primary sequencing, Protocol 3's light-marginalia careful read. This is not one protocol relabelled: the skeleton-versus-reconstruction division of labour and the "a skeleton a colleague could learn from has become a summary" test are precise and enforceable. The Hegel worked scaffold's honesty that "the argument *is* the transition" shows real sensitivity to dialectical form.

**The gap is Adorno.** Hegel and Adorno are both filed under Protocol 2 / Dense class, but they defeat the four-part reconstruction differently. Hegel strains parts (i) claim and (ii) support — the worked-example scaffold handles this. Adorno's parataxis rejects the claim/support form *as a philosophical position* (*Identitätsdenken* — identity thinking — is precisely what the four-part template enacts). For *Minima Moralia* aphorism clusters, *Dialektik der Aufklärung*, and Benjamin's theses on the concept of history, the aphoristic flag exists only as a *time-budgeting* rule (chapter count = aphorism clusters); there is zero guidance on what a reconstruction of a constellation looks like. A reader who applies the four-part template to *Minima Moralia* §29 will either produce a falsifying flattening or conclude they failed. **Replace with a Protocol 2a for aphoristic/paratactic texts**, in which the reconstruction parts become: (i) the phenomenon or figure the cluster circles; (ii) the target (the directed-against — usually identifiable even where theses aren't); (iii) 2–3 load-bearing sentences, located by page; (iv) what the form itself refuses to say and why the refusal is the point. Keep the four-part form for Hegel, where a transition-thesis can be extracted.

Second, smaller gap: nothing on treatise-scale architecture for *Theorie des kommunikativen Handelns*. Per-chapter closed-book reconstruction is right, but across ~1,200 pages and ~6 months, one running one-page architecture map is needed (where the *Zwischenbetrachtungen* sit, where the colonisation-of-the-lifeworld thesis will land). This is *structural* marking, so it doesn't violate the content-notes prohibition — the document should say so explicitly, or the reader will feel it's forbidden.

**German:** better than expected. Practice G (translation comparison) is specific and correct in kind (Ashton's *Negative Dialektik* translation, *Geltungsanspruch*, *Verstand*/*Vernunft*), the AI-use rules separate syntactic from philosophical opacity, and the bilingual marginalia table exists. One inconsistency: Practice G says "read primarily in whichever language is faster (probably English for most texts)"; bridge document §5.C says German is generally preferred for Frankfurt primary texts. These give opposite defaults for, e.g., Honneth. **Resolve in favour of the bridge document** (German for primaries, English for secondaries) and reword Practice G's practical-implementation paragraph.

## 3. Skill progression — Score: 4/5

This is the document's strongest dimension, and the answer to "is the transition from understanding texts to arguing with them scheduled or just hoped for" is: scheduled. The ladder is explicit and gated: the closing sentence ("if I were making this argument, the move I would change is X" — a micro-position from text 1 onward) → Practice H1's external-to-internal objection discrimination (from Module 1) → C1/C2 seminar questions (tension-spotting, cross-thinker positioning) → Practice H2 (full structured evaluation) gated to post-Module-6 with a stated *reason* for the gate (steelmanning requires a competing-positions landscape) → Practice I (Anwendung: applying a concept-tool to a phenomenon from the reader's own field) from Module 2 → Practice J (Skizze: a ~1,500-word argumentative essay defending a positive claim) gated to ~month 25, again with a stated reason → the Comparison Exercise (months 37–38) → writing exercise W3.2, the publishable piece — with the Phase III practice-transformation section correctly inverting the relationship (practices serve the piece). Unlocking the empirical-presuppositions component of evaluation from day 1 for this specific reader is smart tailoring. The taper rules (H1, C1, Practice D all shed once the move is trained) show the rare awareness that training practices should *end*.

Two real weaknesses:

**(a) The Skizze → publishable-piece cliff.** The longest thing the reader will have written before the month-40 writing block is ~1,500 words. Writing exercise W3.2 is ~7,000+ words with a single argumentative arc. The Comparison Exercise is a reading-and-positioning bridge, not a prose-arc bridge. The bridge document *already contains* the fix — the cross-disciplinary short essay (~months 29–34, 2,000–3,000 words, *Soziopolis*/*WestEnd* as venues) — but it lives in the community-engagement track and is never integrated into the practice ladder. **Promote it formally: name it in reading_practices Part II as the "Zwischenstück," the mandatory step between the Skizze and the publishable piece**, so it can't be dropped as optional community garnish.

**(b) Genre voice is asserted, never trained.** The Skizze must be "Frankfurt-voiced … not LLM-flavoured," but no practice trains prose. All feedback channels pressure-test *content*. Cheap fix: one imitation exercise per phase — take a 300-word passage from Jaeggi or Honneth, write a paragraph in the same register on the reader's own material, diff them. 45 minutes, twice a programme.

## 4. Retention design — Score: 3/5

The encoding side is genuinely strong: closed-book retrieval practice, proposition-titled synthesis notes, cross-modular concept entries, manual linking with a proliferation guard (the "second parenthetical gloss" trigger for extracting a synthesis note is exactly right). Notes are structured for reuse: a four-part reconstruction with a Presuppositions heading is queryable 18 months later in a way prose summaries aren't.

The *review* side is where it fails the 18-month test. **There is no scheduled loop over the reader's own vault.** The candidates all fall short: weekly review notes are capture, not review; Practice F (strategic re-reading) is four named re-reads of *texts*, not notes; spaced repetition — the only true consolidation mechanism in the document — is buried in the post-session workflow section as an optional plugin recommendation. Retrieval practice at encoding installs the *skill* of reconstruction; it does not keep Module 2's *Warenfetischismus*/*Verdinglichung* distinction alive at month 30 without re-exposure. The document quietly conflates the two. By Phase III, the vault will be a well-structured archive the reader no longer remembers writing — the master-index annotations and Dataview queries mitigate findability, not memory.

**Replace:** (i) Promote spaced repetition from optional plugin to a scheduled module-close item: at each close, write 5–8 retrieval prompts testing the module's confusable distinctions (the document's own example — Lukács's structural concept of *Verdinglichung* versus Honneth's recognition-theoretic reinterpretation — is the model); ~10 minutes/week of review thereafter. Net cost ~20 minutes/module + 10 minutes/week; the cheapest high-yield item in the entire regime. (ii) Add a monthly 30-minute vault walk — reread three old notes (one reconstruction, one concept entry, one synthesis note), add one link or one self-disagreement note if provoked. This doubles as the trigger-based replacement for Practice E's quota (§1c).

## 5. Evidence-informed? — Score: 4/5

The core is the real thing, correctly cited and — more importantly — correctly *operationalised*: retrieval practice (Roediger & Karpicke) drives the closed-book rule; the fluency illusion drives the content-notes prohibition; the collector's fallacy is explicitly designed against (pointer-marginalia, no verbatim copying, "a book full of yellow marks is not evidence of understanding"); write-then-compare guards against anchoring on worked examples and AI outputs; the Ahrens/Matuschak distinction between *Literaturnotizen* and *permanente Notizen* is applied rather than name-dropped. The AI-sequencing discipline (own work first, Claude after; the hallucination protocol requiring cited defences) is better than most institutional guidance. Credit also for the scheduled self-pruning of YAML and template scaffolding.

The ritual, bluntly:

- **The 19-tag bilingual marginalia vocabulary.** Marking/highlighting is the *lowest*-yield activity in the learning-science literature the document itself cites, and it has been given the most elaborate ontology in the document — two languages, a normalisation rule, a spine/sub-type/reader-side taxonomy. At read-time in a 60-minute evening session this collapses to ~4 tags within a month; maintaining the rest is annotation theatre. **Cut to six tags** ([claim]/[These], [prem], [gegen], [!], [??], [→]) for the first six months; reinstate a tag only when its absence demonstrably costs something at reconstruction time.
- **The mandatory negative-space note on every debate map.** The move itself — identifying a constructible but unoccupied position in the debate — is the most generative in the document; but *requiring* one per module manufactures forced pseudo-positions in modules that don't have one (Weber; philosophy of social science). Make it "write it when you see one; record 'no credible negative space found' in one line otherwise."
- **Quota-fied synthesis notes** (§1c) — insight on a schedule is ritual by definition. (Note: the quota, not the practice — Practice E itself is load-bearing.)
- **Five overlapping module-close positioning artifacts** (comprehension check, Dialogue Moment, Practice H1 note, C1 questions, C2 questions, plus Practice D). The document needs a full paragraph *and* a table to explain to its own reader why the Dialogue Moment ≠ H1 ≠ C1 ≠ C2. When a practice set requires a disambiguation apparatus, it is over-differentiated. The cognitive distinctions are real; at 7.5 hours/week the marginal value of artifacts four and five is near zero. See the top-3 changes.

## 6. Worked examples quality — Score: 4/5

The Honneth "Pathologien des Sozialen" reconstruction is at standard, not toy: the claim is stated at the right grain (pathology versus injustice versus inequality as the discriminating move), the support part actually identifies the mode of inference (normative reconstruction, textual-interpretive evidence) rather than listing points, the directed-against part names three distinct opponents with distinct failure modes, and the presuppositions carry forward-references (Zurn, *Das Recht der Freiheit*) that make them usable later. The Practice B *immanente Kritik* concept entry and both C1 seminar-question examples (the Jaeggi circularity question, the Honneth autonomy-criterion question) are genuine graduate-seminar grade. The unfilled Hegel scaffold is pedagogically the best object in the document — refusing to model a contested reconstruction, and saying why, is exactly right. (Caveat per house rules: the auditor has not verified the Honneth example against the 1994 text; it reads at the right grain, which is what an exemplar must do.)

Two deficits:

**(a) The Skizze "placeholder" is not a placeholder.** It contains a substantive, named argumentative move (the pre-discursiveness/legibility dilemma, resolved via Renault's phenomenology of social suffering, against a specified steelman). The document's own write-then-compare protocol predicts what happens: the first real Skizze at month 25 will unconsciously reproduce this shape — and this one is plausibly an LLM artefact, which the warning box half-admits. **Recommended: replace the substantive content with a purely formal skeleton** (section functions + word counts + the four quality criteria). [User decision 2026-07-13: keep the example, but relocate it to an appendix where it does not immediately anchor, with the warning label retained.]

**(b) No worked Anwendung.** Practice I (Anwendung) is the practice closest to the publishable-piece skill, and its hardest discrimination — theoretical purchase versus mere metaphor — gets one inline example (forward guidance / commodity form) but no full worked instance and, crucially, no worked *failure*, despite "failure is informative" being a stated principle. Either write one full Anwendung including a failed-binding verdict, or apply the Hegel-scaffold solution: a fill-in scaffold with the discrimination prompts spelled out, canonised by the reader's first real instance at ~week 22.

## 7. Failure-mode realism — Score: 3/5

The tier system itself encodes the right macro-priorities: importance-driven Full/Light/Orientation tiers, the bridge document's cut order (bonus reads → SHOULD-class items → tier downgrades → engagement last), taper rules that shed training-wheel practices, and the peak-week rule. Under *reading* pressure, the system degrades correctly.

Under *practice* pressure it is blind. The falling-behind protocols measure weeks-behind on the reading schedule. A reader can be perfectly on schedule and have silently stopped writing — the classic outcome for exactly this profile (reading Adorno at 22:00 is tractable; opening Obsidian afterwards is not) — and no trigger fires. The document's own theory says this reader has become a passive reader accumulating fluency illusion, i.e., the programme's core failure mode, and it is unmonitored.

The 20% that delivers 80% of the value: (1) Full-tier Practice A reconstructions on MUST-Anchor texts (the highest importance class in the Phase III critical path); (2) the Anwendung→Skizze production pipeline; (3) Practice B concept entries on the ~10 load-bearing concepts (*Anerkennung*, *Verdinglichung*, *immanente Kritik*, *Lebensform*, *Pathologie*, *Sittlichkeit*, *Lebenswelt*, *Rechtfertigung*, *Entfremdung*, *Kritik*). Everything else — C2 questions, Dialogue Moments and H1 notes post-taper, negative-space notes, review notes, dashboards, most marginalia — is scaffolding. The document never says this. It should, in one blunt paragraph.

Also: the peak-week rule ("Anwendung or Skizze slips; module-close items hold") has the priority *backwards* from mid-Phase II onward. By month 25 the module-close receptive artifacts are consolidation of a trained skill; the production pipeline is the scarce asset on the month-48 critical path. Invert the rule from month 25: the Skizze holds; the comprehension check compresses to 1 hour and the Dialogue Moment slips.

**Replacements:** (i) Add a **practice-debt trigger** to the falling-behind protocol: two or more tier-mandated filed notes missing = equivalent of the 2-week reading trigger; the response is tier downgrade, not catch-up writing. (ii) Codify **graceful degradation**: a Full reconstruction that can't get its 45 minutes becomes a Light note (claim + directed-against, 150 words, 10 minutes) *that same week* — never nothing; a Light note degrades to a reading-log line, never to nothing. The ladder Full→Light→log-line already exists as a tier system; repurpose it as the compliance floor. (iii) Add the one-paragraph "what to protect" statement naming the 20% above.

## (b) Three highest-value changes

1. **Consolidate the module close from five artifacts to three (~4h, from a real 7–11h).** Keep: the comprehension check (trimmed) and Practice D (debate map, with the negative-space note made opportunistic). Merge the Practice H1 discrimination note + Dialogue Moment + C2 cross-thinker questions into one "adversarial module note" (~1h): the strongest external impulse, translated internal or shown untranslatable (the H1 function); stated as the strongest objection and answered (the Dialogue-Moment function); plus one cross-thinker question (the C2 function). Delete the Practice E quota; fold synthesis into the monthly vault walk. This removes the document's largest internal inconsistency and returns ~3h to every module-close week. [User note 2026-07-13: comprehension checks must be preserved — confirmed, they are kept in this design.]
2. **Add a practice-debt trigger and a degradation floor to the falling-behind apparatus.** Missing ≥2 tier-mandated filed notes fires like the 2-week reading trigger; the sanctioned response is Full→Light→log-line degradation in the same week, never silent omission. This is the only change that protects the regime's actual high-value 20% against the statistically dominant outcome, and it costs zero hours.
3. **Bridge the 1,500→7,000-word cliff: promote the bridge document's cross-disciplinary short essay (~months 29–34) into the practice ladder as a mandatory Zwischenstück.** One 2,500–3,000-word piece with a single arc, built from 2–3 Skizzen, before the first Phase III writing block. It already exists in the bridge document's community-engagement section with a venue and a time budget; it just needs to be made non-optional and named in reading_practices Part II so the production ladder has no missing rung.

## (c) Verdict

As written, the regime survives contact with a full-time job in its skeleton but not in its skin: the core loop — read without running notes, reconstruct closed-book, get the reconstruction checked, escalate through evaluation to Anwendung and Skizze — is evidence-grounded, honestly costed at the macro level, and has the best-designed skill ladder toward Phase III writing one could expect in a self-study programme; that part will hold at 7.5 hours/week if defended. What will not survive is the periphery it is wrapped in: a module close that really costs 7–11 hours against a 5–7-hour budget, a 19-tag marginalia ontology, quota-fied synthesis notes, five overlapping module-close artifacts, and a post-session Obsidian workflow whose true 60–120-minute cost appears nowhere in the α calibration — and because the falling-behind apparatus watches only the reading schedule, the inevitable shedding of this periphery will proceed undetected and will take Practice B (concept tracking) and the synthesis layer (both declared load-bearing for the publishable piece) down with the genuinely disposable items. The fix is not more discipline but three edits: shrink the module close, install a practice-debt trigger with a Full→Light→log degradation floor, and hard-schedule one mid-length essay before month 40. With those, this is a regime a working economist can actually run for 42 months; without them, expect the reconstructions to survive, the vault's connective tissue to quietly die around month 20, and the gap to surface — expensively — at the month-37 Comparison Exercise.
