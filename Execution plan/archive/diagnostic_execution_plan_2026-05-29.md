### D1 — Pacing realism (capacity-calibrated)

**What good looks like.** Weekly core load sits inside ~7–8 h/week for an ECB economist working full-time; month-level clusters average ≤ ~9 h/week sustained; peaks ≤ ~10 h/week for ≤ 2–3 weeks; reading speeds (the bottom-of-range pp/h) are flagged as the *aspirational fast end*, not silently assumed. **Bad:** sustained months at 38–42h with no slack; "bottom-of-range" treated as the planning default for a reader whose calibration hasn't happened yet.

**Phase I (week-level) — 4/5.** Weekly hours mostly land ~5–8h; the GovC reroute (W17–W21) is genuinely capacity-aware; the W1–W8 ramp and the explicit W13 two-gate trigger are exactly the right instinct for a reader whose true pp/h is unknown. The residual risk is structural-honest: the plan *admits* it is calibrated to bottom-of-range and that midpoint pacing pushes Phase I to ~17 months — so the realism gap is disclosed, not hidden. Minor: a few weeks (W16 at ~8h on Phän IV.A + Full recon; W21 at ~10h) sit at the top of the band back-to-back. **Calibration-dependent** — the W13 trigger is the correct resolution mechanism; do not "fix" by re-pacing now.

**Phases II–III (month-level) — 3/5.** Module 7 at ~162h over M20–M25 (~32h/month ≈ ~7.4 h/week net of the ~12 non-productive weeks) and the M30–M32 cluster at ~40h/month are at the very top of the sustainable envelope *and assume bottom-of-range speeds hold for the densest German systematic texts in the programme* (TKH, KuA, Forst, Jaeggi). If W13 shows midpoint pacing, these months break first. The plan's mitigation (Layer 2 first cut, late-binding weekly cadence) is real, but the baseline month-load is set against the optimistic speed. **Calibration-dependent** (decision rule: if W13 trigger fires, the M50 cascade *and* Layer 2 must both be assumed for the Module 7/8 peak — confirm the plan's month tables are read as "bottom-of-range; expect +30–40%").

**Phase IV — n/a (log-only).**

### D2 — Slippage resilience

**What good looks like.** Graduated triggers; a re-entry rule that prefers phase-boundary re-entry over catch-up; cuts ordered so that the *last* thing to go is the thing the programme exists for; protocols that survive a 2–3 month motivational dropout. **Bad:** all-or-nothing catch-up; cut order that sacrifices the publication spine or community track early.

**Phase I — 4/5.** Appendix A (2/4/8-week triggers) + the "re-enter at next phase boundary, don't catch up" rule at 8 weeks is well-matched to the stated dominant failure mode (slippage → motivational collapse). The honest flag that "Phase I has no first cuts beyond Layer 0 bonus (~2–5h headroom)" is a strength — it tells the reader the only real Phase I lever is the M49/M50 timeline slip, not scope. **Local** at most.

**Phases II–III — 3/5.** The Layer 2→3→4→5 cascade is well-structured and the "community engagement never cuts first" rule is preserved. But two resilience gaps: (a) the cascade's hour-savings figures (~65h Layer 2, ~145h cumulative) are quoted against the M48 +122h overage — i.e., the plan *needs roughly all of Layer 2 + Layer 3-non-conflict just to hold M48 at baseline pacing*, leaving thin reserve for an actual mid-programme dropout on top of overage; (b) Phase III is declared "event-driven, ±1 month expected" but has **no explicit falling-behind protocol of its own** — Appendix A is written in Phase-I/II vocabulary (modules, bonus readings) that doesn't map onto Phase III's writing-block/feedback-cycle structure. A 6-week slip in M40–M43 (CFP rejection, feedback delay, ECB surge) has no codified response other than the generic "build slack into W1/W2." **Structural** for the Phase III gap (needs a Phase-III-specific slip protocol section); **Calibration-dependent** for (a).

**Phase IV — 4/5 (direction).** Re-entry into Phase IV is unconstrained by design; fine.

## Consequential design trade-offs already made

**T1 — Bottom-of-range pacing as the planning default (explicit).** *Choice:* schedule against the fast end of every pp/h band, with W13 as the correction valve and M50/M52 as the timeline release. *Defensible?* Yes, given Class A locks capacity and forbids adding hours — the only honest alternatives are "plan to midpoint (→ ~17-month Phase I, blows the 48-month anchor)" or "cut scope now (premature, before calibration data exists)." Planning optimistic + measuring early + releasing timeline is the right structure. *Evidence that would flip it:* if the W9–W13 log shows midpoint-or-slower on *all three* metrics under steady-state capacity, the default should flip to midpoint for Phases II–III and scope should take Layer 2 immediately rather than waiting for a 4-week trigger.

**T2 — Front-loaded Phase II community engagement vs. reading-time protection (explicit, bridge-driven).** *Choice:* spend ~25–30h/year + book-review + short-essay in the Module 7/8 peak. *Defensible?* Yes — it is the only thing pure reading cannot deliver toward the actual goal (becoming an interlocutor), and the bridge makes it Class A last-to-cut. *Flip evidence:* if Phase II pacing data shows the engagement is displacing core apparatus reading (Module 7/8 Recons systematically slipping), the *timeline* should absorb it, not the engagement — but if even M52 can't, the bridge's own Class A ranking would have to be revisited (a user decision, not a planning one).

**T3 — 48-month motivational anchor vs. sustainability (explicit, resolved "sustainability wins").** *Choice:* M48 baseline with M50/M52 fallbacks, explicitly subordinated to capacity. *Defensible?* Yes and well-reasoned. *Unintended tension:* the fallbacks are real but each timeline slip is itself motivationally costly (the anchor exists *because* open-ended self-study collapses) — so the plan's safety valve (extend, don't overcommit) trades pacing-sustainability against motivation-sustainability. There is no free resolution; the plan picks the defensible side.

**T4 — Per-chapter Recon granularity (by omission of the alternative).** *Choice:* one Recon per chapter/section for Full, generating ~227 programme Recons. *Defensible?* Yes for apprenticeship density — but it is also the source of the D6 counting fragility: with ~227 hand-tracked artefacts across many tables, the counts drift (32≠27, 39≠49). A coarser "per-text Recon + chapter sub-notes" convention would be easier to keep coherent but would lose the per-chapter discipline the bridge values. The trade is defensible *only if* R1's reconciliation is maintained as texts are reclassified.

**T5 — Calibrate-then-release rather than buffer (by omission).** *Choice:* there is essentially no slack buffer in Phase I (~2–5h headroom admitted) — resilience comes from the timeline-slip cascade, not from reserve hours. *Defensible?* Given Class A, yes — reserve hours would mean planning below capacity, lengthening the timeline anyway. But it means there is *no* absorption for a single bad month except slipping a milestone, which raises the stakes on the W13 trigger firing correctly.

**Unintended-tension note (cross-dimension).** Improving **D6 granularity** (more per-chapter Recons, finer tier annotations) directly degrades **D9 coherence** (more numbers to keep synchronised across more docs) — this is the mechanism that produced the current count drift. And improving **D5 motivation** (front-loaded community wins, fixed 48-month horizon) constrains **D2 resilience** (those wins are un-cuttable, so the only slip lever is the timeline, which is the motivational anchor). Both tensions are inherent, not fixable — they should be *managed* (keep R1's reconciliation current; treat timeline slip as the designed-in resilience mechanism) rather than "solved."
