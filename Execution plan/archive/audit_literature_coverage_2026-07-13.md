## A. Missing or under-weighted literature

### A.1 The recognition spine's own sources (branch A — the default endpoint)

**1. Frantz Fanon, *Peau noire, masques blancs* (1952), ch. 5 ("L'expérience vécue du Noir") + the Hegel-and-recognition section of ch. 7.** — **Category (i); the single sharpest gap in the syllabus.**
*Why:* The field map itself names Fanon as a founder of the decolonial paradigm, yet Module 8A (*Außereuropäische Kritik der Moderne*) contains no Fanon — an internal map↔syllabus inconsistency. Substantively worse: Fanon's argument that the Hegelian master–slave dialectic fails under colonialism (the master wants work, not recognition; the slave is denied even the struggle) is *the* hinge between the recognition spine and everything in Module 8A. Wilderson (8A.7) and Mbembe (8A.8) presuppose Fanon; Honneth's critics in this terrain cite him constantly. A recognition-track Phase III piece that engages Module 8A (which importance_tiers §1 says reviewers now expect) with Wilderson but without Fanon reads as having skipped the original.
*Where/tier:* Module 8A, before items 8A.6–8A.7. **MUST-Engage, Light tier** (~40–60pp, 5–8h).
*Cut:* demote Mignolo (8A.5) from essential-secondary [ES] to optional [O] — Quijano (8A.4) carries the decolonial master concept; frees ~3h — plus Layer-0 bonus headroom for the remainder.
*Verify:* Fanon chapter numbering varies across editions; the Hegel section sits in ch. 7 of the standard French edition.

**2. Honneth, *Leiden an Unbestimmtheit* (2001).** — **Category (ii): present only as a one-line signpost in Appendix I's map of the Hegel-interpretation dispute; no reading entry anywhere.**
*Why:* Honneth's own reconstruction of the *Rechtsphilosophie* as a theory of freedom is the missing bridge between Module 1 (Neuhouser + the *Rechtsphilosophie*, MUST-Anchor territory) and Module 7 (*Das Recht der Freiheit*, 7.5, MUST-Anchor) — it is where "normative reconstruction" is first tried out on Hegel, and it is standard reviewer-visible commentary for exactly this spine. ~130pp, German, fast.
*Where/tier:* Module 1 (after 1.1/1.2) or as a Module 7 pre-block. **SHOULD, Light tier** (8–10h).
*Cut:* move Pilapil (7.20, SHOULD, 8–12h) to Layer-1 baseline-bonus — Ikäheimo (Appendix H item H.1.9) + Laitinen already cover the "constructive extender" role phase3 assigns Pilapil.

**3. Honneth, *Verdinglichung* — specify the Oxford Tanner-Lectures edition: *Reification: A New Look at an Old Idea* (OUP 2008), with the commentaries by Judith Butler, Raymond Geuss, and Jonathan Lear plus Honneth's reply.** — **Category (ii): item 7.6 is already [ES]/SHOULD, but the syllabus cites only the 2005 Suhrkamp text.**
*Why:* For ~50 extra pages you acquire, in one volume: Butler's recognition-critique (otherwise wholly absent — the only Butler anywhere is a passing mention inside the Reckwitz annotation), Geuss's deflationary objection (Geuss is otherwise absent; his commentary substitutes at near-zero cost for *The Idea of a Critical Theory*, CUP 1981, the classic compact statement of ideology-critique), and the strongest available pressure-test of the Lukács→Honneth chain the syllabus already builds (3A.1 → 7.6). The highest marginal-value-per-hour fix in this report.
*Where/tier:* amend 7.6's entry; +3–4h within the existing SHOULD/Light slot. *Cut:* none needed.

**4. Fichte, *Grundlage des Naturrechts* (1796/97), §§1–7 (the *Aufforderung* — summons — and the deduction of the other).** — **Category (i), cheap structural fix.**
*Why:* The actual origin of *Anerkennung* as a transcendental condition of self-consciousness — what Honneth's *Anerkennung* ideengeschichte (7.18) and Siep narrate. Appendix I.2 currently assigns Fichte only via *Wissenschaftslehre* selections (I.2.3), which serve the Hegel-metaphysics build, not the recognition spine.
*Where/tier:* swap I.2.3's selection to *Naturrecht* §§1–7 (or add as alternative). **Zero net hours.**
*Also here, one line each:* Ricoeur, *Parcours de la reconnaissance* (2004) — the third recognition monograph after Honneth and Taylor; Context-Layer/COULD-Orientation suffices; promote only if the piece surveys the concept's career. Honneth/Rancière, *Recognition or Disagreement* (Columbia UP 2016, eds. Genel & Deranty) — COULD; the one place Honneth debates a poststructuralist head-on; verify exact title.

### A.2 The Habermas arc (Module 6) — two real holes

**5. Habermas, "Diskursethik — Notizen zu einem Begründungsprogramm," in *Moralbewußtsein und kommunikatives Handeln* (1983).** — **Category (i).**
*Why:* Module 6 covers communicative rationality (6.3), System/*Lebenswelt* (6.5), law and democracy (6.6/6.7) — but **discourse ethics proper (the universalisation principle U and the discourse principle D) is nowhere assigned**. The syllabus even contains a dangling cross-reference: the Kant *Grundlegung* entry (Appendix I.1.5) says "→ Connects: Habermas's *Diskursethik* (principle U…, Module 6)" — but no Module 6 item exists. Forst's *Das Recht auf Rechtfertigung* (7.7) is now a MUST-Anchor at Full tier, and Forst's constructivism *descends from* discourse ethics; positioning against Forst without the U/D apparatus means reconstructing the child without the parent. Optional Context-Layer companion: "Treffen Hegels Einwände auf die Diskursethik zu?" (in *Erläuterungen zur Diskursethik*, 1991) — Habermas replaying the *Moralität*/*Sittlichkeit* objection against himself, i.e., exactly the axis this spine turns on.
*Where/tier:* Module 6, new item 6.9, read between 6.5 and 6.6. **MUST-Engage, Light tier** (~60–90pp, 8–12h).
*Cut:* cap 6.4 (*Theorie des kommunikativen Handelns* vols. II–III selections, [RS], 15–20h) at the already-flagged essential passages (~8–10h); frees ~6–8h.

**6. Habermas, *Legitimationsprobleme im Spätkapitalismus* (1973), Teil II (the crisis typology: economic / rationality / legitimation / motivation crises), paired with a short Offe selection (*Strukturprobleme des kapitalistischen Staates*, 1972).** — **Category (i), with a direct line to the reader's professional wedge.**
*Why:* Streeck's *Gekaufte Zeit* (14.1) is a MUST-Anchor at Full tier, and it explicitly frames itself as a return to — and revision of — the early-1970s Frankfurt crisis theories: Streeck's "buying time" is the answer to *why the legitimation crisis was deferred*. An ECB economist deploying Streeck's apparatus under his own pen without the crisis typology it revises will be caught by any reviewer of that generation. This is also the one first/second-generation text that theorises *exactly* the reader's professional object (state management of economic crisis tendencies).
*Where/tier:* Module 14, companion slot before/with 14.1. **SHOULD, Light tier** (~60–80pp, 7–10h).
*Cut:* trim Wright (14.5, SHOULD, 12–15h) to chs. 1–2 (~7–9h); frees ~5h. — *Category (iii) note:* late Habermas (*Auch eine Geschichte der Philosophie*, 2019) can stay off; a one-line Context-Layer awareness note suffices.

### A.3 Marx and Western Marxism

**7. Marx, *Die deutsche Ideologie* (1845/46), Feuerbach-chapter selections + the "Thesen über Feuerbach."** — **Category (i).**
*Why:* The programme builds an entire ideology-critique strand — Jaeggi's ideology-critique paper (9.4, conditional MUST-Anchor), Celikates (9.3, MUST-Anchor), Althusser's ideological-state-apparatuses essay (13.5), Shelby (9.5) — **without ever assigning the founding text of Ideologiekritik** (the camera obscura figure; "die Gedanken der herrschenden Klasse"; base/superstructure). Althusser's essay is explicitly a *revision* of this text; Jaeggi's "ideology critique without false consciousness" is a revision of the same. Module 2 currently jumps from the 1844 manuscripts straight to *Kapital*.
*Where/tier:* Module 2 (new item 2.5) or the Module 13 preamble. **MUST-Engage, Light tier** (~40pp selection + the Thesen, 6–8h).
*Cut:* Layer-0 bonus absorption, or trim the *Kapital* working-day chapter selection (2.2b ch. 10) to a Light skim (frees ~4–6h). — *One line, category (iii)-adjacent:* the *Grundrisse* "Einleitung" (1857, the method chapter the *neue Marx-Lektüre* philology leans on) belongs in Appendix C.I as a COULD; promote only if the capitalism-as-form branch fires at retreat R5.

**8. Franz Neumann, *Behemoth* (1942/44), selections (the "totalitarian monopoly capitalism" argument), as the counterpart to Pollock's state-capitalism essay (4.3).** — **Category (i), small.**
*Why:* Pollock's essay was added precisely to stage a Frankfurt-internal debate — but the actual debate was Pollock *versus Neumann* (does the primacy of politics supersede the market economy, or does Nazi Germany remain a — monopoly — capitalism?). Reading 4.3 alone gives one side's framing as if uncontested; the Pollock–Neumann controversy is also the tradition's one genuinely *economic-institutional* argument, i.e., the reader's terrain. Kirchheimer can stay out entirely.
*Where/tier:* Module 4, footnote-level entry after 4.3. **COULD / Context Layer** (~20–30pp, 3–4h); promote to SHOULD only if a state/economy Phase III angle fires. *Cut:* none (Jay's *Dialectical Imagination* provides the frame).

**9. Ernst Bloch.** — **Category (iii), flag only.** *Das Prinzip Hoffnung* is the major Western-Marxist absence, but its natural docking point (utopian surplus, connecting to Honneth's *Die Idee des Sozialismus*, 14.7, and Wright, 14.5) is served well enough by those two texts. One Context-Layer sentence at 14.7 ("Honneth's 'experimental' socialism is a deflated Blochian utopianism — know the reference") suffices. Do not schedule.

## C. Field-map comprehensiveness — Score: 4/5

As a *purpose-relative* map (six spines with a claim on this endpoint), it is honest about its own selectivity (§2's "neither exclusive nor exhaustive" paragraph names the pragmatist, feminist/care, republican, race, Spinozist, and critical-phenomenology spines it excludes). Deductions:

1. **Arendt is entirely absent** — not a spine, not a rival paradigm, not a frontier name. For a tradition whose central concept of communicative power is taken from her, and given the map's own deliberative row, this is the largest single-figure omission.
2. **No actor-network-theory / science-and-technology-studies row.** The rival-paradigm inventory has Luhmann, Bourdieu, critical realism, and analytic political philosophy — but Latour and actor-network theory, the third major contemporary sociology rival (and the one the digital-society frontier debate actually runs through), appear only inside a Reckwitz annotation in the syllabus.
3. **The digital/algorithmic-governance frontier has no home.** The syllabus's own Part IV names it a live debate; the map's spine inventory and watchlist are silent on it.
4. **The Fanon inconsistency**: named as a decolonial founder in the map's §3 while the syllabus omits him — a map↔syllabus alignment failure the map is where one would catch.
5. **The recognition frontier column is thin** (Ikäheimo, Laitinen, Pilapil) — no Deranty (the standard Honneth commentary), no Testa/Hirvonen, no Butler; and the Habermasian row lists Lafont et al. while none had a syllabus hook.
6. Minor: psychoanalytic critical theory (Whitebook, Allen 2021) appears only inside the socialisation branch's frontier list, though it is arguably a cross-cutting method strand like genealogy.

What the map does unusually well: the spine/track/coverage distinction, the lens-carrying-track hazard, the honest equip-levels, the wedge column, and the citation-verification caveats — the last being exactly the uncertainty discipline this audit is asked to enforce.
