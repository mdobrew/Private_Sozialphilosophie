## Dimension-by-dimension

### Dimension 1 — Per-session time-budget realism at 7–8 h/week
**Good indicator:** every practice states a per-instance time that, summed over a realistic week, fits inside ~7.5h including reading; peak weeks are flagged and have a documented relief valve. **Bad indicator:** practice times that only add up if reading is near-zero that week, or that silently assume a full-time cadence.

**Assessment.** The per-instance budgets are individually plausible and well-grounded (Full Recon 30–45 min on top of reading; Light 10–15 min; H1 30–45 min; Anwendung 3.5–4.5h; Skizze 4.5–6h). The "Time allocation rule of thumb" section is genuinely strong: the per-author multipliers (Hegel/Adorno 1:1, Habermas 2:1, analytic 3–4:1) are calibrated to the right reader and the learning-curve caveat is honest. The risk is concentrated at module-close and at productive-practice collisions — handled under Dim 2. The doc's own "do not co-schedule Anwendung/Skizze with a module-close week" rule is the correct instinct but is a *constraint stated in the method doc that the schedule must honour* — it lives here, not in the plan's weekly binding, so it can be violated silently.

**Score: 3.** Per-instance realism is good; the aggregation discipline is asserted rather than enforced. *Weakness type: Calibration-dependent (the multipliers are explicitly flagged as planning estimates to validate against W5/W13 logs — decision rule: if measured overhead deviates >20%, revise here + C.2 + plan App A) + Local (the anti-co-scheduling rule could be stated as a hard scheduling constraint).*

### Dimension 2 — Practice composition vs. competition
**Good indicator:** the practices form one workflow where each feeds the next (A → C1 → B → D → E; H1 → H2; Anwendung/Skizze → W3.2), and the module-close stack has an explicit, metric-gated relief mechanism. **Bad indicator:** practices that nominally coexist but in fact contend for the same fixed module-close hours, with overload "managed" only by exhortation.

**Assessment.** The *feed-forward* design is excellent and is the document's strongest structural idea: reconstructions seed concept entries, concept entries + debate maps seed synthesis notes, synthesis notes + Anwendungen + Skizzen seed W3.2. The "write-then-compare" protocol and the H1↔C1 de-duplication note (line 154) show real attention to not double-counting work. But the *module-close convergence* is the weak point. At a standard module close the reader owes: comprehension check (2–3h, bridge), DM (1h, bridge), H1 (0.5–0.75h), C1 tally, C2 (0.5h), Practice D + negative-space note (1.5–2h) — the doc's own table totals ~5–7h, ~7–10h with QM. That is one to two full weeks' capacity landing in a single week, on top of any reading still owed that week. The taper machinery (H1 M1–M6/alt M7–M14/ad-hoc; C1 M1–M12/then-conditional; D M1–M9/alt M10) is the intended pressure-relief and is well-reasoned, but every taper is justified by "the move consolidates by ~Module 7" — an empirical claim with no measurement hook, unlike the pacing multipliers which DO have the W13 hook. So the composition is sound in principle but the overload mitigation is faith-based.

**Score: 2.** *Weakness type: Structural — the module-close bundle needs either a spread-over-two-weeks rule baked into the method (not just the "slip the Skizze" exception) or the tapers need a falsifiable trigger analogous to the W13 calibration gate. Note this is diagnosed WITHIN Class A: the fix is sequencing/scope, never added hours.*

### Dimension 3 — Checkability / falsifiability against real session output
**Good indicator:** each practice specifies what a *correct* artifact looks like, so the reader can tell pass from fail. **Bad indicator:** practices whose only success criterion is "you did it."

**Assessment.** Practice A is the model: the four-part structure plus the per-part *demarcation cues* (e.g., "if you can substitute the author's name and the sentence still works, you haven't stated the claim precisely enough"; "support is what the author gives you, presupposition is what they need but don't") are genuinely falsifiable tests. The tier table specifies word counts and filed-output formats. The "30% of what you thought you understood dissolves" framing gives a felt success signal. Weaker: Practice D's "name the negative space" is high-value but its success criterion (incoherent/unmotivated/historically-suppressed) is a taxonomy, not a check — a reader can mislabel and not know. Practice E (synthesis) has a soft check ("a W3.2 candidate that doesn't lean on 3–4 synthesis notes is under-integrated") that only bites retrospectively. Practice B has no per-entry quality check beyond the worked example.

**Score: 3.** *Weakness type: Local — A and the tier outputs are checkable; D/E/B would benefit from a one-line "you got it wrong if…" cue each, on the Practice-A model.*

### Dimension 4 — Worked examples from the hardest texts
**Good indicator:** the fully-worked exemplars are drawn from the Dense / Protocol-2 texts (Hegel RP, Phänomenologie, Adorno ND/MM, Lukács) where the method is most at risk. **Bad indicator:** exemplars only from the accessible end, leaving the reader to extrapolate the method to the hard case unaided.

**Assessment.** This is a real gap. The single fully-worked Practice A example is Honneth "Pathologien des Sozialen" — Layered class, Protocol 3, the *easiest* primary on the path (and the doc says so, line 102). Practice B's worked example is *immanente Kritik* (a clean received trajectory). The Skizze worked example is explicitly a **placeholder** with placeholder content (lines 381–399), to be replaced at M22. There is **no** worked reconstruction of a Dense Protocol-2 text. Yet the doc's own time-allocation section concedes Hegel/Adorno run at a 1:1 write:read ratio and that "your first Hegel reconstruction takes 90 minutes" — i.e., precisely the texts where a four-part reconstruction is hardest to produce and where "the support structure has gaps" (Protocol 1 language) is most acute. The reader's first Dense reconstruction is Hegel RP around W14 (per the plan), and they will attempt it with only an easy-text template in hand. The doc partially compensates with Protocol 2 prose-guidance and the Use-2/Use-5 callouts, but a *worked* Hegel or Lukács reconstruction (even an abbreviated one showing how the four parts come apart on resistant prose) would de-risk the highest-failure-probability moment in Phase I.

**Score: 2.** *Weakness type: Structural — add a worked Dense/Protocol-2 exemplar. Best candidate: Lukács reification §I or Hegel RP §§142–157 (Familie), both on the path early and both Dense. This is the single highest-leverage content addition.*

### Dimension 5 — Match to bridge + execution_plan session-structure rules
**Good indicator:** the reading protocols (1/2/3) and per-session rhythm cohere with the execution_plan §B session-structure key (`[dense — 1h daily]`, `[systematic …]`, `[accessible …]`, `[writing — 2h block]`) and the §D.1 reconstruction-week protocol. **Bad indicator:** the method assumes a session rhythm the schedule doesn't provide, or vice versa.

**Assessment.** Strong alignment here. Protocol 1 (systematic, one read then reconstruct) maps to `[systematic/accessible — 1h daily]`; Protocol 2 (two-pass dense) maps to `[dense — 1h daily, don't skip >1 day]` and the consolidation-sitting variant; the "writing 2h block" rule matches `[writing — 2h+ block]`. The §D.1 reconstruction-week protocol in the plan is a faithful instantiation of Practice A Full tier + closing sentence + empirical-presuppositions sub-head + C1 + Use-1 check. The aphoristic-flag handling (MM tolerates 30-min sessions) is consistent between the plan's W35 note and the practices' Appendix C.2 aphoristic rule. One soft mismatch: the practices doc gives reading-protocol choice as a per-text reader decision ("Which reading protocol to use"), while reading_speed_classes.md fixes prose-class per text; these are compatible (class ≠ protocol) but the doc never states the class→protocol correspondence explicitly, leaving the reader to infer that Dense⇒Protocol 2, Layered/Systematic⇒Protocol 1, short⇒Protocol 3.

**Score: 4.** *Weakness type: Local — add one sentence mapping prose-class → reading protocol so the two systems are visibly reconciled.*

### Dimension 6 — Claude-use discipline vs. hallucination risk
**Good indicator:** Claude is sequenced strictly after the reader's own written work, with use-specific hallucination caveats escalating with risk, especially on continental primaries where confident misreadings are the failure mode (per CLAUDE.md). **Bad indicator:** Claude positioned as a source of input, or uniform caveats that don't track actual risk.

**Assessment.** This is the most mature part of the document and is well-aligned with the project's stated posture. The Use 1–5 system is correctly ordered by risk: Use 1 (reconstruction check, both text + reconstruction as anchors) low-risk; Use 4 (steelman) flagged higher-risk with the explicit "require citation of a passage or named secondary; treat uncited defence as invention" discipline; Use 5 (grammar) cleanly separated from Use 2 (philosophical opacity). The "what NOT to use Claude for" list correctly fences off Practice B generation (highest outsourcing risk because it *looks* like reference material) and interpretation-adjudication on contested texts (Hegel metaphysician-vs-social-theorist). The Skizze novelty-check caveat (Claude's "no one has made this move" is moderate-confidence at best) is exactly right. Minor: the inline-callout distribution means the governing sequencing principle is stated late (line 503) relative to where Uses first appear — a reader could hit Use 2 before reading "after your own written work, never before it."

**Score: 4.** *Weakness type: Local — the discipline is sound; only the ordering of the general principle vs. first inline use could be tightened.*

## Most consequential design trade-offs already made

**T1 — Reconstruct-from-memory-then-consult (Protocol 1) over annotate-as-you-read.** *Choice:* one holistic read, close the book, write the reconstruction, then targeted re-consult. *Defensible?* Yes — it is the right call for the goal (producing *reconstructions* not *summaries*) and the cited retrieval-practice evidence (Roediger & Karpicke) supports it. *What would flip it:* if the W5/W13 logs show the reader systematically cannot retain enough Dense-text structure to produce even a skeletal reconstruction from memory (e.g., Hegel first-pass yields no usable claim), the protocol would need a light scaffolding-notes concession for Dense texts specifically — which is partly why the missing Dense worked example (Dim 4) matters.

**T2 — Tiering reconstruction output (Full/Light/Orientation) decoupled from reading-input difficulty.** *Choice:* tier governs the written artifact; prose-class governs reading time; a Light-tier Hegel still pays the 1:1 input multiplier (line 489). *Defensible?* Yes, and it is one of the cleaner ideas in the programme — it lets the capacity envelope flex on output without pretending Hegel reads faster. *What would flip it:* if in practice the reader finds Light-tier output on a Dense text impossible to produce honestly (the two-part claim+directed-against can't be stated without effectively doing the full support analysis), the Light tier on Dense texts would be a fiction and should be re-examined.

**T3 — Heavy productive-practice apparatus (H1, H2, Anwendung, Skizze, synthesis) layered on top of receptive practice.** *Choice:* build the W3.2 muscle early on small stakes via a graded commitment ladder. *Defensible?* Yes given the goal is a *contribution*, not coverage — this is the part of the method most directly serving the endpoint, and the feed-forward chaining is genuine. *What would flip it:* if the cumulative additional hours (Phase II Skizze ~20h + Anwendung ~20–25h + H2 ~10h, additive per line 479) push sustained weekly hours above the ~8–9h overage band for more than 2–3 weeks, the falling-behind signal fires and the productive cadence (not the receptive core) should be the first internal cut — the doc should perhaps say this explicitly, since right now the cut order lives in the bridge/plan, not here.

**T4 — Phase III collapses practices into the piece.** *Choice:* receptive practices suspend during writing blocks; the piece is primary. *Defensible?* Yes — sustaining Phase II cadence through a 7,000–10,000-word production would be unrealistic at 7–8 h/week. *What would flip it:* nothing about the principle; only its execution is at risk via stale anchors (Dim 7/8).

### Unintended tensions (improving one dimension degrades another)

- **Dim 2 (reduce module-close overload) vs. Dim 3 (checkability) and the feed-forward design.** Every taper that relieves load (skip H1, skip C1, alternate D) removes a checkable artifact and a feeder into synthesis/W3.2. Push the taper too hard and the apparatus that distinguishes "knowing each thinker" from "having an integrated apparatus" (Practice E's stated purpose) thins out. The doc's own tapers are calibrated to *skill consolidation*, not *load*, which is the right axis — but if load-driven cutting is what actually happens under pressure, it will silently degrade the integration the programme is built to produce.
- **Dim 4 (add a Dense worked example) vs. Dim 1 (doc length / reader time).** A full Hegel/Lukács worked reconstruction is long; over-specifying it risks the reader treating it as the canonical answer to copy (the same outsourcing failure the doc warns about for Claude/Practice B). Mitigation: show an *abbreviated* or *deliberately-imperfect* Dense reconstruction (where the four parts visibly come apart) rather than a polished model — i.e., demonstrate the method's *struggle*, not a clean output.
- **Dim 8 symbolic-anchor fix vs. reader concreteness.** Replacing literal months with "at R5 / during Writing Block W1" makes the doc rebuild-proof (good) but slightly less concrete for a reader who wants to know *when* — the cost is small and the durability gain is large, but it is a real trade.
