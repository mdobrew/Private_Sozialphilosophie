# Marxist Political Economy: A Self-Study Syllabus

**Goal:** Working command of the Marxist tradition in political economy — from Marx's own critique of political economy through the major twentieth-century schools to the current frontier. The syllabus is designed for someone with PhD-level training in mainstream economics who wants to understand what Marxist economics *is* as an intellectual project, engage with its strongest claims, and evaluate where it challenges or complements the mainstream.

**Assumed background:** Graduate-level micro, macro, and econometrics. Familiarity with general equilibrium, growth theory, and the history of economic thought. Some prior reading of Marx (at least *Kapital* vol. 1, ch. 1). German and English reading fluency.

**Note on the relationship to mainstream economics:** Marxist political economy is not simply "heterodox economics." It is a distinct intellectual tradition with its own questions, methods, and criteria of explanatory success. The central question is not "how do markets allocate resources efficiently?" but "what are the social forms through which economic reproduction is organised, and what contradictions do they generate?" This means that many Marxist debates (the transformation problem, the tendency of the rate of profit to fall, the nature of abstract labour) will look like bad economics to a mainstream economist — until you recognise that they are asking different questions. The syllabus is structured to make these different questions visible before asking you to evaluate the answers.

**Tags:** [P] Primary, [ES] Essential secondary, [RS] Recommended secondary, [O] Orientation, [NE] Non-essential.

## Relationship to the Sozialphilosophie programme (spine B)

This syllabus is the **economic-theoretic** wing of the critique of political economy (CPE). It is meant to be read *alongside* — not inside — the Sozialphilosophie programme's Marxist material (that programme's Modules 13–14 and Appendix C, and "spine B" of its field map). The two divide the labour cleanly:

- **Sozialphilosophie Modules 13–14 / Appendix C** = Marxism *as critical social theory*: the value-form / *neue Marx-Lektüre* (Backhaus, Reichelt, Sohn-Rethel, Arthur), Postone, Bonefeld, Gramsci, Althusser, the Budapest School, operaismo, eco-Marxism, and the recognition-meets-capitalism handshake (*Nach Marx*). The question there is what it means for social relations to take the *form* of relations between commodities.
- **This syllabus** = Marxism *as a research programme in economics*: the transformation problem, crisis dynamics, the falling rate of profit, imperialism-as-economics, the régulation school, financialization, money and central banking. The question here is quantitative, dynamic, and institutional.

They overlap at exactly three nodes: **Heinrich** (the value-form bridge — Module 1 here, Module 2 / Appendix C there), **Lapavitsas** (financialization — Modules 5 and 7 here, item 14.14 there), and the **régulation school** (Module 6 here, item 14.9 + Appendix C.IX there). To avoid double-counting, treat Lapavitsas's central-bank material as *owned* by the Sozialphilosophie side (it is load-bearing for the spine-B wedge) and read it once.

**The productive asymmetry.** Note what the division implies: the *philosophical* value-form texts that constitute spine B can pose a wedge — "real abstraction as an operation," "money as measure" (Engster), "the central bank as the missing institution of value-form theory" — that they **cannot themselves service**. None of Backhaus, Sohn-Rethel, or Engster can formalise what real abstraction *is* as an operation. The quantitative apparatus assembled here — the monetary expression of labour time (the New Interpretation, Module 2), Shaikh's empirical value–price work, profit-rate measurement, the Marxist theory of money and central banking (Module 7) — is the toolkit for exactly that. **The highest-value use of this syllabus for the Sozialphilosophie endpoint is as the instrument the spine-B target requires.** Where a reading here arms that wedge, it is flagged **[→ spine B]**.

## MODULE 1: MARX'S CRITIQUE OF POLITICAL ECONOMY — THE SYSTEM

**Why this matters:** Everything else in Marxist political economy is a development, correction, or rejection of Marx's own argument. The mainstream reception of Marx (through Samuelson, Morishima, or Roemer) typically extracts individual claims — the labour theory of value, the falling rate of profit — and evaluates them against neoclassical standards. The *neue Marx-Lektüre* and the value-form tradition argue that this fundamentally misunderstands the project: Marx's critique is not a rival *theory of prices* but a theory of the *social forms* through which economic life is constituted. This module covers both readings.

## MODULE 2: THE TRANSFORMATION PROBLEM AND VALUE THEORY DEBATES

**Why this matters:** The "transformation problem" — how to move consistently from labour-values to prices of production — has been the central technical controversy in Marxist economics since Böhm-Bawerk's critique (1896) and Bortkiewicz's correction (1907). Whether you think it is a genuine problem or a pseudo-problem generated by misreading Marx depends on your interpretation of value theory. This module covers the main positions.

| # | Text | Type | Est. hours | Notes |
|---|------|------|-----------|-------|
| 2.1 | Ian Steedman, *Marx after Sraffa* (1977) | [P] | 15–20 | The Sraffian critique: given Sraffa's *Production of Commodities by Means of Commodities* (1960), labour values are redundant — prices of production can be determined from physical input-output data without passing through values. If Steedman is right, the value concept has no explanatory role. The most influential analytical challenge to Marxist value theory. |
| 2.2 | Andrew Kliman, *Reclaiming Marx's "Capital": A Refutation of the Myth of Inconsistency* (2007), chs. 1–6 | [P] | 15–20 | The "temporal single-system interpretation" (TSSI): Marx's transformation is consistent if values and prices are determined simultaneously in a single system and if inputs are valued at their historical (temporal) rather than replacement cost. Kliman argues that the entire "inconsistency" literature rests on a misinterpretation. Technically demanding but clearly argued. |
| 2.3 | Fred Moseley, *Money and Totality: A Macro-Monetary Interpretation of Marx's Logic in Capital and the End of the "Transformation Problem"* (2016), chs. 1–5 | [P] | 18–22 | A "macro-monetary" reading: Marx's theory is about the total surplus-value produced by the total social capital, not about individual commodity values. The transformation problem is dissolved by recognising that Marx works at the macro level — total profit = total surplus-value — and the distribution of surplus-value among individual capitals is a secondary, derivative question. |
| 2.4 | Michael Heinrich, "Monetäre Werttheorie" (in *Die Wissenschaft vom Wert*, 2011, ch. 5); or Heinrich, *An Introduction to the Three Volumes of Karl Marx's Capital* (2012), ch. 3 | [ES] | 8–12 | Heinrich's "monetary theory of value": value has no pre-monetary existence. Value is constituted only in exchange, which means the substance of value (abstract labour) is a real abstraction (*Realabstraktion*) that occurs in the exchange process, not a property of individual commodities. The most philosophically sophisticated position in the debate. |
| 2.5 | Anwar Shaikh, *Capitalism: Competition, Conflict, Crises* (2016), chs. 6–9 | [RS] | 20–25 | Shaikh's "classical-Marxian" approach: a rigorous alternative to both neoclassical and Sraffian economics that retains labour values as explanatory. The most technically demanding work on the list. Shaikh argues that actual ("real") competition — not perfect competition — is the organising principle, and demonstrates empirically that labour values closely track actual prices. Given your econometric background, the empirical chapters will be the most accessible entry point. |
| 2.6 | Gérard Duménil & Duncan Foley, "The New Interpretation" — read via Duncan Foley, *Understanding Capital: Marx's Economic Theory* (Harvard UP, 1986), chs. 2–6, with Foley, "The Value of Money, the Value of Labour Power and the Marxian Transformation Problem" (*Review of Radical Political Economics* 14(2), 1982) | [ES] | 8–12 | **The most influential academic solution, and the one closest to your training.** The "New Interpretation" (also "New Solution"; Duménil and Foley independently, c. 1980–82) dissolves the transformation problem by reinterpreting two aggregate equalities: the value of labour-power is set equal to the *wage share* (not to a bundle of wage goods), and the **monetary expression of labour time (MELT)** converts aggregate value into money directly. Total price = total value *and* total profit = total surplus-value then both hold by construction. The MELT is an *estimable ratio* — the entry point for treating value theory econometrically. **[→ spine B]** The MELT is the most precise existing candidate for "the operation by which qualitatively distinct labours are rendered commensurable" that the value-form tradition asserts but does not formalise. |
| 2.7 | Nobuo Okishio, "Technical Changes and the Rate of Profit" (*Kobe University Economic Review* 7, 1961) — read via a modern exposition (Foley, *Understanding Capital*, ch. 8; or John Roemer, *Analytical Foundations of Marxian Economic Theory*, 1981, ch. 4) | [ES] | 5–8 | The formal pivot of the entire falling-rate-of-profit debate. Okishio's theorem: a viable cost-reducing technical change, with the real wage held constant, *raises* the equilibrium rate of profit — the opposite of Marx's tendency. Every later position on the falling rate — Kliman's TSSI (2.2), the temporalist reply, Shaikh (2.5) — is in effect a response to Okishio. You cannot properly evaluate Module 3's crisis theories without it. |
| 2.8 | Michio Morishima, *Marx's Economics: A Dual Theory of Value and Growth* (Cambridge UP, 1973), chs. 1–6 (selections) | [RS] | 12–15 | The mathematical economist's Marx: von Neumann growth models applied to *Kapital*. The "Fundamental Marxian Theorem" — positive profit iff positive surplus-value (the rate of exploitation is positive) — is the rigorous result that survives the transformation debate. Morishima is sympathetic but reconstructs Marx in equilibrium-theoretic terms the value-form tradition rejects; read for the contrast in *method*, not only result. |

**Module total: 69–94 hours (core without [RS]: 69–94 hours; with [RS]: 101–134 hours)**

## MODULE 3: CRISIS THEORY

**Why this matters:** Marxist political economy's most distinctive claim is that capitalist crises are not exogenous shocks or policy failures but *endogenous* to the logic of capital accumulation. The tradition offers three major crisis theories — underconsumption, disproportionality, and the falling rate of profit — and the debate between them remains unresolved.

|---|------|------|-----------|-------|

| 3.2 | Henryk Grossman, *Das Akkumulations- und Zusammenbruchsgesetz des kapitalistischen Systems* (1929); or Rick Kuhn, *Henryk Grossman and the Recovery of Marxism* (2007), chs. 4–6 as substitute | [P] | 18–22 | The most rigorous classical statement of the falling-rate-of-profit thesis as a theory of capitalist breakdown. Kuhn's intellectual biography is the best secondary. |

## MODULE 4: THEORIES OF IMPERIALISM AND WORLD-SYSTEMS

**Why this matters:** Marxist political economy has always insisted that capitalism is a global system, not a collection of national economies. The theories of imperialism (Luxemburg, Hilferding, Lenin, Bukharin) and the world-systems tradition (Wallerstein, Arrighi) are the major attempts to theorise capitalism's global dynamics.

## MODULE 6: THE REGULATION SCHOOL AND INSTITUTIONAL APPROACHES

**Why this matters:** The French *école de la régulation* (Aglietta, Boyer, Lipietz) represents the most influential attempt to bridge Marxist crisis theory and institutional economics. The core concept: capitalism passes through historically specific "modes of regulation" (Fordism, post-Fordism) that temporarily stabilise its contradictions through institutional arrangements — until those arrangements themselves enter crisis.

|---|------|------|-----------|-------|

## MODULE 7: MONEY, CREDIT, AND CENTRAL BANKING

**[Added 2026-06-30 — optional content module; cut if the schedule is tight.]**

**Why this matters:** The Marxist theory of money is the tradition's most underdeveloped and — for a central banker — most exploitable wing. Marx's own monetary theory is scattered (Module 1: *Kapital* vol. 1 ch. 3 on money as measure / means / hoard; vol. 3 Part V on credit, interest-bearing and fictitious capital), and the twentieth-century tradition left the *modern central bank* almost untheorised: the *Staatsableitung* (state-derivation) debate derived the capitalist state but stopped short of its monetary institutions. This is precisely the gap your professional knowledge can fill. **[→ spine B]** "The central bank as the institutional form of the social validation of money" is a contribution only someone who knows monetary operations from the inside can write; this module assembles the tradition such a piece would intervene in.

**[Economist's note]** This is where the asymmetry flagged in the opening section is sharpest. The literature here *asserts* that money is the social validation of private labour but treats the institution that actually performs that validation — the central bank, the monetary standard, the lender of last resort — as a black box. You operate that box. The intervention is not "apply Marx to central banking" but the reverse: specify, with the operational detail the tradition lacks, *what a value-form theory of money commits one to* about the institution that anchors it. Pair with Sozialphilosophie 14.13 (Engster, money as measure) and 14.14 (Lapavitsas, central banks).

## FRONTIER: LIVE DEBATES

Modelled on the "Reaching the Frontier" layer of the Sozialphilosophie syllabus. This section introduces almost no new body of texts; it stages the **live disputes** — each as a question with its opposing camps and the texts (mostly already assigned above) that fight it out. Work any one as a position-mapping exercise. Where a debate needs a short reading not assigned above, it is listed inline; citation-uncertain items are flagged for verification.

**1. Does the rate of profit fall — and did it, empirically?**
Two layers. *Theoretically:* the Okishio theorem (2.7) says no (viable technical change raises the profit rate); the TSSI (Kliman, 2.2) and Shaikh (2.5) say the theorem's simultaneous-equilibrium assumption is the error. *Empirically:* whether the US / world profit rate actually fell in recent decades is itself disputed — Kliman and Michael Roberts (3.3) say yes; critics (Deepankar Basu and collaborators) reach different conclusions depending on the treatment of fixed-capital valuation, deflators, and the financial sector. *This is the most quantitatively tractable debate in the field and your most direct entry point.* (E.g. Basu & Vasudevan, "Technology, distribution and the rate of profit in the US economy," *Cambridge Journal of Economics* 2013 — verify title before citing.)

**2. What caused the post-2008 crisis and the long downturn?**
Three Marxist explanations of the *same* events: falling rate of profit (Roberts, 3.3; Grossman lineage, 3.2); over-capacity from inter-capitalist competition (Brenner, 3.6); monopoly-stagnation-plus-financialization (Foster & Magdoff, 3.8; Lapavitsas, 5.2). They are not reconcilable and they imply different politics. Map them against each other and against the mainstream (financial-frictions / savings-glut) account you know professionally.

**3. Did late Marx abandon the breakdown theory?**
A MEGA²-philological dispute with large stakes for Modules 1 and 3. Michael Heinrich, "Crisis Theory, the Law of the Tendency of the Profit Rate to Fall, and Marx's Studies in the 1870s" (*Monthly Review* 64(11), April 2013), argues that Marx never completed — and may have been abandoning — the falling-rate law, and that Engels's editing of vol. 3 imposed a systematic argument Marx had left open. The TSSI camp (Kliman, Freeman) replied sharply. Bears directly on how much weight a falling-rate crisis theory can carry. (~2–3 h; the Module 1 reading lists also lack any treatment of the Engels-editorship / unfinished-*Kapital* / MEGA² question that frames this.)

**4. Is there a systematic "drain" from South to North?**
The classical unequal-exchange thesis (Emmanuel, 4.7; Marini's super-exploitation, 4.6; John Smith, 5.3) has been revived in quantitative form: recent national-accounts estimates of value / resource transfer from the global South (Jason Hickel, Dylan Sullivan and collaborators, e.g. "Plunder in the Post-Colonial Era," *New Political Economy* 2021 — verify). Critics (e.g. Charles Post on super-exploitation; the Brenner "internalist" line) dispute both mechanism and magnitudes. Fully specifiable, hence open to an economist. (~4–6 h.)

**5. Capitalism and the biosphere (eco-Marxism / metabolic rift).**
The most active Marxist frontier circa 2026, and the major gap in this syllabus *by design*: it is housed in the companion programme (Sozialphilosophie Appendix C.VIII — Saito, Foster, Moore, Malm). The economic-register version — ecological-economics accounting of the "metabolic rift," and the degrowth-vs-ecomodernism dispute — would be the natural extension here if a contribution turned toward nature / Anthropocene themes. Left as a pointer rather than duplicated.
