### Capability targets — by end of the Motivational Checkpoint you should be able to:

- [ ] Follow the argumentative structure and main references of a recent journal article in Sozialphilosophie, identifying which concepts you command, which you don't, and which thinker (typically Habermas) is the missing piece.
- [ ] Apply the Frankfurt apparatus reflexively to a phenomenon from your professional world: identify a practice's self-understanding, find a tension on its own terms, attempt a structural explanation, and explicitly name where your apparatus runs out.
- [ ] State (a) the 2–3 *strongest* and (b) 2–3 *weakest* points in Part I's intellectual apparatus as you now understand them — the weak points being candidate openings for your own contribution.

### Why this checkpoint matters
The Motivational Checkpoint is the Phase I hinge between historical foundations (Modules 1–5) and the systematic core (Module 6 Habermas, beginning in Phase II). Its function is twofold: (a) confirm via the Journal Test that the apparatus you've built actually unlocks access to contemporary debates — that you can now follow what couldn't be followed at QM1 (W17); (b) via the Field Report, generate *motivated reading questions* for the systematic core by applying the apparatus to a phenomenon from your own world and noticing where the apparatus runs out. The Field Report is the *primary seed* for the Phase III W3.2 publishable piece — its part (d) ("what apparatus do you still need?") directly drives Phase II Module 6 reading.

### Week 57 | 📋 Motivational Checkpoint — Field Report
- **Core (~8–10h):** **Field Report (Motivational Checkpoint Week 2).**
  - Write a **1,500–2,000 word diagnostic exercise** on a phenomenon from your ECB world: e.g., ECB communication / forward guidance practices; DSGE modelling conventions; the institutional logic of central bank independence; "financial stability" as a normative category in policy discourse.
  - Structure:
    (a) Identify the practice and its self-understanding. What norms does it implicitly invoke?
    (b) Identify a tension or failure on the practice's own terms (immanent-critique move).
    (c) Attempt a structural explanation: why is the failure systematic, not accidental?
    (d) Note where your apparatus runs out — what concepts do you need that you don't yet have?
  - Part (d) generates motivated reading questions for Module 6 (Habermas). This is the *point* of the exercise.
- **File the report alongside the Honneth and Horkheimer reconstructions. Return to it after Part II (end of Phase II).**
