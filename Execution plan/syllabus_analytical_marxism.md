# Analytical Marxism: A Self-Study Syllabus

**Goal:** Working command of the "analytical Marxist" or "rational-choice Marxist" tradition — the attempt, primarily by Anglophone philosophers and social scientists from the late 1970s onward, to reconstruct Marx's central claims (the theory of history, exploitation, class, ideology) using the tools of analytic philosophy, rational-choice theory, and game theory. The tradition is sometimes called "No-Bullshit Marxism" (the September Group's self-description) or, by its critics, "Marxism without Marx" — both labels capture something real.

**Assumed background:** PhD-level economics. Familiarity with rational-choice theory, game theory, general equilibrium, and the basics of analytic philosophy of science (explanation, causation, reduction). Some prior reading of Marx.

**Why this tradition matters even if you don't end up endorsing it:** Analytical Marxism represents the most rigorous attempt to hold Marxist claims to the standards of explanatory precision that mainstream social science demands. Even if the Frankfurt tradition is right that these standards miss something essential — that the "critique of political economy" is not a social-scientific theory in the standard sense but a diagnostic enterprise — the analytical Marxists force you to ask: what exactly *is* the alternative? When Jaeggi says that capitalism "generates problems it cannot solve," is this a causal claim, a functional claim, a normative claim, or something else? G.A. Cohen, Elster, and Roemer push these questions with a clarity that the Frankfurt tradition often avoids.

**Relationship to the Sozialphilosophie syllabus:** The Frankfurt tradition largely rejects analytical Marxism — Habermas's and Honneth's projects are built on different methodological foundations (hermeneutics, pragmatism, Hegelian reconstruction) that are incompatible with rational-choice individualism. But the *questions* analytical Marxism raises — about exploitation, functional explanation, the micro-foundations of class — are questions the Frankfurt tradition must answer, and reading the analytical Marxists sharpens your ability to identify where the Frankfurt tradition's arguments are rigorous and where they rely on unexamined assumptions.

**Tags:** [P] Primary, [ES] Essential secondary, [O] Orientation, [NE] Non-essential.

## MODULE 1: G.A. COHEN — HISTORICAL MATERIALISM RECONSTRUCTED

**Why this matters:** Cohen's *Karl Marx's Theory of History* is one of the most rigorous pieces of social philosophy written in the twentieth century. He reconstructs Marx's theory of history as a systematic thesis about the relationship between productive forces and relations of production, using the tools of analytic philosophy to distinguish what the theory actually claims from what it doesn't. Even if you reject the theory, the *method* — precise statement of claims, clear identification of the mode of inference, explicit discussion of what would count as a counterexample — is what the Frankfurt tradition could learn from.

| # | Text | Type | Est. hours | Notes |
|---|------|------|-----------|-------|
| 1.1 | G.A. Cohen, *Karl Marx's Theory of History: A Defence* (1978; expanded ed. 2000) | [P] | 40–50 | The founding text of analytical Marxism. Cohen reconstructs historical materialism as two theses: (1) the *development thesis* (the productive forces tend to develop throughout history); (2) the *primacy thesis* (the relations of production are explained by the level of development of the productive forces, via a functional explanation). The expanded edition includes a valuable new preface and appendix responding to critics. The central methodological debate: is functional explanation legitimate in social theory? Cohen says yes; Elster (Module 2) says no. ~400 pages but analytically clear — will read faster than Hegel. |
| 1.2 | Cohen, "Reconsidering Historical Materialism" (in *History, Labour, and Freedom*, 1988) | [P] | 3–5 | Cohen's own partial retraction: he concedes that the development thesis may be empirically false and that historical materialism may need to be replaced by a theory centred on class struggle rather than productive-force determinism. ~25 pages. Important because it shows analytical Marxism's willingness to revise core claims in light of argument — a quality the Frankfurt tradition sometimes lacks. |
| 1.3 | Cohen, *Self-Ownership, Freedom, and Equality* (1995), chs. 1–5 | [P] | 18–22 | Cohen's engagement with Nozick and libertarianism. Argues that self-ownership — the thesis that people own themselves and the products of their labour — does *not* justify capitalism (contra Nozick) because the initial appropriation of natural resources cannot be justified on self-ownership grounds. A model of analytically precise political philosophy. Connects to the exploitation debate (Module 3). |
| 1.4 | Cohen, *Why Not Socialism?* (2009) | [RS] | 2–3 | A short (~80 pages), accessible late essay. Cohen's positive case for socialism based on the values of community and equality, using the extended metaphor of a camping trip. Useful as an entry point but philosophically light compared to the major works. |

**Module total: 61–77 hours (core without [RS]: 61–77 hours)**

## MODULE 2: JON ELSTER — METHODOLOGICAL INDIVIDUALISM AND THE CRITIQUE OF FUNCTIONAL EXPLANATION

**Why this matters:** Elster is the analytical Marxists' methodological enforcer. His central claim: Marxism is full of functional explanations (capitalism exists *because* it serves the interests of the bourgeoisie; the state has a certain form *because* it functions to reproduce capitalist relations) that are illegitimate unless a *mechanism* is specified that explains how the beneficial consequence brings about or sustains the arrangement. Without a mechanism, functional explanation is just teleology in disguise. This critique is directly relevant to the Frankfurt tradition: when Habermas says that systemic media "colonise" the Lebenswelt, or when Jaeggi says that forms of life "solve" or "fail to solve" problems, are these functional explanations? If so, what are the mechanisms?

| # | Text | Type | Est. hours | Notes |
|---|------|------|-----------|-------|
| 2.1 | Jon Elster, *Making Sense of Marx* (1985), chs. 1–4 (methodology), 6–7 (exploitation and class), and 15 (ideology) | [P] | 35–45 | The most comprehensive analytical reconstruction of Marx. Elster systematically evaluates every major Marxist concept against the standard of methodological individualism: can this claim be reduced to claims about individual agents, their beliefs, desires, and interactions? Where it can't, Elster argues it should be rejected or reformulated. Chapters on functional explanation (ch. 1), dialectics (ch. 2), and ideology (ch. 15) are the most relevant for the Sozialphilosophie trajectory. ~500 pages but selectively readable. |
| 2.2 | Elster, *Nuts and Bolts for the Social Sciences* (1989) | [O] | 8–10 | Short, accessible introduction to mechanism-based social explanation. Useful independently of the Marxism context. |
| 2.3 | Elster, "Marxism, Functionalism, and Game Theory: The Case for Methodological Individualism" (*Theory and Society* 11(4), 1982) | [P] | 3–5 | The article-length version of the methodological argument. ~25 pages. The most concentrated statement of the anti-functionalist position. If you read only one Elster text, read this. |

## MODULE 3: JOHN ROEMER — EXPLOITATION, CLASS, AND RATIONAL CHOICE

**Why this matters:** Roemer reconstructs exploitation and class using general equilibrium theory. His central result: exploitation can be defined without the labour theory of value — it is a consequence of differential ownership of productive assets. A person is exploited if they would be better off under an equal distribution of assets and their exploiter would be worse off. This is technically precise and philosophically radical: it severs exploitation from the labour theory of value, which means you can be a Marxist about exploitation without being a Marxist about value.

| # | Text | Type | Est. hours | Notes |
|---|------|------|-----------|-------|
| 3.1 | John Roemer, *A General Theory of Exploitation and Class* (1982), chs. 1–5 and 7 | [P] | 25–30 | The technical core. Uses general-equilibrium models to derive exploitation from unequal asset ownership. Chapter 7 extends the analysis to "status exploitation" and "needs exploitation," broadening the concept beyond the labour-capital relation. Formally demanding but given your training, the models will be familiar. |
| 3.2 | Roemer, *Free to Lose: An Introduction to Marxist Economic Philosophy* (1988) | [O] | 10–12 | The accessible version of the argument, written for non-specialists. Covers exploitation, class, historical materialism, and the normative case for socialism. If you want a quick orientation before the technical work, start here. |
| 3.3 | Roemer, "Should Marxists Be Interested in Exploitation?" (*Philosophy & Public Affairs* 14(1), 1985) | [P] | 3–5 | Roemer's own provocative question: if exploitation can be reduced to unequal asset ownership, and if the normative problem with unequal asset ownership is *injustice* (unfair distribution), then exploitation is a *symptom* of injustice, not a distinct normative wrong. This has radical implications: it suggests that the Marxist concept of exploitation adds nothing to a Rawlsian (or luck-egalitarian) theory of distributive justice. ~25 pages. The Frankfurt tradition would reject this reduction — exploitation is a *social relation*, not merely a distributional outcome — but Roemer forces you to say precisely why. |
| 3.4 | Nicholas Vrousalis, "Exploitation, Vulnerability, and Social Domination" (*Philosophy & Public Affairs* 41(2), 2013) | [P] | 3–5 | The best recent philosophical treatment of exploitation. Argues that exploitation is fundamentally about *taking advantage of someone's vulnerability for one's own benefit* — a relational concept that cannot be reduced to distributional inequality (contra Roemer). Connects to the recognition-theory tradition: exploitation as a form of instrumentalising disrespect. |

**Module total: 41–52 hours**

## MODULE 4: ERIK OLIN WRIGHT — CLASS ANALYSIS AND REAL UTOPIAS

**Why this matters:** Wright is the analytical Marxist who most seriously engages with empirical social science. His class-analysis framework attempts to operationalise class as a structural concept that can be measured, modelled, and tested — making Marxist class theory commensurable with mainstream stratification research. His late work on "real utopias" provides a systematic framework for evaluating institutional alternatives to capitalism.

| # | Text | Type | Est. hours | Notes |
|---|------|------|-----------|-------|
| 4.1 | Erik Olin Wright, *Classes* (1985), chs. 1–4 | [P] | 15–18 | The concept of "contradictory class locations": positions in the class structure that are simultaneously exploiting and exploited (e.g., managers who exploit workers but are exploited by owners). Uses game-theoretic and Roemerian exploitation concepts. |
| 4.2 | Wright, *Class Counts: Comparative Studies in Class Analysis* (1997), chs. 1–3 | [P] | 12–15 | Empirical operationalisation of the class framework across multiple countries. Uses survey data. The most rigorous attempt to bridge Marxist class theory and quantitative social science. |
| 4.3 | Wright, *Envisioning Real Utopias* (2010) | [P] | 20–25 | Three transformation strategies: ruptural (revolutionary overthrow), interstitial (building alternatives within capitalism), symbiotic (using the state to expand non-capitalist institutions). The framework is agnostic about which strategy is correct and instead provides criteria for evaluating each. Connects to Jaeggi's problem-solving model: if a form of life is pathological, what alternatives are feasible? |
| 4.4 | Wright, "Understanding Class" (*New Left Review* 60, 2009) | [P] | 3–5 | Already in Appendix F of the Sozialphilosophie syllabus (item F.8). The most efficient overview of Wright's class framework. If you read only one Wright text, read this. |

**Module total: 50–63 hours**

## A NOTE ON RELATIONSHIP TO THE FRANKFURT TRADITION

The analytical Marxists and the Frankfurt School share a common ancestor (Marx) but are methodologically incompatible in important ways. Three points of tension are worth tracking as you read:

**1. Methodological individualism vs. social ontology.** Analytical Marxism insists that all social explanations must ultimately be reducible to claims about individual agents (their beliefs, desires, and interactions). The Frankfurt tradition denies this: social forms (commodity, recognition, communicative action) have a reality that is not reducible to individual psychology. When Honneth says that recognition is the "normative grammar" of social life, he is making a claim about a social structure that cannot be captured by a game between rational individuals. Who is right? The analytical Marxists force you to be precise about what "social structure" means and what explanatory work it does.

**2. Functional explanation.** Elster's critique of functional explanation in Marxism applies with equal force to the Frankfurt tradition. When Habermas says that systemic media "colonise" the Lebenswelt, this looks like a functional explanation (the colonisation happens *because* it serves the functional requirements of the economic and administrative systems). Is a mechanism specified? The Frankfurt tradition would respond that its explanatory mode is not functional but *diagnostic* — it identifies pathologies, not mechanisms. Whether this response is satisfying is a question you should track.

**3. The normative status of exploitation.** Roemer's argument that exploitation reduces to unjust asset distribution, and that therefore exploitation is not a distinct normative wrong, is a direct challenge to the Frankfurt tradition's use of "exploitation" as a diagnostic category. Jaeggi's response (implicit in *Kritik von Lebensformen*) would be that exploitation is a *social practice* with its own internal norms, not merely a distributional outcome — and that Roemer's reduction misses the phenomenological and social-ontological dimension of what it means to be exploited. Vrousalis's domination-based account of exploitation (3.4) is the best available bridge between these positions.
