## APPENDIX I: BUILDING TOWARD HEGEL — KANT, GERMAN IDEALISM, AND HEGEL

**Purpose:** Module 1 covers exactly as much Hegel as the Sozialphilosophie frontier requires — the *Rechtsphilosophie* read through Neuhouser, the master–slave dialectic, and the method of normative reconstruction. This appendix is for the reader who wants to *build the full prerequisite arc into Hegel* (Kant → Fichte/Schelling → Hegel) and/or to read Hegel in depth as Hegel. It is a deepening: like Appendix G, the bulk is [NE] relative to the programme's deliverables. The conversation underlying it (`hegel_reading_conversation.md`) costs the full deep-Hegel programme at ~725–940 hours net new — roughly half the core programme — and concludes that only ~45–60h of it actually earns a place on the critical path. Those ~45–60h (*Moralität* §§34–141; *Phänomenologie* ch. VI) have been folded into core **Module 1** and the bonus note after **Module 4**; they reappear here, marked **[core]**, so the build-toward-Hegel arc is complete in one place.

**Structure:** the appendix builds *toward* Hegel rather than starting with him. **I.0** orients the Kant→Hegel arc as a whole (and maps the live interpretive dispute); **I.1** is Kant (the non-negotiable prerequisite); **I.2** is Fichte and Schelling (orientation level, read largely through Hegel's own early polemics); **I.3** is the Greek background (Aristotle elevated, Plato optional); **I.4** is Hegel, with a separate table per major work so that each primary text and its dedicated secondary literature stay together.

**Two reading rules (carried from the conversation, with one correction):**
- *Read in German where the terminology is load-bearing.* For Hegel and Kant **primary** texts, German is non-negotiable — *Aufhebung*, *Sittlichkeit*, *Geist*, *an sich / für sich / an und für sich*, *Vermittlung* lose structural precision in translation. The rule does **not** extend with the same force to **secondary/orientation** literature, which *explains* rather than *enacts* the conceptual moves; there, choose the best text regardless of language (several best-in-class arc-surveys are anglophone).
- *Do not skip Introductions — but do not open the* Phänomenologie *on the* Vorrede. The *Einleitung* states the method (determinate negation); the *Vorrede* was written last, presupposes the whole, and is the single most over-attempted-too-early text in Hegel. Read it after chs. VI–VIII.

**A note on markers:** the whole appendix is [NE]-by-default relative to the frontier; items carry their natural tiers within that. **[core]** marks items the conversation/critique judged to actually bite for Sozialphilosophie (now folded into Modules 1/4). **[HaH]** ("Hegel-as-Hegel") marks items that matter mainly for reading Hegel *as Hegel* — the metaphysics question, the große Logik in full — so they stay visibly distinct from the build-toward-Hegel spine. The [P]/[ES]/[RS]/[O]/[NE] tiers are as defined in the main marker key. *Page counts and hour figures are approximate and edition-dependent; treat them as ballpark.*

### I.0a: Map of the Interpretive Dispute

This is a *signpost*, not a reading list to complete: when a passage or a debate sends you into the secondary literature, it tells you which camp a given book speaks from. The conversation presents the "non-metaphysical" Hegel (Pippin, Pinkard, Brandom) as "contemporary consensus." **That is itself a partisan position in a live dispute, and a minority-trending one.** It matters directly for the endpoint: Honneth, Jaeggi, and Khurana (syllabus 1.6) increasingly lean on a more *ontological / life-based* Hegel, against the deflationary reading — so anchoring on Pippin would place you on the wrong side of the live Sozialphilosophie debate. All items below are [NE] / [HaH]; page counts approximate.

1. **Old "cosmic-spirit" metaphysical reading** — *Geist* as a cosmic subject realising itself through nature and history. Largely treated as caricature now, but worth knowing as the foil. Charles Taylor, *Hegel* (1975, ~580 pp); J.N. Findlay, *Hegel: A Re-examination* (1958, ~370 pp).
2. **Non-metaphysical / post-Kantian / "Pittsburgh" reading** — Hegel completes Kant's critical project; rational norms are self-authorising; no pre-critical ontology. Klaus Hartmann, "Hegel: A Non-Metaphysical View" (1972, ~25 pp; *the source*); Robert Pippin, *Hegel's Idealism* (1989, ~300 pp); Terry Pinkard, *The Sociality of Reason* (1994, ~450 pp); Robert Brandom, *A Spirit of Trust* (2019, ~800 pp). (John McDowell adjacent.)
3. **Revised / "new" metaphysical reading (the revival, currently ascendant)** — Hegel *does* have a metaphysics, but a post-Kantian one (of the *Begriff*, the *Idee*, the Absolute), not pre-critical rationalism. James Kreines, *Reason in the World* (2015, ~290 pp); Brady Bowman, *Hegel and the Metaphysics of Absolute Negativity* (2013, ~290 pp); Robert Stern, *Hegelian Metaphysics* (2009, ~390 pp); Stephen Houlgate, *The Opening of Hegel's Logic* (2006, ~470 pp) and *Hegel on Being* (2 vols., 2021–22, ~1,100 pp); Karen Ng, *Hegel's Concept of Life* (2020, ~320 pp). (Also Kenneth Westphal, *Hegel's Epistemological Realism*, ~250 pp; Beiser, *Hegel*, ~350 pp.)
4. **German strands** — Dieter Henrich, *Hegel im Kontext* (1971, ~210 pp) and *Grundlegung aus dem Ich* (2 Bde., 2004, ~1,700 pp; genesis of self-consciousness); Michael Theunissen, *Sein und Schein* (1978, ~530 pp; Logik — claimed by more than one camp); Joachim Ritter, *Hegel und die französische Revolution* (1957, ~80 pp; the liberal *Rechtsphilosophie* reading, influential in postwar German political philosophy); Klaus Vieweg, *Hegel: Der Philosoph der Freiheit* (2019, ~800 pp); Pirmin Stekeler-Weithofer, *Hegels Phänomenologie des Geistes* (2 Bde., 2014, ~1,500 pp; Brandom-adjacent).
5. **Critical-theory appropriation** — *where your endpoint sits*; trending toward camp 3/5. Theodor Adorno, *Drei Studien zu Hegel* (1963, ~110 pp) and *Negative Dialektik* (1966, ~410 pp); Herbert Marcuse, *Reason and Revolution* (1941, ~430 pp); Axel Honneth, *Leiden an Unbestimmtheit* (2001, ~130 pp); Thomas Khurana, *Das Leben der Freiheit* (2017, ~550 pp; syllabus 1.6). Karen Ng bridges camps 3 and 5.

### I.1: Kant — The Non-Negotiable Prerequisite

The minimum Kant you need: transcendental idealism, *Verstand* vs. *Vernunft*, the thing-in-itself problem, and why Kant thinks traditional (dogmatic) metaphysics is impossible. Hegel's whole programme is the response: Kant was right to reject dogmatic metaphysics, wrong to conclude that reason cannot know the unconditioned. The selection below is re-weighted from the conversation per the critique — the Antinomies elevated, the B-Deduction capped, the Aesthetic kept short, the Amphiboly added. *References use Kant's standard **A/B** pagination (KrV: A = 1st ed. 1781, B = 2nd ed. 1787) and **AA** (Akademie-Ausgabe) / **BA** (original edition) pagination for the practical works — all printed in the margins of the Weischedel/Suhrkamp study edition, so navigate by those, not by volume page numbers. As an orienting lead-in, read the* **B-Vorrede + Einleitung** *(BVII–BXLIV, B1–B30: the analytic/synthetic distinction and "wie sind synthetische Urteile a priori möglich?", B19) before I.1.1.*

**[Economist's note]** The B-Deduction (I.1.2) is where a strong autodidact can burn 30 hours for marginal Hegel return. Resist the urge to "solve" it: what Hegel needs is the *shape* of the transcendental argument (conditions of possibility of experience), not mastery of every step. Conversely the Antinomies (I.1.4) repay close reading — Hegel's entire reconstrual of contradiction-as-productive begins there, and it is the single Kant passage whose logic the rest of the appendix keeps returning to.

### I.2: Fichte and Schelling — Orientation Level

Read this largely *through Hegel's own early polemics* rather than through much primary Fichte/Schelling. The key issues: Fichte's self-positing *Ich* and the problem of getting from the self-positing subject to objective knowledge; Schelling's Absolute as the point of indifference. Hegel's critique (in the *Differenzschrift* and the *Phänomenologie* Vorrede): Fichte gets stuck in an infinite subject–object oscillation; Schelling's Absolute is "the night in which all cows are black."

### I.3: Greek Background

Helpful, not blocking — with one exception. Aristotle is **elevated toward near-essential for this reader specifically**, as the cheapest hedge against the economist's-framing trap flagged throughout the syllabus: Hegel's *Wirklichkeit* is Aristotelian *energeia* reworked, and the practical-reason-vs-decision-theory wedge that runs through the whole Frankfurt tradition is Aristotelian.
