# How to Read Sozialphilosophie: Active Practices for Philosophical Self-Study

**Context:** This document accompanies the *Sozialphilosophie and Adjacent Fields: Integrated Self-Study Syllabus*. It describes the specific practices that turn reading into philosophical competence. The practices are ordered by importance; the first two are non-negotiable, the rest are strongly recommended.

**Why a separate document:** The mode of engagement that produces philosophical competence is fundamentally different from the mode that produces competence in quantitative social science. In economics, you learn by doing problem sets, replicating empirical exercises, and building models — the text is a vehicle for technique. In philosophy, the text *is* the technique: the argument's structure, its moves, its presuppositions, and its vulnerabilities are the object of study. "Reading" a philosophical text without an active practice around it is like reading a textbook on econometrics without ever running a regression — you'll recognise vocabulary but you won't be able to do anything with it.

---

## Reading protocols by text type

The practices below apply to all texts, but the *reading protocol* — how you approach the text before writing — differs by text type. The syllabus covers texts that range from clearly argued systematic prose to deliberately anti-systematic constellations, and no single reading method works for all of them.

### Protocol 1: Systematic argumentative texts

**Applies to:** Honneth, Jaeggi, Forst, Celikates, Fricker, Fraser, Dewey, most [ES] and [RS] secondary literature, most journal articles over ~20 pages.

**Method:** One careful read without paragraph-by-paragraph notes, followed by writing the Practice A reconstruction from memory, then targeted re-consultation. Read the chapter slowly enough to follow the argument but without stopping to annotate every paragraph. When you finish, close the book and write the reconstruction from what you retained. You will find that you can reconstruct the main claim and what it's directed against, but that the support structure (the specific argumentative moves, the mode of inference) has gaps. Now return to the text — not to re-read linearly but to look up the specific passages where your reconstruction has holes. This targeted return is far more efficient than a full second pass because you know exactly what you're looking for.

**Why not take notes during the first read?** If you annotate paragraph-by-paragraph on the first pass, you tend to produce a *summary* (a compression of what the author said, tracking the text's order) rather than a *reconstruction* (an identification of the argument's structure, which may not follow the text's order). The shift from summary to reconstruction is one of the core skills you're building. It is easier to make this shift if your first encounter with the text is holistic — following the argument from start to finish — rather than atomised into paragraph-level notes. The reconstruction answers the question "What is this author trying to establish, how, and against whom?" — a question that is easier to answer after seeing the whole.

**Time calibration:** The syllabus hour estimates for these texts assume this protocol. A text estimated at 35–45 hours means roughly 75–80% reading time and 20–25% reconstruction writing and re-consultation.

The cognitive science supporting this approach — retrieval practice produces stronger retention and deeper understanding than re-reading, even when re-reading feels more fluent — is well established. See Henry Roediger & Jeffrey Karpicke, "Test-Enhanced Learning: Taking Memory Tests Improves Long-Term Retention" (*Psychological Science* 17(3), 2006).

### Protocol 2: Dense, allusive, or anti-systematic texts

**Applies to:** Hegel (*Rechtsphilosophie*, *Phänomenologie*), Adorno (*Negative Dialektik*, *Minima Moralia*), Lukács (*Geschichte und Klassenbewußtsein*), Benjamin, parts of Habermas's TKH (especially Bd. 2, ch. VI).

**Method:** Two passes with different purposes. First pass: read for the *shape* of the argument — what is this chapter about, where does it start, where does it end up, what are the major transitions? Do not try to understand every sentence. Mark passages that seem crucial and passages that are opaque. Second pass (which can be partial — focus on the marked passages and their surroundings): read for the *detail* of the argument, with the relevant [ES] secondary text (Neuhouser, Freyenhagen, McCarthy) open alongside. Then write the Practice A reconstruction.

The [ES] secondary is meant to be read *before* the primary precisely so that your first pass through the primary is already informed — you are not going in blind but checking your understanding of Neuhouser against what Hegel actually says.

**Time calibration:** The syllabus hour estimates for these texts already build in the two-pass structure. Hegel's *Rechtsphilosophie* at 40–60 hours is roughly 25–35 hours of first-pass reading, 10–15 hours of targeted second-pass work, and 5–10 hours of reconstruction writing. Do not be discouraged if your first pass through Adorno or Hegel produces less retained structure than your first pass through Honneth — this is a property of the texts, not a failure of your reading.

> [!note] Claude — Use 2: Targeted clarification
> When you hit a passage you cannot parse after two careful readings — common with Hegel, Adorno, and parts of Habermas — bring the specific passage to Claude *with your best attempt at interpretation* and ask: *"Here is how I'm reading this passage. Am I on the right track, or is Hegel/Adorno doing something I'm not seeing?"* This is different from asking "What does this passage mean?" — the latter outsources the work; the former uses Claude to calibrate your existing interpretation. Always include your attempt. If you can't produce any interpretation at all, that itself is diagnostic — usually you're missing a piece of context (a concept from an earlier section, a position being implicitly targeted) rather than the passage being simply hard. Distinguished from Use 5 (language assistance) below: Use 2 is for philosophical opacity after grammar is parsed; Use 5 is for grammatical opacity.

### Protocol 3: Short programmatic texts

**Applies to:** Horkheimer's "Traditionelle und kritische Theorie," Benjamin's Geschichtsphilosophische Thesen, Honneth's "Pathologien des Sozialen," Forst's "Noumenal Power," Fraser's "Rethinking the Public Sphere," most journal articles under ~30 pages.

**Method:** One very careful read with light marginal annotation, then reconstruction. The text is short enough (15–40 pages) that a full careful read takes only 2–3 hours, and the density means a fast first pass would miss too much. Annotate lightly in the margins: mark the main claim, the key transitions, anything that surprises you. Then write the reconstruction. For these short texts, you typically do not need a separate re-reading step — the reconstruction writing itself will send you back to specific passages, and the text is short enough that locating them is fast.

**Time calibration:** The syllabus estimates 5–12 hours for these texts, which includes the careful read plus reconstruction.

> [!note] Claude — Use 5: Language assistance
> When you encounter a syntactically tortured German sentence — routine in Hegel, Adorno, and parts of Habermas — it is legitimate to ask Claude to unpack the sentence *linguistically* without asking for the philosophical interpretation. *"Can you parse the grammatical structure of this sentence and identify what the main clause is, what the subordinate clauses modify, and what the referents of the pronouns are?"* This is language assistance, not philosophical outsourcing. Adorno in particular writes sentences where the difficulty is partly syntactic (nested subordinate clauses, displaced predicates, ambiguous pronoun reference) and partly philosophical — separating the two lets you focus your cognitive effort on the philosophical difficulty rather than fighting grammar simultaneously. Always attempt the sentence yourself first. If you can parse the grammar but not the argument, that's a philosophical difficulty (Use 2 above), not Use 5.

# Part I — Reading protocols and receptive practices

The practices in this part build the receptive skill: turning the encounter with a text into reliable understanding of what the author argued, what concepts are at work, where the argument sits in the debate landscape, and what survives translation back into the original language. These practices are the foundation. The productive practices in Part II depend on them — Part II's exercises assume that you can already reconstruct an argument, track a concept across thinkers, and locate a position in a debate.

---

## Practice A: Written argument reconstruction (Full and Light tiers; Orientation tier reads without a filed note)

After finishing a chapter or section, close the book and write — in your own words, not paraphrase — a reconstruction of the argument. The Full-tier format has four components (Light tier uses parts (i) and (iii) only — see *When and at what depth* below):

**(i) What is the claim?** State the thesis in one or two sentences. Be as precise as possible. "Honneth argues that self-realisation depends on three forms of recognition" is too loose. "Honneth argues that the formation of an undamaged self-relation (*intaktes Selbstverhältnis*) requires three structurally distinct forms of intersubjective recognition — love (generating self-confidence), legal respect (generating self-respect), and social esteem (generating self-esteem) — and that the denial of any of these constitutes a form of *Missachtung* that motivates social struggle" is the right grain. *Demarcation cue:* if you can substitute the author's name with another's and the sentence still works, you have not stated the claim precisely enough.

**(ii) What supports it?** Identify the premises, the evidence, and the mode of inference. Is this a transcendental argument (X is a necessary condition for Y)? An empirical generalisation? A conceptual analysis? A normative reconstruction? A genealogical derivation? Philosophical arguments often combine these without flagging the transitions, and identifying the mode of inference is where you catch weaknesses. For instance, when Jaeggi argues that forms of life can be rationally evaluated by whether they solve the problems they generate, the argument moves between a conceptual claim (forms of life *are* problem-solving ensembles), an empirical presupposition (we can identify when problems are being solved or not), and a normative claim (failure to solve problems constitutes a rationally assessable deficiency). These are distinct kinds of support and can fail independently. *Demarcation cue:* support is what the author *gives* you; presupposition (part iv) is what they *do not* and *need*.

**(iii) What is it directed against?** Every claim in the Frankfurt tradition is positioned against alternative views. If you can't identify what the author is rejecting, you haven't understood the claim. For example, Jaeggi's claim that forms of life are rationally evaluable is directed against liberal neutrality (Rawls: forms of life are beyond the scope of justice) *and* against relativism (forms of life are incommensurable) *and* against moral perfectionism (there is one correct way to live). These are three different opponents, and the argument works differently against each. *Demarcation cue:* name at least one opponent. If none, the claim is either uncontroversial or you have not found the right framing.

**(iv) What does it presuppose?** What must you already accept for the argument to go through? These presuppositions are often the most productive points for critical engagement — and for original contribution. For example, Honneth's recognition theory presupposes that humans have a quasi-anthropological need for recognition, which Jaeggi explicitly refuses in her alienation book. Jaeggi's problem-solving model presupposes that we can distinguish between genuine solutions and mere displacements, which Amy Allen's critique of progressive philosophy of history calls into question. *Demarcation cue:* a presupposition is not a missing premise the author forgot — it is a commitment they would *have to* accept if asked, but never explicitly state. (This is the part most easily conflated with (ii) by a reader trained on formal arguments; resist that conflation.)

**Target length.** *Full tier:* 500–800 words per chapter or major section. *Light tier:* 200–300 words per text (not per chapter). 30–45 minutes per Full reconstruction on top of reading time, 10–15 minutes per Light. The Full reconstruction is the philosophical analogue of replicating an econometric exercise — you discover what you actually understand versus what you merely followed along with. Roughly 30% of what you thought you understood dissolves when you try to reconstruct it; the point is identifying the gaps *during study* rather than discovering them in use. See *When and at what depth* below for tier routing.

**Which reading protocol to use.** See "Reading protocols by text type" above. The protocol differs depending on whether the text is systematic-argumentative (Protocol 1), dense/anti-systematic (Protocol 2), or a short programmatic piece (Protocol 3). The worked example below (Honneth's "Pathologien des Sozialen") follows Protocol 3. After your first Full-tier reconstruction (W5 Honneth "Pathologien" per the execution plan), bring it to Claude for a Use 1 check (see the `> [!note] Claude — Use 1` callout below in this practice). The Use 1 check is part of Practice A's first-instance protocol, not a separate practice.

### When and at what depth

Reconstruction depth is tiered. The syllabus marks every text with a tag ([P\*], [P], [ES\*], [ES], [RS], [O\*], [O], [NE]); the tag routes to a reconstruction tier by the default rule below. The execution plan's per-text Tier column carries the actual assignment, which is the default unless the plan explicitly flags a deviation.

| Syllabus tag | Default tier | Filed output |
|---|---|---|
| [P\*], [ES\*] | **Full** | One reconstruction note per chapter or major section. 500–800 words. Full four-part structure (claim / support / directed-against / presuppositions) + forward-looking closing sentence + `## Empirical presuppositions` sub-heading + 2–3 C1 seminar questions filed at the bottom of the same note. |
| [P], [ES] | **Light** | One reconstruction note per text (not per chapter). 200–300 words. Two parts only: claim + directed-against. No empirical-presuppositions sub-heading required (add inline if salient); no C1 questions required (file if a tension surfaces). Closing sentence optional. |
| [O\*], [O], [RS] | **Orientation** | No filed reconstruction note. A 1–2 sentence entry in the reading log (date, text, "main point I want to retain") is sufficient — provenance without artifact. |
| [NE] | — | Out of scope. If a [NE] item is read, treat as Orientation. |

**Definitions.**

- *Chapter or major section.* A "major section" is a Roman-numeral or §-numbered division that the author themselves treats as a self-contained argumentative unit (e.g., RP §§142–181 *Familie*, §§182–256 *bürgerliche Gesellschaft*; TKH Bd. 2 ch. VI; KuA chs. 1, 2, 3). When in doubt, one note per chapter; subdivide further only if a chapter exceeds ~80 pages.
- *Per text vs. per chapter.* Full tier is per chapter / major section. Light tier is per text. A Light-tier text running over multiple weeks generates one filed note at the end, not running notes folded into a later artifact.

**The execution plan is canonical for tier assignment.** The default rule above is the routing; the execution plan's Inventory tables (Tier column) carry the actual per-text tier. Where the plan deviates from the default (e.g., a [P] upgraded to Full because the text is structurally load-bearing for Module 7 — see Phänomenologie IV.A 1.3 — or a [P*] downgraded to Light for capacity reasons), the plan's assignment governs, and the deviation must appear explicitly in the Inventory column. Silent deviation breaks the practices ↔ bridge ↔ plan triangle.

**Every text whose tier specifies a filed note appears as an explicit row in the schedule's Output column.** No silent folding into module-close artifacts. If a text was read but no Output appears in the schedule, it is Orientation tier by default; if it should have been Light or Full, the schedule is wrong and the Inventory tier is the authority for the correction.

*Time-budget consequences of the tier choice (per-author writing-time multipliers, peak-week guidance, productive-practice budgeting) live in the [Time allocation rule of thumb](#time-allocation-rule-of-thumb) section below.*

**One closing sentence — the forward-looking move (Full tier required; optional at Light).** After completing the four-part reconstruction, add a single sentence in the form: *"If I were making this argument, the move I would change is X."* Be specific — name a premise, a method, an example, or a directed-against opponent. This is not yet an evaluation (Practice H) and does not require defence. The point is to plant a small seed of disagreement that you'll either nurture into a productive intervention later (Practice I Anwendung, Practice J Skizze) or discard as ill-considered once you read further. Across 60+ texts these sentences compound into a record of *your own* judgments, distinct from the reconstructive voice. Without this closing move, the four-part reconstruction is purely backward-looking — it asks only what the author does, never what *you* would do differently. At Light tier, file a closing sentence only where the text actually provokes one; do not force generic dissent.

> [!note] Claude — Use 1: Reconstruction check
> After you've written your Full or Light reconstruction, bring both the text and your reconstruction to Claude with a prompt like: *"Here is my reconstruction of the argument in chapter X. What have I gotten wrong, what have I missed, and where have I stated something ambiguously?"* This is the highest-value use because *you've already done the hard cognitive work*; Claude functions as a knowledgeable interlocutor who checks your output, not as a source of input. Claude can identify structural misreadings (wrong-step attribution, premise/conclusion conflation, missed qualifications) and gaps. Run the check after the first Full reconstruction (W5 Honneth "Pathologien") and after first-of-author Full reconstructions thereafter; ad hoc beyond that. See [Using Claude as an interlocutor — general principles](#using-claude-as-an-interlocutor--general-principles) for the governing sequencing rule.

### Worked example: Practice A applied to Honneth, "Pathologien des Sozialen" (1994)

This is the first [P*] text on the accelerated path and the first text for which you will write a full reconstruction. Use it as a template for calibrating grain and structure.

**(i) Claim:** Honneth argues that Sozialphilosophie is a distinct intellectual enterprise — irreducible to political philosophy, moral philosophy, or empirical sociology — defined by a specific diagnostic orientation: identifying *pathologies* of social life, understood as developmental disturbances (*Fehlentwicklungen*) in the process by which a society realises its own rational potential. The critical theorist's task is not to apply an external moral standard to social arrangements (as in Rawlsian political philosophy) but to identify the norms already implicit in social practices and show where those practices systematically violate their own standards. The concept of *social pathology* — not injustice, not inequality, but pathology — is the field's central diagnostic category.

**(ii) Support:** Honneth proceeds genealogically, tracing the concept of Sozialphilosophie through Rousseau, Hegel, and Marx to show that it has always been oriented toward diagnosing pathological developments rather than constructing ideal theories. The mode of inference is normative reconstruction: Honneth does not argue that social pathology is a useful *concept to introduce* but that it is the *concept the tradition has always been using*, whether or not it was explicit about this. The evidence is primarily textual-interpretive — readings of Rousseau's *Discours*, Hegel's *Rechtsphilosophie*, and Marx's early writings — combined with the claim that this reconstructed tradition is normatively superior to alternatives (liberal political philosophy, positivist sociology) because it can capture phenomena — damaged subjectivity, distorted self-relations, systematically blocked social learning — that those alternatives structurally exclude.

**(iii) Directed against:** Three targets. First, against Rawlsian political philosophy, which begins from ideal principles of justice rather than from diagnoses of existing pathology — Honneth's claim is that this gets the order of explanation wrong (you must first identify what is going wrong before you can articulate what a just arrangement would look like). Second, against purely empirical sociology, which can describe social arrangements but cannot normatively evaluate them as *pathological* without importing evaluative criteria from elsewhere. Third, implicitly against Habermas's specific version of critical theory, which locates social pathology exclusively in the "colonisation of the Lebenswelt" — Honneth is preparing the ground for a broader, recognition-based account.

**(iv) Presuppositions:** The argument presupposes (a) that societies have a "rational potential" against which their actual development can be measured — a quasi-Hegelian assumption that Honneth does not fully defend in this essay; (b) that the concept of pathology is applicable to social arrangements, not only to organisms or individuals — what makes a social arrangement "pathological" rather than merely unjust or dysfunctional remains underspecified here (this is what Zurn's "second-order disorders" paper, Module 8.3, later clarifies); (c) that the genealogical reconstruction Honneth offers is not merely one possible reading of the tradition but captures what the tradition is *really about* — a strong claim that depends on the method of normative reconstruction being defensible, which Honneth only fully articulates in *Das Recht der Freiheit* (Module 7.5).

## Practice B: Conceptual tracking document

Create a single running document — a spreadsheet or a structured markdown file works well — with an entry for each major concept you encounter. The columns:

- **Term** (German and English): e.g., *Verdinglichung* / reification
- **Originator**: who introduces the concept in the form the tradition uses it (Lukács, *Geschichte und Klassenbewußtsein*, 1923)
- **Definition in context**: what the term means for its originator, stated precisely
- **Transformations**: how subsequent thinkers modify it. For example, for *Verdinglichung*: Lukács 1923 (structural: the commodity form pervades all consciousness) → Adorno/Horkheimer 1944 (generalised: instrumental reason as such, not only commodity relations, produces reification) → Honneth 2005 (psychologised: forgetting the primacy of recognition) → Jaeggi (critique of Honnett: reification is a structural, not psychological, pathology — Honneth confuses a social-ontological category with a psychological deficit)
- **Live disputes**: what is currently contested about this concept. For *Verdinglichung*: Is it an empirically identifiable condition or a theoretical construct? Does it require a contrast case (non-reified social relations)? Can it be rehabilitated without a philosophy of history?

This document becomes increasingly valuable as you progress through the syllabus. Many debates in Sozialphilosophie turn on the same concept being used differently by different authors — *Anerkennung*, *Entfremdung*, *Kritik*, *Lebensform*, *Pathologie*, *Sittlichkeit*, *Lebenswelt* — and if you don't track the shifts explicitly, you'll conflate positions that are actually in tension. By the time you reach the systematic core (Part II), you should have 20–30 entries. By the time you finish Part III, perhaps 50–60.

**Practical tip:** Maintain a separate "disputed definitions" sub-section for terms where you encounter genuinely incompatible uses. For instance, *Pathologie* is used in at least four distinct ways (see Laitinen & Särkelä 2019, Module 8.4); documenting this multiplicity is itself a form of philosophical understanding.

### Worked example: Practice B applied to *immanente Kritik* (immanent critique)

- **Term:** *Immanente Kritik* / immanent critique
- **Originator (in the form the tradition uses it):** Hegel, developed through Marx; the Frankfurt tradition's appropriation runs through Adorno and is most systematically theorised by Rahel Jaeggi and Titus Stahl.
- **Definition in context:** Critique that evaluates a social practice, institution, or form of life *by its own standards* — by the norms that the practice itself implicitly claims to realise — rather than by an external moral principle imported from outside. The critic does not bring an independent normative standpoint; instead, the critic shows that the practice *contradicts itself*, failing to live up to what it purports to be.
- **Transformations:** Hegel (the dialectical self-critique of categories that fail to capture their own content) → Marx (bourgeois society claims freedom and equality but structurally produces exploitation — critique proceeds by measuring bourgeois society against its own norms) → Adorno (immanent critique retains its force but can never achieve positive resolution — the critic shows that the norms are violated but cannot construct an alternative; see *Negative Dialektik* Introduction) → Habermas (partially: validity claims implicit in communication provide the immanent normative standard — but Habermas also introduces quasi-transcendental elements that go beyond pure immanence) → Jaeggi (*Kritik von Lebensformen*, 2014: immanent critique is reconstructed as *problem-solving assessment* — a form of life is criticised not for violating an external norm but for failing to solve the problems it generates; this is immanent because the problems are *the form of life's own problems*) → Stahl (*Immanente Kritik*, 2013: the most systematic account of immanent critique as a *method* — what it means for critique to be "immanent" to its object, how it differs from both external moral critique and ideology critique).
- **Live disputes:** (1) Is immanent critique genuinely distinct from external critique, or does the identification of a practice's "own norms" always presuppose an external standpoint? (Amy Allen's challenge: the judgment that a form of life has "failed" seems to require a developmental narrative — progress — that is itself external.) (2) Can immanent critique generate *transformative* conclusions, or can it only identify failures within existing practices without pointing beyond them? (Freyenhagen's Adornian position: critique can identify what is wrong without specifying what would be right.) (3) How does the critic identify the "implicit norms" of a practice — by philosophical reconstruction (Honneth, Jaeggi) or by empirical investigation of participants' own normative commitments (Celikates's "lay normativity")?

**Write-then-compare protocol.** The worked example above traces a *received* trajectory (Hegel → Marx → Adorno → Habermas → Jaeggi → Stahl). If you read it before writing your own first entry on *immanente Kritik*, you will unconsciously reproduce its shape. Reverse the order: when you encounter a concept that warrants a Practice B entry, write your version *first* from the reconstructions you've done so far. Then compare against the worked example (or against a Claude-generated trajectory, treated with the cautions in [Discipline — what not to do](#discipline--what-not-to-do) and the Use callouts). The divergences are evidence — either of error you should correct, or of genuine noticing that the received trajectory misses. The same applies to Practice C seminar questions: write yours first, then compare.

## Practice C: Seminar questions (every major text)

Practice C has two complementary sub-practices: C1 internal-tension questions written per text, and C2 cross-thinker or translation questions written at module boundaries. Both train the capacity to identify *where the action is* — distinct from comprehension (do I understand?) and from critique (do I object?).

### C1: Internal-tension questions (every major text)

For each major text, write 2–3 questions that you would ask if presenting the text in a graduate seminar. This is *not* the same as "things I didn't understand" — a seminar question identifies a genuine tension, ambiguity, or productive difficulty *within the author's own framework*. The discipline of formulating these forces you to distinguish between confusion (which is resolved by re-reading) and genuine philosophical problems (which are not).

**Format:** A short setup (2–3 sentences stating the tension) followed by the question.

**Example:** "Jaeggi argues that forms of life can be evaluated by whether they successfully process the problems they generate. But the concept of 'success' here seems to presuppose a standpoint from which success and failure can be assessed — and it's not clear that this standpoint can itself be internal to the form of life under critique. If the standpoint is external, isn't this *external* critique dressed up as immanent critique? If it's internal, how does Jaeggi avoid the circularity of evaluating a form of life by its own standards when those standards may be part of the pathology?"

**Example:** "Honneth's 'Anerkennung als Ideologie' (2004) proposes that genuine recognition must (a) not be merely rhetorical, (b) be materially realised in institutions, and (c) increase the autonomy of those recognised. But criterion (c) seems to presuppose exactly the kind of substantive normative standard (autonomy) that recognition theory was supposed to *derive* from the structure of social relations rather than import from outside. Does this make the criteria circular, or does Honneth's argument show that the concept of recognition always already contains a commitment to autonomy?"

**Relationship to H1 (post-Pass-6 audit clarification, 2026-05-19).** The H1 light-discrimination exercise (below) starts from an *external* objection you were tempted to make, then translates it into an internal one. If the translation succeeds, the resulting internal version *is* a C1 seminar question — file it under whichever folder you reach first, not both. The distinction is *direction of generation* (C1 = tension noticed *in the text*; H1 = external impulse *in the reader*, translated inward), not artifact form. Reserve C1 for tensions you notice without having been tempted to attack the author externally. Running both faithfully on the same text duplicates work.

**Cadence taper (post-Pass-10 audit addition 2026-05-19).** C1 is "every major text" indefinitely as originally specified, but by Phase II the in-author-tension move is consolidated. Recommended taper: every major text M1–M12 (Phase I + early Phase II — training); from M13 onward (mid-Phase II), file C1 questions only where the chapter genuinely surfaces a substantive tension you can name in 2–3 sentences; pro-forma C1 questions on every major text become busy-work. By Phase III, C1 is reserved for texts where the tension is load-bearing for the W3.2 piece. Cadence guidance is consistent with H1 + D taper rationale — productive moves consolidate at similar pace once trained.

### C2: Cross-thinker and translation questions (once per module)

At each module boundary, write 1–2 questions in one of two further forms. These exercise the *positioning* skill that internal-tension questions do not — the skill of holding two frameworks far enough apart to see where they actually disagree, or close enough to see what each one's vocabulary loses when forced into the other's.

**Cross-thinker form.** "Does X's framework require what Y calls Z, even when X claims it does not?" Example: *Does Jaeggi's account of forms of life require what Allen calls a 'progressive philosophy of history' even when it claims not to? If the answer is yes, what work does Jaeggi's denial do? If no, what is Allen actually objecting to?*

**Translation form.** "If we rewrite X's claim in Y's vocabulary, what is lost?" Example: *If we rewrite Honneth's three-sphere recognition theory in Forst's vocabulary of justification, what is lost — and is what is lost essential to the theory, or an artefact of its expression?*

**Why C2 matters separately.** C1 questions are about a single author. C2 questions force you to hold two authors in tension, which is the cognitive operation underwriting both the Comparison Exercise (Phase III M32–34, post-Path-D' shift from M30–32) and the W3.2 publishable piece. Without C2, the Comparison Exercise will be your first attempt at positioning thinkers against each other — at maximum stakes.

**Apply the write-then-compare protocol** (see Practice B): write your questions *before* asking Claude for objections or comparisons. If you ask Claude first, your questions will sound like Claude's questions.

## Practice D: Debate maps (once per module)

At the end of each module, draw — literally, as a diagram, mind map, or structured outline — the debate landscape: who holds what position, who is responding to whom, what the axes of disagreement are. The Frankfurt tradition is highly referential — everyone is positioned relative to everyone else — and without a spatial representation of this relational structure, you will lose track of the topology.

**Example:** At the end of Module 7, you should be able to draw the recognition debate as a space with axes like:

- Monist vs. pluralist normative foundation (Honneth: recognition is the single fundamental category vs. Fraser: recognition and redistribution are irreducible)
- Reconstructive vs. genealogical method (Honneth/Jaeggi: normative reconstruction of existing institutions vs. Allen: genealogical critique that refuses developmental narratives)
- Recognition vs. justification (Honneth: recognition is more fundamental than discourse vs. Forst: the right to justification is the basic norm)
- Genuine vs. ideological recognition (Honneth's own internal problem, addressed in "Anerkennung als Ideologie")

**Format:** Any visual format that captures positions *and* the relations between them — not just a list. The point is to make the structure of the debate visible at a glance so you can locate a new text within it immediately. Keep these maps; they accumulate into a comprehensive atlas of the field.

**Minimal template for your first debate map:** If you've never drawn a debate map, start with this structure:

1. **Identify 2 axes of disagreement** — the two most important questions on which the thinkers in this module disagree. Write these as axis labels (e.g., Axis 1: "Monist ↔ Pluralist normative foundation"; Axis 2: "Reconstructive ↔ Genealogical method").
2. **Place each thinker** as a point in the resulting 2D space. Label each point with the thinker's name and a 5–10 word summary of their position.
3. **Draw arrows** between thinkers who explicitly respond to each other. Label each arrow with the nature of the response ("critiques," "extends," "rejects premise X of," "accepts conclusion but not method of").
4. **Identify the open question** — the point in the space where no thinker is located, or where an arrow has no target. This is often the most productive spot for original work.

In Obsidian, use the Canvas feature: create a new canvas file, add cards for each thinker, and draw connections. The visual layout is what matters — seeing that Forst and Honneth disagree on Axis 1 but agree on Axis 2 (both reconstructive), while Allen disagrees with both on Axis 2 (genealogical), makes the debate topology visible in a way that a list of positions never can.

**Practical tip:** Start each module's debate map with the "why this matters" framing from the syllabus as a skeleton — it already identifies the key positions and disagreements. Then revise and complicate as you read. The syllabus says, e.g., that Honneth's recognition theory is a correction of Habermas (recognition vs. communication as the fundamental normative category) — begin with that axis, then add Fraser (redistribution vs. recognition), Forst (justification vs. recognition), and the internal tension (genuine vs. ideological recognition) as you encounter them.

**Required: name the negative space (~200 words per map).** After placing the thinkers, write a short note naming *one constructible position no current thinker occupies in this debate*, plus a sentence on why no one occupies it. Three diagnostic possibilities:

- *Incoherent:* the position can be stated but combines commitments that cannot consistently be held together. (E.g., a "genealogical normative reconstruction" — a Frankfurt-style developmental story explicitly disavowing its developmental commitments.)
- *Unmotivated:* the position is coherent but no live problem currently presses anyone toward it.
- *Historically suppressed:* the position was occupied earlier in the tradition but dropped out, often because of a generational shift or institutional pressure. (E.g., Heller-style radical needs theory in the German-language recognition debate.)

This is the most generative move in the practice. Identifying the *occupied* corners is comprehension; identifying the *unoccupied* corner is the seed of an original contribution. The 200-word note is short on purpose — it's an exercise in *naming* a position you might develop, not in developing it.

**Cadence taper (post-Pass-10 audit addition 2026-05-19).** Like H1, Practice D should taper once the move is in the toolkit. Recommended cadence: every module M1–M9 (training period — the cross-thinker positioning move is being learned); alternating modules M10 onward (consolidation); in Phase III, Practice D survives only as input to the M32–M34 Comparison Exercise rather than as per-module routine. By the time you can produce a usable debate map in ~1h without effort, the practice has done its work.

Apply this alongside the H1 taper specified in Practice H1 — both productive moves consolidate at similar pace.

> Once you've finished a module and drawn your debate map, bring it to Claude and ask: *"I've just completed Module 7. Here is my understanding of the debate landscape. What connections to other thinkers or debates am I missing? Are there positions I've mislocated?"* This is where Claude is genuinely useful in a way solitary reading cannot match — it can flag that your reading of Forst connects to a debate in Module 11A you haven't reached yet, or that an objection you've formulated against Honneth was already made by someone you'll encounter in Module 8. The use enriches the *relational* structure of your knowledge; it does not replace comprehension of individual texts. Run after the debate map is filed, not before — the discrimination move is yours to make first.

## Practice E: Synthesis notes

While Practice D (debate maps) captures the integration of thinkers *within* a single module, Practice E captures integration *across* modules. A synthesis note articulates a single cross-module connection: a concept that mutates across thinkers in incompatible ways, a question that recurs in different vocabulary, a position that one module's thinker holds and another module's thinker refuses. Synthesis is where the apparatus you are building becomes *yours* — the connections you notice that the syllabus doesn't pre-package.

**When to write one.** Ad hoc, triggered by noticing. Most commonly at: (a) module-close weeks, when you've just completed a debate map and one thinker in the current module connects to a thinker in an earlier module in a way that surprises you; (b) re-reading events (Practice F below), when a re-read with new apparatus surfaces a connection invisible on first reading; (c) Anwendung or Skizze drafting (Practices I and J), when you reach for a concept-tool and discover it has two incompatible uses across the literature.

**Format.** 300–500 words. Three components:
- *(i) The connection.* Name the cross-module link in one or two precise sentences. "Honneth's three spheres of recognition (Module 7) require a substantive theory of human needs that Honneth resists giving — but Heller's *radical needs* (Phase IV Appendix C) offers exactly such a theory, and reading the two together produces a tension Honneth's framework cannot internally resolve."
- *(ii) What it implies for both sides.* What does the connection do to your reading of each side? Does it strengthen one, weaken the other, complicate both?
- *(iii) Where to take it.* What downstream work does this open up? An Anwendung topic? A W3.2 candidate? A specific Phase IV reading to flag?

**Filing.** Under `Synthesen/` in the Obsidian vault. Link to the relevant module-level reconstructions, concept entries (Practice B), and debate maps (Practice D). Tag the connection in the concept-tracking document (Practice B) under "Live disputes" if it surfaces a contestation worth tracking.

**Budget.** ~30 min – 1h per synthesis. Ad hoc cadence; expect ~3–5 per module-close week pair (mid-Phase II onwards) as the corpus grows large enough to generate cross-module connections.

**Cumulative function.** Across Phase II–III the synthesis notes accumulate into the relational structure that distinguishes "knowing each thinker" from "having an integrated apparatus." A Phase III W3.2 candidate that doesn't lean on at least 3–4 prior synthesis notes is probably under-integrated; revise.

## Practice F: Strategic re-reading

You will need to re-read certain texts. Not because you failed to understand them the first time, but because philosophical texts — especially Hegel, Adorno, and Jaeggi's methodological chapters — yield different things when you come to them with different questions. The Craft Block (Part V of the syllabus) already builds this in for method-focused re-reading. Beyond that:

- After completing Module 7 (Honneth), re-read Hegel's *Rechtsphilosophie* §§142–180 on the family and civil society. You will see things Neuhouser didn't prepare you for because they only become visible through Honneth's lens — specifically, how Hegel's argument that the family is a sphere of immediate, affective unity (§158) maps onto Honneth's "love" sphere, and where it diverges.
- After completing Module 8 (Jaeggi), re-read Dewey's *The Public and Its Problems* (Module 4A.1). The pragmatist architecture of Jaeggi's argument — inquiry as the transformation of indeterminate situations, publics as constituted by problems — will be transparent in a way it wasn't on first encounter.
- After completing Module 11B (Luhmann), re-read Habermas's *TKH* Bd. 2, ch. VI (the system/Lebenswelt distinction, Module 6.5). Habermas's insistence on the Lebenswelt as an irreducible horizon of meaning gains urgency once you've seen the Luhmannian alternative in which the Lebenswelt is simply one system's self-description.
- After completing Zurn (Module 8.3) and Laitinen & Särkelä (Module 8.4), re-read Honneth's "Pathologien des Sozialen" (Part 0, item 0.2). The programmatic essay will now appear both more ambitious and more ambiguous than it did on first reading — you'll see how much analytical work was still needed to make the concept of "social pathology" precise.

**Budget roughly 15–20% of your total reading time for re-reading.** This is not wasted time; it is the mechanism through which comprehension becomes command. A text you have read twice with different questions is worth more than three texts read once.

**Two modes of re-reading.** The four cases above are all *comprehension-upgrade* re-reads: you come back to text X with apparatus Y in hand and see things you couldn't see before. There is a second mode worth naming: *self-disagreement* re-reading. When you re-read a text whose reconstruction you wrote months ago, you sometimes find that you disagree with your past self — not because the text has changed but because you have. When this happens, write a 200-word note answering: (i) what does my earlier reconstruction get wrong, by my current lights? (ii) what specific philosophical work I have done since produces the correction? Part (ii) is the part you can later use — it isolates *what understanding moved* and is the closest thing to a record of your own intellectual development you will produce. File these notes alongside the original reconstruction (use the `date_revised` and `revision_trigger` fields from the Note-taking infrastructure section's frontmatter schema).

**Time budget.** Named module-transition re-reads each take ~5–10h (re-reading 30–150pp with new apparatus + writing a short integration note linking the re-read to the new module's concepts). Phase II–III total: ~30–50h across the four named events (post-M7, post-M8, post-M11B, post-Zurn/Laitinen-Särkelä). Ad-hoc self-disagreement re-reads triggered by a revisit to an old reconstruction: ~30 min per instance (~5–10 across Phase II–III). The execution plan should show each named re-read as an explicit Schedule row at the trigger week.

The general 15–20% comprehension re-reading is **not** budgeted here — it is subsumed into the Style × Tier matrix pacing norms in [Appendix C §C.2](#c2-style--tier-matrix-and-time-formula) (Dense DE at 5 pp/h bottom-of-range already factors in re-reading) and into the per-class writing multiplier α (which absorbs re-read of key chapters during reconstruction).

## Practice G: Translation comparison (during reading, selectively)

Since you read fluently in both German and English, you have access to a practice unavailable to monolingual readers: comparing the German original with the English translation for key passages. This is not a general recommendation to read bilingually (that would double your reading time for marginal gain) but a targeted diagnostic for specific situations.

**When to compare:** When a passage in the English translation seems unclear, awkward, or philosophically thin, check the German. The difficulty may be translational rather than philosophical. Translations of Hegel, Adorno, and Habermas routinely flatten or distort meaning. Three common patterns:

**(i) Conceptual compression.** German philosophical vocabulary often carries more semantic structure than the English translation preserves. *Geltungsanspruch* becomes "validity claim" — adequate but losing the speech-act-theoretic precision of *Anspruch* (a claim one *raises*, actively, in the context of communication). *Sittlichkeit* becomes "ethical life" — losing the contrast with *Moralität* (individual moral reflection) that Hegel's entire argument depends on. *Aufhebung* becomes "sublation" — a technical coinage that conveys nothing to someone who doesn't already know what it means. When you encounter these English terms and the argument seems to lose force, the German often restores it.

**(ii) Structural ambiguity.** Adorno in particular writes sentences where multiple readings are grammatically possible in German, and the translator was forced to choose one. The Ashton translation of *Negative Dialektik* is notoriously loose — passages that are deliberately ambiguous in Adorno's German are rendered as if they have a single clear meaning. When an Adorno passage in translation seems suspiciously straightforward, check the German; the original is likely doing something the translation obscures.

**(iii) Register loss.** Habermas's German distinguishes sharply between *Verstand* (understanding in the Kantian sense: rule-following, categorising) and *Vernunft* (reason in the Hegelian sense: self-reflective, dialectical). Both are often translated as "reason" or "rationality" in English, collapsing a distinction that does real philosophical work.

**Practical implementation:** Keep both the German and English editions accessible. Read primarily in whichever language is faster for you (probably English for most texts, German for those only available in German). When a passage matters — it contains a key claim, a crucial transition, or a definition you'll use in your conceptual-tracking document — check the other language. Note significant translation discrepancies in your Practice A reconstruction with a brief gloss: "Ashton translates X as Y; the German Z carries the additional connotation of W." This takes 5–10 minutes per chapter and occasionally saves hours of confusion.

**Time budget.** Translation comparison is absorbed into per-chapter reading time, not separately budgeted. The 5–10 minutes per chapter figure applies only when a passage actually warrants the check; most chapters don't trigger it.

# Part II — Productive practices

The practices in Part I produce understanding *of* a text or *across* a debate. The practices in this part produce something different: claims, arguments, and applications in your own voice. They depend on Part I — you cannot defend a position you don't yet understand, and you cannot apply an apparatus you haven't reconstructed — but they exercise a distinct skill that pure receptive practice does not build. The productive practices are graded by escalating commitment: Practice H evaluates *someone else's* argument (low commitment); Practice I applies a single concept-tool to a phenomenon (medium); Practice J defends *your own* positive claim against objection (high). The Phase III publishable piece (W3.2) is the natural culmination — but the muscle for it has to be built earlier, on smaller stakes.

---

## Practice H: Critical evaluations

Critical evaluation has two scales: a **light discrimination exercise** practised once per module from Module 1, and the **full structured evaluation** (four components) practised from Module 6 onward at ~1 in 3 major texts. Both are productive in the sense that they require positioning yourself relative to an argument; the difference is the cost and the apparatus required.

### H1: Light discrimination exercise (every module, from Module 1)

At each module close (alongside the comprehension check), write one note of 150–250 words: **identify the external objection you were tempted to make against an author in this module, then translate it into an internal one — or explain why translation fails.** Time: ~30–45 minutes.

The skill being trained is the *discrimination* between external and internal critique. External critique attacks an author for not doing something they were never trying to do (the classic move: "Hegel doesn't provide falsifiable hypotheses"). Internal critique attacks the author on the author's own terms. The Frankfurt tradition is structurally internal-critical — immanent critique, normative reconstruction, ideology critique all assume the critic engages a position by the position's own standards. Without explicit discrimination training, the temptation to make external objections (especially from an economist's training) goes unchecked.

Why a note rather than just the discrimination itself: writing forces the discrimination to be concrete. "I was tempted to object that Hegel's account of civil society isn't testable" is the external objection; "translating this into Hegel's framework would require asking whether his identification of the *Pöbel* picks out a structural feature of *bürgerliche Gesellschaft* or contingent failure of administration — and that *is* answerable on Hegel's own terms" is the translation move. Some external objections will resist translation, and naming why is itself diagnostic.

These notes are short on purpose. They are not full evaluations; they are quick discriminations that train the muscle. Filed at module close, they later feed into full H2 evaluations from Module 6 onward.

**Cadence taper (post-Pass-6 audit recommendation, 2026-05-19).** H1 is the practice most clearly tapering by skill acquisition rather than content coverage — the external→internal translation move either consolidates by ~Module 7 or it doesn't. Recommended cadence:

- **Every module M1–M6 (training period)** — the discrimination move is being learned. File one H1 note per module close, no exceptions.
- **Alternating modules M7–M14 (consolidation)** — the move is in the toolkit. File a H1 note only at modules where you noticed a substantive external impulse during reading; skip modules where translation was effortless.
- **Phase II onward (ad hoc)** — deploy when an external impulse is strong, not on schedule. By the time you can write a H1 note in 15 minutes without effort, the practice has done its work as a training device; further instances should be motivated by the live external impulse, not by the calendar.

This taper preserves the training value where it matters (early modules, where economist-instincts most strongly produce external objections) while preventing the late-programme upkeep of writing 25+ near-identical translation notes by Phase III.

### H2: Full structured evaluation (~1 in 3 major texts, from Module 6 onward)

After the reconstruction (Practice A), write a separate 300–500 word evaluation. Not "I agree/disagree" but a structured assessment with four components.

**Gate:** Do not attempt the full Practice H2 until you have completed at least Module 6 (Habermas) or, on the accelerated path, until you have read Honneth's *Kampf um Anerkennung* (accelerated-path item #8). You need enough command of the debate landscape to steelman the author's position (component iii below), and that requires having at least two or three major competing positions in hand. The H1 discrimination exercise from Module 1 onward, plus the empirical-presuppositions component (ii) practised inside Practice A reconstructions, together build the apparatus.

**⚠ Claude Use 4 hallucination caveat applies to component (iii) below.** Component (iii) explicitly invokes the steelman move ("Steelman the argument against your own objection") that Claude Use 4 warns may be a "plausible fiction" — Claude can generate fluent defences with no anchor in the author's text. When using Claude for H2(iii), require citation of specific passages or specific secondary authors; treat any uncited defence as Claude's invention. See the `> [!note] Claude — Use 4: Sparring partner` callout at the end of this practice below.

**Exception — component (ii) from the start:** Component (ii) below — "What empirical claim is being presupposed, and is it defensible?" — can and should be practised from the very first module. Your economics training makes this immediately productive and does not require the debate-landscape command that components (i), (iii), and (iv) need. When reading Hegel on civil society and the *Pöbel* (Module 1.2), or Marx on the commodity form and wage labour (Module 2.2), you can already identify and evaluate the empirical presuppositions. Keep a running note of these evaluations in your Practice A reconstructions under a subheading "Empirical presuppositions" — they will feed directly into full H2 evaluations once you reach Module 6.

**(i) Where is the weakest premise?** Every argument has one. Identify it and explain why it's vulnerable. Be specific: "Honneth's claim that love constitutes a distinct sphere of recognition presupposes that intimate relations can be theorised independently of market relations and legal structures — but the sociology of the family (e.g., Illouz, Module 15.7) suggests that intimate life is increasingly structured by market logics, which would collapse the three-sphere architecture."

**(ii) What empirical claim is being presupposed, and is it defensible?** This is where your economics training gives you a genuine advantage. When Honneth reconstructs "the market" as a sphere of social freedom in *Das Recht der Freiheit*, he presupposes certain things about how markets actually function — things you are better positioned to evaluate than most philosophy students. When Streeck claims that capitalism's crises are managed through time-buying mechanisms (inflation, public debt, private debt, central bank intervention), you can assess this claim with professional-grade knowledge. When Jaeggi argues that capitalism systematically generates problems it cannot solve, the question of what counts as "solving" an economic problem is one where your training provides genuine traction.

**(iii) What would a defender say?** Steelman the argument against your own objection. If you can't construct a plausible defence, your objection probably misses the point — or attacks a position the author doesn't actually hold. This practice guards against the most common failure mode in interdisciplinary reading: criticising a philosophical argument for not doing something it was never trying to do. The H1 discrimination notes you've been writing since Module 1 are training for exactly this move.

**(iv) Where could you intervene?** Is there a question the author doesn't ask that you could ask, given your background? Is there an empirical claim you could strengthen, challenge, or reformulate with greater precision? Is there a structural analogy to something in your own field that the author misses? This is the seed of original contribution — and the natural input to Practice I (Anwendung) and Practice J (Skizze). Keep a running list of these.

> [!note] Claude — Use 4: Sparring partner
> When you've written your H2 evaluation — identifying the weakest premise and constructing your objection — bring it to Claude and ask Claude to steelman the author's position against your objection. *"Here is what I think the weakest premise in Jaeggi's problem-solving model is, and here is my objection. Can you construct the strongest possible defence of Jaeggi's position against this objection?"* This simulates the graduate-seminar dynamic of having your criticism tested, which is otherwise nearly impossible to replicate in solitary study.
>
> **Hallucination risk for Use 4.** When Claude steelmans an author's position, it is *generating* argumentative prose, not reporting on the author's actual writing. The defence Claude constructs may be a plausible fiction — fluent, internally coherent, and not anywhere in the author's text. This risk is higher than for Use 1 (reconstruction check) because Use 1 has both your reconstruction and the author's text as anchors, while Use 4 asks Claude to invent a defence. **Specific discipline:** when asking Claude to steelman, explicitly require Claude to point to either (a) a specific passage in the author's own work where this defence is made or implied, or (b) a specific secondary author whose defence this is. Treat any uncited defence as Claude's invention rather than the author's. Without this discipline, you risk training your sense of "what defenders say" on prose Claude made up.

## Practice I: Anwendung — phenomenon-to-apparatus

A short, recurring exercise from Module 2 (or later in Phase I, on second-opinion review) of taking *one phenomenon from your professional world* and applying *exactly one* concept-tool you have just acquired to it. 800–1,200 words. ~3.5–4.5 hours including iteration. Roughly every 8–10 weeks in Phase I; the cadence can adjust as you find your rhythm.

**Why this practice.** The W3.2 publishable piece (Phase III M32–35) requires that you take a phenomenon from your professional world — central-bank communication, the price-stability mandate, DSGE methodology, financial-stability discourse — and bring a Frankfurt-tradition apparatus to bear on it. This is a specific skill. It is not the same as understanding the apparatus (Practice A) or evaluating it (Practice H). It is the skill of *deploying* an apparatus on an object the apparatus was not built to address. Without practice, W3.2 will be the first time you've ever attempted this, at maximum stakes. Anwendung is the apprenticeship.

**Structure (one Anwendung).**

1. **Pick the phenomenon.** One specific thing from your ECB world — not "monetary policy" but "the ECB Governing Council's July 2024 forward-guidance phrasing" or "the price-stability mandate as it operates in the strategic review process" or "DSGE-based scenario analysis as used in the QPM." Specific is essential; generality kills traction.
2. **Pick exactly one concept-tool.** Marx's commodity-form, Lukács's *Verdinglichung*, Hegel's *Sittlichkeit*, Adorno's instrumental reason, Weber's *Wertfreiheit*, Habermas's colonisation thesis, Honneth's normative reconstruction. *Exactly one.* Combining two concepts is more advanced and dilutes the discipline — save it for Skizze (Practice I).
3. **Apply.** Write the application directly: what does the concept register about the phenomenon that ordinary economic vocabulary does not? Where does the concept *not* fit — what does it leave out or distort? The honest cases will have both.
4. **Distinguish theoretical purchase from metaphor.** This is the discipline. A metaphor uses the concept as a colourful redescription ("forward guidance is fetishistic"); theoretical purchase shows that the concept's structural features bind something specific in the phenomenon ("forward guidance has the structure Marx identifies in the commodity-form because the central bank's act of stating becomes the object the bank then responds to in the next round — the bank's communication takes on a 'value' that determines further communication, displacing the underlying economic situation"). Flag explicitly when you're confident which side you're on and when you're not.
5. **Close with two sentences.** (a) What did this concept-tool *do* that ordinary economic vocabulary cannot? (b) What did ordinary economic vocabulary *register* that this concept-tool missed? Both directions matter; the honest report has answers for each.

**Topic-tool pairings the syllabus already suggests.**
- Post-Marx commodity-form (~W22): financial-product-as-commodity; central-bank communication as a fetish-form.
- Post-Lukács *Verdinglichung* (~W31): DSGE / structural-modelling methodology as reification of social relations into model-relations.
- Post-Adorno instrumental reason (~W40): policy quantification (KPIs, dashboards) as the displacement of substantive evaluation by formal rationality.
- Post-Weber *Wertfreiheit* (~W52): the ECB's analytical practice as an instance of the Frankfurt critique that value-freedom is itself a normatively committed position.

(Final placement is pending second-opinion review — see the placement prompt at the end of the execution plan. The weeks above are indicative, not committed.)

**Iteration with Claude.** This practice has the cleanest Claude-iteration profile of any in this document. You supply professional knowledge of the phenomenon (Claude cannot reliably judge whether your description of forward guidance is accurate); Claude supplies pressure on the apparatus (does the concept actually do what you say, or are you stretching it metaphorically?). Workflow: write the Anwendung, then bring it to Claude with "*here is my Anwendung — pressure-test the apparatus side: is the concept actually binding here or am I using it metaphorically? Specifically attack steps 3 and 4 of the structure.*"

**Filing.** Anwendungen accumulate as candidate W3.2 topic-seeds. The good ones (those with genuine theoretical purchase, not metaphor) are exactly the kind of material the W3.2 introduction will draw on. File in a `field-reports/anwendung-XX.md` folder.

**Failure is informative.** Some Anwendungen will fail — the concept won't bind, the phenomenon won't yield, the application will reveal itself as metaphor. Note the failure mode explicitly: "Marx's commodity-form does not bind this phenomenon because the act of forward guidance is not a commodity in the relevant Marxian sense — it does not exchange for a general equivalent, it does not embody abstract labour." This *is* progress; it tells you something about the limits of the concept and about the phenomenon.

## Practice J: Skizze — argumentative essay defending a positive claim

Beginning in Phase II (after Honneth, ~M22), at roughly 6–8 week intervals, write a 1,000–1,500 word essay defending *one* positive philosophical claim of your own. 4.5–6 hours per Skizze including iteration. The genre is the Frankfurt tradition's own *Versuch* — an attempt, a sketch, a probe — not a finished position.

**Why Phase II, not Phase I.** Defending a positive philosophical claim requires having a competing-positions landscape — Honneth vs. Jaeggi vs. Allen vs. Forst — to position yourself within. In Phase I with only Hegel + Marx + Weber + Lukács + first-generation Frankfurt School in hand, a Skizze attempt produces wisps, not training. Post-Honneth (M22) is the natural earliest point; the apparatus is dense enough that the practice has something to work on.

**What a Skizze is not.**

- *Not a reconstruction.* You're not summarising what someone else argued. The voice is "I want to argue that…", not "Honneth argues that…".
- *Not an evaluation* (Practice H). You're not assessing someone else's argument. The object is your own claim.
- *Not a finished position.* The genre is *Versuch*. A Skizze is allowed to be tentative, to have unresolved tensions, to acknowledge moves you cannot yet make. Trying to write a finished piece at 1,500 words guarantees overreach.

**What a Skizze is.**

- *A defended positive claim.* "I want to argue that X — for these reasons, against these alternatives."
- *Frankfurt-voiced.* The argumentative apparatus is from the tradition you've been reading: immanent critique, normative reconstruction, ideology critique, recognition theory, problem-solving model. Not Anglo-analytic premise-conclusion style; not pure exegesis; not editorial commentary.
- *Small.* One claim. One central argumentative move. Two thinkers maximum. 1,000–1,500 words.

**Structure (one possible scaffold; deviate as the claim demands).**

1. **The claim.** 100–200 words. State precisely what you want to argue and why it matters. The Honneth-Pathologien-grain calibration applies: be as precise as you would demand in a Practice A reconstruction.
2. **The position you're against.** 200–300 words. Reconstruct briefly the alternative your claim is rejecting or complicating. Steelman it.
3. **The defence.** 500–800 words. Develop your claim. This is the core. Apply discrimination from Practice H1 — make sure your argument engages the alternative on its own terms.
4. **What your claim cannot yet do.** 150–250 words. Honest acknowledgement of what the *Versuch* leaves unfinished. This is not weakness; it is the genre. A Skizze that claims to have resolved everything is overreaching.

**Iteration with Claude — a specific caveat.** This is the practice where my (Claude's) hallucination risk is highest. I can pressure-test internal coherence, flag unstated premises, construct the strongest objection to your claim, and ask whether you've smuggled in commitments. What I *cannot* reliably do is tell you whether your positive claim is *novel* — i.e., whether the move you're making has already been made by a secondary author you haven't yet read. My training-data view of "who occupies what position in this debate" is patchy and confidently wrong in places. **Specific workaround:** when you ask me "has this move been made before?", treat my answer as a starting hypothesis for *you* to verify, not as ground truth. Until you have done the verification, treat my claim of "no one has made this move" as a claim of moderate confidence at best. This is the one practice where solitary study without external feedback creates a real gap; the deferral to Phase II helps because by then you'll have read enough secondary literature to do parts of the novelty-check yourself.

**Filing.** Skizzen accumulate as candidate W3.2 *spines*. The best ones — claims that survived your own pressure-testing and Claude's objections, and that you still believe months later — are the natural raw material for the publishable piece. File in a `drafts/skizze-XX.md` folder.

**Title:** Recognition's experiential thinning — why Honneth's "love" sphere needs a phenomenology Jaeggi's problem-solving model cannot supply

**1. The claim (placeholder, ~150 words).** *"Honneth's three-sphere recognition framework treats the love sphere as a self-relation grounded in pre-discursive caregiving. But the pre-discursive-ness is itself doing structural work — it grounds the developmental priority of self-confidence over self-respect and self-esteem. Jaeggi's problem-solving model, when applied to forms of life that include intimate relations (e.g., *KvL* ch. 9), assumes the structure of those relations is *legible* enough that practice-internal problems can be identified. But the legibility assumption is exactly what the pre-discursive-ness of the love sphere denies. I want to argue that the way out is not to choose but to recognise the dilemma as itself diagnostic of a missing piece — a phenomenology of the recognition's experiential thinning under late-capitalist intimate-life conditions, of the kind Renault gestures at but does not develop systematically."*

## Discipline — what not to do

**Don't highlight or underline extensively without writing.** Highlighting creates an illusion of engagement without producing the cognitive work that generates understanding. If you find yourself highlighting a passage, stop and write one sentence about *why* this passage matters — what argumentative work it's doing, what it presupposes, what it rules out. If you can't articulate this, you don't yet understand the passage, and highlighting it won't help. A book full of yellow marks is not evidence of understanding; a document of reconstructions is.

**Don't read secondary literature *instead of* primary texts for the core modules.** The syllabus already structures this correctly with the [ES] tag for genuine scaffolding cases like Neuhouser on Hegel and McCarthy on Habermas. Once past the historical foundations, reading a handbook article about Jaeggi instead of reading Jaeggi herself will produce a flattened, de-motivated understanding — you'll know the positions without feeling the force of the arguments. The difference matters: the force of an argument — why it is compelling, where it creates pressure, what it leaves unresolved — is what you need to contribute to a debate, not just follow it.

**Don't take extensive verbatim notes.** Copying passages into a notebook feels productive but is mostly mechanical. Your reconstructions (Practice A) and evaluations (Practice H) are your notes — they contain the intellectual work. The only exception: when you encounter a passage that crystallises a position in a way you want to be able to cite precisely, note the page reference and a one-sentence gloss of why it matters. A "quotation file" of 5–10 key passages per major text is useful; a notebook that reproduces half the book is not.

**Don't try to resolve every difficulty on first encounter.** Some things in Hegel, Adorno, and the *Dialektik der Aufklärung* will remain opaque after careful reading. Note the difficulty (in your reconstruction: "I don't see how step X follows from step Y"), move on, and return to it when a later text illuminates it. The Frankfurt tradition is highly self-referential: Honneth clarifies what Hegel was trying to do; Jaeggi clarifies what Dewey was trying to do; Allen clarifies what Jaeggi was trying to do. Premature resolution — forcing a text to make sense before you have the subsequent resources to understand it — is worse than tolerating temporary opacity.

### What not to use Claude for

**Don't ask Claude to summarise a text you haven't read yet.** This produces the illusion of preparation without the substance. The only exception: when the syllabus marks a text [O] (orientation) and you want to decide whether to read it at all, a brief overview of its argument and scope is legitimate — but this is a triage decision, not a substitute for reading.

**Don't ask Claude to write your seminar questions or critical evaluations.** These must come from your own encounter with the text. The point is not to have good questions; the point is to develop the capacity to generate them.

**Don't use Claude as a substitute for re-reading.** The point of re-reading is that *your* perception of the text changes — this is a first-person cognitive event that cannot be outsourced.

**Don't use Claude to generate Practice B (conceptual tracking) entries.** This is arguably the highest-risk form of outsourcing because the tracking entries *look* like factual reference material — and Claude is good at producing fluent, accurate-seeming conceptual histories. But the act of writing them is where you discover how concepts mutate across thinkers. If you ask Claude "trace the concept of *Verdinglichung* from Lukács through Adorno to Honneth," you'll get a competent summary, but you won't have done the work of noticing *for yourself* that Honneth psychologises what Lukács intended as structural — and that noticing is the philosophical insight. Generate all Practice B entries from your own reading; use Claude only to check entries you've already written (Use 1 protocol: "Here is my tracking entry for *Verdinglichung*. What have I missed or mischaracterised?").

**Be cautious about asking Claude to adjudicate between interpretations.** For many texts in this tradition — especially Hegel and Adorno — there are genuine interpretive disputes where multiple readings are defensible. You want to develop the capacity to hold competing interpretations in tension rather than prematurely closing them down. If you ask "Is Hegel a metaphysician or a social theorist?", the honest answer is that this is one of the most contested questions in Hegel scholarship, and both Neuhouser and Pippin (both on the syllabus) give different answers. Claude should flag this kind of dispute rather than resolve it, and you should be suspicious if it doesn't.

## Time allocation rule of thumb

For each hour of reading, budget approximately 20–30 minutes of active writing (reconstructions, evaluations, seminar questions). This average is calibrated to the Full tier on systematic texts; Light tier runs ~10–15 min/h of filed writing and Orientation tier ~0 min/h (the reading-log line is incidental). Debate maps and conceptual tracking entries are done at module boundaries and take 1–2 hours per module. Re-reading is built into the schedule at 15–20% of total time. In total, the active practices add roughly 40–50% to the raw reading time, and re-reading adds another 15–20%.

**Module-boundary additions on top of the per-text rate:** debate map with open-question note (~1.5–2h), Practice H1 discrimination note (~30–45 min, with Pass-6 taper from M7 onward), 1–2 Practice C2 cross-thinker questions (~30 min). Total *receptive-practice* module-boundary writing load: ~3–4h. **Plus the bridge-document module-close items: comprehension check (~2–3h per bridge §3.3) + Dialogue Moment (~1h per bridge §3.7) + QM where applicable (~2–3h every ~5 module closes per bridge §3.3).**

|---|---|---|
| Standard module close | Comp check + DM + H1 + C2 + 🗺 Practice D + negative-space note | ~5–7h |

**Peak-week guidance (post-Pass-10 audit addition).** Some weeks combine module-close load with a due Anwendung (~3.5–4.5h) or due Skizze (~4.5–6h). **Productive practices (Part II) are budgeted separately:**
- *Practice I Anwendung:* ~3.5–4.5h per instance (including Claude iteration). Phase I target: 3–4 Anwendungen (~12–18h). Phase II–Phase III: continued at 8–10 week cadence.
- *Practice J Skizze:* ~4.5–6h per instance. Begins in Phase II (~M22) at 6–8 week cadence. ~6–8 Skizzen in Phase II–Phase III before W3.2 (~30–45h).
- *Practice H1 light discrimination:* ~30–45 min per module, ~6h Phase I total (one per module).
- *Practice H2 full evaluation:* begins Module 6 (Phase II), ~2–3h per evaluation, ~1 in 3 major texts.

These productive-practice hours are not absorbed by the reading-plus-writing ratio — they are additional commitments. In Phase I the addition is modest (~18–25h on top of the ~474h reading+practice target, absorbable by the bonus-reading cuts already named in the execution plan). In Phase II–Phase III the addition accelerates as the productive practices become the centre of work.

**Variance by text type:** The 20–30 minute average obscures enormous differences between texts. Calibrate your expectations by author and style:

- **Hegel and Adorno** (extremely dense, deliberately resistant prose): budget a 1:1 ratio — one hour of writing for every hour of reading. A 30-page Hegel section may take 4–5 hours to read and 2+ hours to reconstruct. This is normal; the difficulty is partly constitutive of the philosophical content.
- **Habermas's systematic sections** (dense but clearly structured argumentation): budget 2:1 — one hour of writing for every two hours of reading. The difficulty is conceptual density, not opacity; once you grasp the architecture, reconstruction proceeds more quickly.
- **Contemporary analytic-style arguments** (Forst, Fricker, Zurn, Fraser's articles): budget 3:1 or 4:1. These authors write clearly and the argumentative structure is usually explicit. A 25-page article may take 2 hours to read and 30 minutes to reconstruct.
- **Jaeggi and Celikates** (clear but conceptually layered): budget 2:1 to 3:1. The prose is accessible but the arguments involve multiple levels (empirical, conceptual, normative) that require careful disentangling.
- **Orientation and secondary texts** (McCarthy, Outhwaite, Neuhouser, Feenberg): budget 3:1 to 4:1. These are designed to be transparent; reconstruction is mostly tracking the author's own structure.

*These per-author multipliers describe how time-per-page-read varies with text difficulty; they are independent of the Full/Light tier choice (which describes the **output**, not the **input**). A Light-tier Hegel reconstruction still incurs the 1:1 input multiplier — Hegel is Hegel — but the lighter output (200–300 words, two parts) is what reduces the all-in overhead.*

Don't panic if your first Hegel reconstruction takes 90 minutes — this is on track, not a sign that you're failing.

**Important note on syllabus hour estimates:** The syllabus's per-text hour estimates are calibrated to reading plus basic comprehension, not to the full active practice regime described here. If the syllabus says a text takes 30 hours, expect closer to 45–55 hours with systematic written reconstruction, evaluation, and re-reading. This means the core programme's ~1,230–1,640 estimated hours becomes more like ~1,900–2,500 hours in practice. A legitimate calibration decision is to reserve the full regime (Practices A–G) for the [P] and [P*] texts in Parts I–II (the historical foundations and systematic core), while adopting a lighter touch — reconstruction only, no evaluation, no re-reading — for the instrumental readings in Part III. The Part II productive practices (H, I, J) are commitments at a different scale: their hours are budgeted in the execution plan directly, not absorbed into the per-text reading-plus-writing ratio.

**Note on the learning curve:** The 20–30 minutes of writing per hour of reading is an *average across the programme*. Your first few reconstructions (Hegel, early Marx) will take 45–60 minutes each; by the time you reach Jaeggi, a year or more in, you may need only 15–20 minutes because you've developed the skill of identifying argumentative structure quickly. Don't panic if the first months feel slow — the ratio improves, and the early investment in slow, careful reconstruction pays compound returns. See also "Reading protocols by text type" for how the reading-plus-writing time breaks down differently for systematic texts (Protocol 1), dense texts requiring two passes (Protocol 2), and short programmatic texts (Protocol 3).

**On word counts for orientation:** 500–800 words (Full tier per the *When and at what depth* section above) is roughly 1–2 single-spaced pages — something you could read aloud in 3–5 minutes. 200–300 words (Light tier) is roughly half to one page, a single dense paragraph. Orientation tier produces no filed reconstruction — see [Appendix C §C.2 Style × Tier matrix](#c2-style--tier-matrix-and-time-formula). These are not essays; they are compressed, high-density notes-to-self.

## Using Claude as an interlocutor — general principles

The sequencing matters enormously. The governing principle: **after your own written work, never before it**, and with a specific protocol for each use case.

The reason is that the cognitive work of reconstructing an argument — struggling with what the claim actually is, discovering that you can't state premise three, realising you've conflated two distinct arguments — *is* the learning. If you ask Claude to explain a chapter before writing your own reconstruction, you get a fluent summary that feels like understanding but hasn't produced the neural pathways that constitute understanding. You'll nod along, think "yes, that's what I thought," and retain very little. This is the philosophical equivalent of reading someone else's solution to a problem set: it looks obvious in retrospect but you couldn't have produced it, and the capacity to produce it is what you're trying to build.

*Specific Uses 1–5 are distributed inline at the relevant practices (A, D, H) and reading-protocol sections. See `> [!note] Claude — Use N:` callouts throughout this document. What NOT to use Claude for is in [Discipline — what not to do](#discipline--what-not-to-do).*

The vault operates on two layers, and the linking practice connects them.

**Layer 1: Source-bound composite notes (Practice A reconstructions).** One file per chapter or major section, titled by source (`Honneth-KuA-ch1.md`), containing all four reconstruction components under markdown headers (`## Claim`, `## Support`, `## Directed against`, `## Presuppositions`). Seminar questions (Practice C) are embedded at the bottom of the same file. These are *Literaturnotizen* — bound to a source, tracking what an author argued.

**Layer 2: Concept-bound atomic notes (Practice B entries and synthesis notes).** These are where atomicity earns its keep. A concept entry tracks one concept across multiple sources; a synthesis note crystallises an insight spanning multiple sources, titled as a proposition. These are *permanente Notizen* — they express your own thinking.

**When you write a reconstruction and notice a conceptual connection, do two things:**

1. Create a bidirectional link to the relevant concept entry: "Honneth's concept of *[[Missachtung]]* motivates social struggle."
2. Check whether the connection involves a *claim* that recurs across multiple reconstructions. If you find yourself writing the same parenthetical gloss in two different reconstructions — "(this parallels Jaeggi's critique that Honneth psychologises structural categories)" — extract it into a synthesis note. Until that recurrence, the inline link suffices. This prevents premature proliferation of thin notes while ensuring cross-cutting insights get their own addressable location once they prove their weight.

**Link manually.** The act of linking is a form of thinking — it forces you to articulate *what the connection is*, not just that one exists. When you write a reconstruction of Honneth's *Kampf um Anerkennung* and notice that his concept of *Missachtung* connects to Fricker's hermeneutical injustice, create that link yourself: `[[Fricker-Epistemic-Injustice]]`. By month 12, the graph of links in your vault will constitute a visual map of your understanding of the field.

**Heading-level links for composite notes:** Obsidian supports linking to specific headings within a note: `[[Honneth-KuA-ch1#Presuppositions]]`. Use this when you need to reference a specific component of a reconstruction from an evaluation or debate map, but don't make it your primary linking unit — the reconstruction as a whole is the natural unit of reference.

### L.4: Navigation — index notes and Maps of Content (MOCs)

Without explicit navigation hubs, you will rely on search and memory to find notes. This works at 50 notes; it fails at 200+.

**Master index (`_index.md` at vault root):** Links to every module index, the `concepts/` folder, the `synthesis/` folder, and any active debate maps. Include a brief annotation for each module ("Module 7: Honneth's recognition theory — reconstructive method, three-sphere architecture, *Missachtung* as motor of social struggle"). Update this note at the end of each module.

**Module indices (optional, post-Pass-6 audit demotion 2026-05-19): create on demand.** Module indices (`modules/Module-07-Honneth/_index.md`) can serve as entry points when you return to a module after months away — but maintaining them pre-emptively for every module is aspirational scaffolding the user is unlikely to sustain. **Rule:** if you find yourself searching for "what was in Module X" more than twice in a single month, build a module index then. Until that trigger fires, the folder listing plus a `TABLE author, work, status FROM "modules/Module-XX"` Dataview query is sufficient. Pre-creating module indices is upkeep that buys nothing if you don't return to the module.

**Review notes (`reviews/2026-W21.md`):** A brief weekly or fortnightly note (200–300 words) capturing what you read, what connections you noticed, and what questions remain open. These provide a chronological layer complementing the topical organisation. They are the low-pressure space for emergent thoughts — "I keep noticing that every thinker who invokes *Sittlichkeit* is doing something slightly different with it" — that then trigger new concept entries or synthesis notes. Title them by ISO week (`2026-W21.md`) for easy chronological browsing.

### L.5: Note maturation and revision protocol (optional, post-Pass-6 audit demotion 2026-05-19)

Practice F (strategic re-reading) generates revisions to earlier notes. The infrastructure can support this, but the formal protocol is **optional** — it had high upkeep and low consumer in practice.

The `status` field in frontmatter (`draft` / `revised` / `mature`) and the `date_revised` / `revision_trigger` fields are useful where the revision was substantial enough that you'd want to find it later by Dataview query ("show me all notes I revisited after Module 8 because Jaeggi's problem-solving model reframed them"). **For minor edits, just edit and move on** — don't fill the maturation fields out of obligation.

The `status: revised/mature` distinction is also optional and should be applied only when you actively need to query for it. The doc's own principle holds (§L.2): *do not add fields speculatively. Unused fields create friction and pollute queries.* That principle applies to this section's own design.

**Synthesis notes as a maturation stage.** Reconstructions are *Literaturnotizen*; they mature into *permanente Notizen* (synthesis notes) when insights crystallise across sources. A reconstruction of Hegel §§142–180 written in month 3 should generate revisions after reading Honneth in month 14. But the *insight* — "Honneth's love-sphere maps onto Hegel's family but diverges at the point where Hegel insists on the family's dissolution into civil society" — belongs in a synthesis note, not buried in a revised reconstruction. The reconstruction records what Hegel argued; the synthesis note records what *you* now see.

**Version history via Git.** Use the [obsidian-git](https://github.com/denolehov/obsidian-git) plugin to automate periodic commits. You already use Git professionally, so the overhead is minimal. The payoff: you can diff your month-3 Hegel reconstruction against your month-18 revision and see concretely what changed in your understanding. This also provides a safety net — you can revise freely knowing earlier versions are recoverable.

### L.7: Tags — cross-cutting thematic vocabulary

Tags like `#social-pathology`, `#immanent-critique`, `#verdinglichung`, `#capitalism-critique` let you pull up all notes related to a theme regardless of which module or practice folder they belong to. Keep the tag vocabulary small (20–30 tags) and stable — adding tags is easy, renaming them later is painful. Prefer conceptual tags (`#Anerkennung`, `#immanent-critique`) over structural tags (`#important`, `#revisit`) — the latter are better served by the `status` field in frontmatter.

**Use tags for cross-cutting concerns that don't warrant their own concept entry.** For instance, `#empirical-presuppositions` tags every reconstruction or evaluation where you identified an empirical claim worth scrutinising — useful for pulling up all such instances when you begin Practice H evaluations systematically in Module 6. Each reconstruction note should reference the BibTeX key of the text it reconstructs (the `bibtex_key` field in frontmatter). ### L.10: Writing pipeline — from notes to output

The practices above describe note-taking, but if the project aims at eventual scholarly output (papers, contributions to debates), the vault should support the transition. Create a `drafts/` folder for paper drafts. A draft note consists primarily of links to existing reconstructions, concept entries, and synthesis notes, arranged in argumentative order, with bridging prose written around them. This mirrors the Zettelkasten workflow described in Ahrens: Luhmann's permanent notes *were* his manuscript drafts — he assembled papers by selecting and sequencing existing notes. Without this pathway, you accumulate a rich vault but face a blank page when writing.

### L.11: What about AI-assisted linking and organisation?

Distinguish three levels:

**(a) Full-text search** (use from day one): Obsidian's built-in search (Ctrl/Cmd+Shift+F) lets you find every note mentioning a term. This is basic file management, not outsourcing — use it whenever you need to check whether you've encountered a concept before or find a passage you remember writing. This is no different from using `grep` on a codebase.

**(b) AI-generated links, suggestions, and organisation** (defer for 18–24 months): The "dump everything and let the LLM organise" approach — associated with tools like NotebookLM and various Obsidian AI plugins — solves a retrieval problem, not a learning problem. For the first 18–24 months, when building understanding is the primary goal, do your own linking and structuring. The cognitive work of deciding that Lukács's *Verdinglichung* connects to Honneth's *Verdinglichung* but in a *different way* than it connects to Jaeggi's critique of Honneth is precisely the work that produces philosophical command. If an LLM generates that connection for you, it looks the same on screen but you haven't done the work.

**(c) AI-assisted retrieval and similarity search** (useful after 12+ months): After your vault contains 80+ notes, tools like Obsidian's Smart Connections plugin or periodically querying your vault contents through Claude become genuinely useful for surfacing connections you missed — not because you couldn't have made them but because the corpus exceeds what you can hold in working memory. The principle: *human-created structure, AI-assisted retrieval*. Never the reverse. The trigger for introducing this level is not a calendar date but a felt experience: when you find yourself writing a reconstruction and thinking "I know I wrote something about this concept six months ago but I can't find it," that's when retrieval assistance earns its place.

### L.12: Typical workflow after a reading session

The following is a concrete step-by-step for what happens between closing a book and moving on to the next text. Adapt it as you develop your own rhythm; the sequence matters more than the exact timing.

1. **Close the book. Open Obsidian.** Invoke the reconstruction template (Templater hotkey), fill in the metadata prompts (author, work, chapter, module, protocol, BibTeX key).
2. **Write the Practice A reconstruction from memory** (30–60 min depending on text difficulty). Fill in the four sections: Claim, Support, Directed against, Presuppositions. Write the Empirical presuppositions subsection if anything is identifiable.
3. **Targeted re-consultation.** Return to the text to fill gaps in your reconstruction. Don't re-read linearly — look up the specific passages where your reconstruction has holes. Update the reconstruction. Add Key passages (page references + one-sentence gloss). Add Translation notes if you checked the German/English.
4. **Write 2–3 seminar questions** (Practice C) at the bottom of the same file.
5. **Update or create concept entries.** Open each concept note that the reconstruction touches (or create new ones from the concept template). Add the current author's use of the concept under "Transformations." Link the concept note from your reconstruction using `[[concept-name]]`.
6. **Link to existing notes.** Scan your reconstruction for connections to other texts you've already reconstructed. Create bidirectional links where connections exist. If you notice a cross-cutting claim recurring across multiple reconstructions, consider whether it warrants a synthesis note.
7. **Update the module index.** Add a link to the new reconstruction in `modules/Module-XX/_index.md` with a one-line annotation.
8. **If this is the last text in a module:** *confirm every text read during the module has its filed output (Full reconstruction, Light note, or reading-log line) before any module-close artifacts are produced.* Then draw the debate map (Practice D), write the H1 discrimination note (Practice H1, 150–250 words), update the master index, and write a review note (`reviews/YYYY-WNN.md`) capturing the module-level picture.
9. **Optional — Claude check.** Bring your reconstruction to Claude for a Use 1 check ("What have I gotten wrong, missed, or stated ambiguously?"). Revise accordingly.
10. **Commit.** If obsidian-git isn't set to auto-commit, do a manual commit with a descriptive message ("Module 07: Honneth KuA ch1 reconstruction + Anerkennung concept entry").

**Time budget for steps 1–7:** 60–120 minutes on top of reading time, depending on text difficulty. Step 8 adds 1–2 hours at module boundaries. This matches the document's time allocation rule of thumb (20–30 minutes of writing per hour of reading, plus module-boundary work).

## Appendix A: Recommended tag vocabulary (25 tags)

Tags capture *analytical lenses and recurring problematics* — cross-cutting themes you'd plausibly want to query across multiple modules and note types. They are distinct from Practice B concept entries (which track a single concept's definition and mutations across thinkers) and from frontmatter fields like `status` or `protocol` (which track structural properties of notes). A tag earns its place if "show me everything touching this theme" would return results spanning three or more modules.

Do not create tags speculatively. Start with this vocabulary and add a new tag only when it would retroactively apply to 5+ existing notes. A tag that applies to only two notes is doing work that a direct link between those notes does better. Resist the temptation to create nested tags (`#critique/immanent`) — flat tags are easier to remember and query.

### Modes of critique and method

These tag the *type of argumentative move* a thinker makes. The method debate — what makes critique "critical," whether immanent critique is genuinely distinct from external critique, whether genealogy is a form of critique or an alternative to it — runs across the entire syllabus.

- `#immanent-critique` — Critique that evaluates a practice by its own standards. Hegel, Marx, Adorno, Jaeggi, Stahl. Central to the Frankfurt tradition's self-understanding.
- `#ideology-critique` — Critique that unmasks false consciousness or functionally necessary illusions. Marx, Adorno/Horkheimer, Jaeggi's revision. Partially overlaps with immanent critique but has distinct commitments (a contrast between true and false consciousness that immanent critique need not assume).
- `#normative-reconstruction` — The method of extracting normative content from existing institutions rather than constructing it from first principles. Honneth (explicit), Habermas (partially), Jaeggi (modified). Tag notes where the method is deployed or discussed, not merely where norms appear.
- `#genealogy` — Historical reconstruction that denaturalises present arrangements by showing their contingent origins. Foucault, Allen's critique of Honneth/Jaeggi. Tag the methodological contrast with normative reconstruction — Allen's argument that genealogy and reconstruction are incompatible is a central axis of disagreement.
- `#transcendental-argument` — Arguments that X is a necessary condition for the possibility of Y. Habermas's discourse ethics has quasi-transcendental elements; Forst's right to justification may be transcendental. Tag notes where this argumentative form appears or is contested.

### Core diagnostic categories

These tag engagements with the tradition's central critical concepts. They overlap with Practice B concept entries but serve a different function: the tag marks that a note *engages with* the diagnostic category, while the concept entry *defines and tracks* the concept. A Hegel reconstruction might be tagged `#alienation` because his account of civil society prefigures the concept, even though the concept entry for *Entfremdung* is primarily about Marx and Jaeggi.

- `#social-pathology` — The diagnostic category central to Honneth's programme: social arrangements that systematically damage the conditions of self-realisation. Tag all notes engaging with pathology as a diagnostic (including critiques of the concept, e.g. Zurn, Laitinen & Särkelä).
- `#alienation` — Estrangement from self, others, or social world. Marx, Jaeggi, Honneth (partially). Tag broadly — the concept appears in many forms across the tradition.
- `#reification` — The treatment of relations, persons, or processes as thing-like. Lukács, Adorno, Honneth's psychological reinterpretation, Jaeggi's critique of Honneth. Tag notes where the concept does work, not merely where the word appears.
- `#domination` — Power exercised without adequate justification (Forst's formulation) or the systematic constraint of autonomy. Broader than exploitation; tag notes where domination is theorised as such, not merely described.
- `#exploitation` — Specifically economic extraction; narrower than domination. Marx, some Honneth, Jaeggi on capitalism. Tag notes where the specifically economic dimension of critique is at stake.
- `#ideology` — False or distorted consciousness that is functionally necessary for the reproduction of social arrangements. Tag both the concept itself and debates about whether the concept is still usable (Jaeggi's rehabilitation vs. post-ideological positions).

### Normative foundations

These tag the different candidates for the normative ground of critical theory — the Honneth/Forst/Fraser debate is fundamentally about which of these is foundational, and whether any single category can do the work.

- `#recognition` — Intersubjective recognition as the fundamental normative category. Hegel, Honneth, the Fraser-Honneth debate. Tag notes where recognition is deployed as a normative ground, not merely mentioned.
- `#justification` — The right to justification as the basic norm. Forst's alternative to Honneth. Tag the Honneth-Forst axis specifically.
- `#freedom` — As a normative category: negative, positive, and social freedom (Honneth's *Recht der Freiheit* distinguishes all three). Tag notes where the concept of freedom is explicitly thematised, not merely invoked.
- `#autonomy` — Individual and collective self-determination. Overlaps with freedom but carries Kantian connotations (self-legislation) that freedom does not always carry. Tag notes where the specifically Kantian dimension matters.
- `#solidarity` — As a normative category (not merely an empirical phenomenon): the bonds that hold political communities together and enable collective action. Jaeggi, Celikates, some Habermas.

### Social-theoretic categories

These tag engagements with specific social-structural domains — the objects that critical theory diagnoses, not the diagnostic categories themselves.

- `#capitalism-critique` — Notes engaging with capitalism as a social form, not merely with economic mechanisms. Jaeggi's *Kritik von Lebensformen*, Streeck, Boltanski, Marx. Tag notes where capitalism is treated as a *form of life* or *social totality*, not merely as an economic system.
- `#democracy` — Democratic theory and practice as objects of critical-theoretic analysis. Habermas, Celikates, Dewey, Fraser on the public sphere. Tag notes where democratic participation, legitimation, or institutions are thematised.
- `#public-sphere` — Habermas's *Öffentlichkeit* and its critics (Fraser, Celikates). Tag notes engaging with the public sphere as a structural category.
- `#labour` — Labour as a category of social analysis and normative critique. Marx, Hegel on civil society, Jaeggi on the labour process as a form of life. Tag notes where labour is explicitly thematised.
- `#forms-of-life` — Jaeggi's category (*Lebensformen*) but also its antecedents (Wittgenstein, Hegel's *Sittlichkeit*). Tag notes where the concept of a form of life is doing analytical work.

### Meta-theoretical concerns

These tag the reflexive questions about what critical theory *is* and what it presupposes — the questions that run underneath the first-order debates about recognition, justice, and capitalism.

- `#empirical-presuppositions` — Notes where you identified an empirical claim that a philosophical argument depends on. This is the tag most directly connected to your economics training. Tag every reconstruction or evaluation where an empirical presupposition is identified (Practice H, component ii), so you can later pull up all such instances when you begin writing evaluations systematically.
- `#philosophy-of-history` — Notes engaging with the question of whether critical theory requires a developmental narrative (progress, learning processes) or can do without one. Hegel, Habermas (yes), Allen (no), Jaeggi (ambiguous). A central meta-theoretical axis.
- `#fact-value` — Notes engaging with the relationship between empirical description and normative evaluation. The Frankfurt tradition claims to overcome the fact-value distinction; whether it succeeds is a running question. Tag notes where this methodological issue surfaces.
- `#system-lifeworld` — Habermas's two-level social theory and its critics. Tag notes engaging with the system/lifeworld distinction, the colonisation thesis, or Luhmann's alternative.
- `#critical-theory-self-reflection` — Notes where critical theory reflects on its own conditions, methods, or status. Horkheimer's "Traditionelle und kritische Theorie," Celikates on the theorist's relationship to lay normativity, Allen on critical theory and the philosophy of history.

## Appendix B: Anticipated Practice B concept inventory

This appendix lists the concepts you will likely create Practice B entries for as you progress through the syllabus, ordered roughly by the module where you will first encounter each concept in its canonical Frankfurt-tradition form. The German term is given where it is canonical.

**This is a horizon, not a checklist.** Do not create these entries upfront. Create each one when you first encounter the concept in a reconstruction and need to start tracking it. Some entries below will merge in practice (you may decide that *Entfremdung* and *Verdinglichung* are better tracked in a single comparative entry); others will split (you may find that Habermas's *Geltungsanspruch* requires separate entries for truth-claims, rightness-claims, and sincerity-claims). You will also discover concepts not on this list. The inventory gives you the vocabulary of the field so you can recognise when a term warrants its own note versus when it's a minor variant of an existing entry.

### From the Hegel modules (Part I, Modules 1–1A)

- ***Sittlichkeit*** **(ethical life)** — The institutional fabric of shared norms (family, civil society, state) within which individuals achieve concrete freedom. Distinct from *Moralität* (individual moral reflection): Hegel's entire practical philosophy turns on this contrast. The concept reappears in Honneth (recognition spheres as a modernised *Sittlichkeit*), Jaeggi (democratic *Sittlichkeit*), and Celikates (critical *Sittlichkeit*).
- ***Aufhebung*** **(sublation)** — Hegel's term for the simultaneous cancellation, preservation, and elevation of a concept or form. Notoriously difficult to translate; "sublation" is a technical English coinage that conveys nothing without prior knowledge. Tracks through Marx (material *Aufhebung* of alienation), Adorno (critique of the concept — *Aufhebung* as domination), and Jaeggi (problem-solving as a non-teleological *Aufhebung*).
- ***Anerkennung*** **(recognition)** — In its Hegelian form: the mutual constitution of self-consciousness through the encounter with another self-consciousness (the master-slave dialectic, *Phänomenologie* ch. IV). This concept receives its most extensive later development in Honneth and generates more debate than any other single term in the tradition.
- ***Bürgerliche Gesellschaft*** **(civil society)** — Hegel's term for the sphere of particular interests, market exchange, and the "system of needs" (*System der Bedürfnisse*), situated between the family and the state in the structure of *Sittlichkeit*. Marx radicalises this: civil society is not a moment in the ethical totality but the real basis that generates the state as its ideological complement.
- ***Bildung*** **(formation / education / self-cultivation)** — Self-development through confrontation with otherness; the process by which an individual becomes capable of participating in *Sittlichkeit*. Not "education" in the institutional sense but the formative process of working through resistance. Relevant to Honneth's developmental account of recognition and to Celikates on the formation of critical capacities.
- ***Geist*** **(spirit / mind)** — The supra-individual rational structure that unfolds historically through human practices, institutions, and self-understanding. Whether Hegel's *Geist* is a metaphysical entity or a social-theoretic concept is one of the most contested questions in Hegel scholarship (Pippin vs. Taylor). The critical tradition inherits the concept in secularised form.
- ***Vermittlung*** **(mediation)** — The process by which apparent opposites (individual/society, particular/universal, subject/object) are shown to be internally related rather than externally juxtaposed. Central to Hegel's method and to Adorno's critique of it (Adorno insists on a *Vermittlung* that does not resolve into identity).

### From the Marx modules (Part I, Modules 2–2A)

- ***Entfremdung*** **(alienation)** — In its specifically Marxian fourfold form: alienation from the product of labour, the process of labour, species-being (*Gattungswesen*), and other humans (*Ökonomisch-philosophische Manuskripte*, 1844). Jaeggi's *Entfremdung* (2005/2014) revises this as a relational rather than essentialist concept: alienation as a "relation of relationlessness" (*Beziehung der Beziehungslosigkeit*) that does not presuppose a lost authentic human essence.
- ***Verdinglichung*** **(reification)** — In Lukács's *Geschichte und Klassenbewußtsein* (1923): the universalisation of the commodity form into a structure of consciousness — social relations appear as relations between things, and this appearance is not merely an error but a structurally generated form of experience. The concept undergoes major transformations: Adorno/Horkheimer (1944) generalise it beyond commodity relations to instrumental reason as such; Honneth (2005) psychologises it as the "forgetting of recognition" (*Vergessen der Anerkennung*); Jaeggi critiques Honneth for confusing a social-ontological category with a psychological deficit.
- ***Warenfetischismus*** **(commodity fetishism)** — Marx's concept (*Kapital* Bd. I, ch. 1.4): the products of human labour appear as having inherent value, concealing the social relations of production. Related to but not identical with *Verdinglichung* — commodity fetishism is Marx's specific analysis; reification is Lukács's generalisation.
- ***Ideologie*** **(ideology)** — In the Marx-Engels sense (*Deutsche Ideologie*, 1846): consciousness that is systematically inverted, presenting the particular interests of the ruling class as universal ("camera obscura" metaphor). The concept develops through Lukács (ideology as a necessary form of appearance of commodity society), Adorno/Horkheimer (culture industry as ideology production), Habermas (systematically distorted communication), and Jaeggi (ideology as a practice, not merely a set of beliefs — *Was ist Ideologiekritik?*, 2009).
- ***Praxis*** — Transformative, self-conscious human activity — not mere practice or behaviour, but activity that constitutes and transforms both the agent and the social world. Marx's concept, developed against Hegel's speculative resolution and against Feuerbach's contemplative materialism. Relevant throughout the tradition whenever the relationship between theory and practice is at stake.

### From the first-generation Frankfurt School (Part I, Modules 3–5)

- ***Instrumentelle Vernunft*** **(instrumental reason)** — Horkheimer and Adorno's concept (*Dialektik der Aufklärung*, 1944): reason reduced to the calculation of means for given ends, stripped of its capacity to evaluate ends themselves. The thesis: Enlightenment rationality, which promised liberation from myth, becomes a new form of domination when it reduces all reason to instrumental reason. This is the first-generation Frankfurt School's central diagnostic.
- ***Kulturindustrie*** **(culture industry)** — Adorno and Horkheimer's term for the systematic production of cultural goods as commodities, generating standardised pseudo-individuality and foreclosing genuine aesthetic experience. Not merely "mass culture" (which implies a culture produced by the masses) but culture industrially produced *for* the masses as a mechanism of social integration.
- ***Bestimmte Negation*** **(determinate negation)** — Adorno's appropriation of Hegel: critique that negates something *specific* (a particular claim, arrangement, or concept) and thereby points beyond it — as opposed to abstract negation, which merely rejects without determinate content. Central to Adorno's *Negative Dialektik*: the possibility of critique without a positive programme depends on determinate negation being possible without *Aufhebung* (resolution into a higher unity).

### From the Habermas modules (Part I–II, Modules 6–6A)

- ***Lebenswelt*** **(lifeworld)** — The horizon of taken-for-granted background assumptions, competencies, and interpretive patterns that enable communication and social action. Habermas appropriates the concept from Husserl but gives it a linguistic-pragmatic rather than phenomenological interpretation. The lifeworld is the counterpart to the "system" (functionally integrated domains governed by money and power); the colonisation thesis (*Kolonialisierung der Lebenswelt*) is Habermas's central critical diagnostic.
- ***Kommunikatives Handeln*** **(communicative action)** — Action oriented toward mutual understanding (*Verständigung*) rather than strategic success. Habermas's fundamental category, developed in *Theorie des kommunikativen Handelns* (1981). Communicative action raises three validity claims simultaneously and is the medium through which the lifeworld is reproduced.
- ***Geltungsanspruch*** **(validity claim)** — The three-fold structure of claims raised in communicative action: propositional truth (*Wahrheit*), normative rightness (*Richtigkeit*), and expressive sincerity (*Wahrhaftigkeit*). Each claim is redeemable through a different form of discourse. The concept links Habermas's social theory to his discourse ethics.
- ***Kolonialisierung der Lebenswelt*** **(colonisation of the lifeworld)** — Habermas's central critical diagnostic: systemic media (money, administrative power) penetrate domains of social life that require communicative coordination, producing pathologies — juridification (*Verrechtlichung*), monetarisation, bureaucratisation — that damage the lifeworld's capacity for cultural reproduction, social integration, and socialisation.
- ***Diskursethik*** **(discourse ethics)** — Habermas's moral theory: a norm is valid only if all those affected could accept it in a rational discourse free from coercion. The universalisation principle ("U") is the core: a norm is valid if the consequences and side effects of its general observance for the interests of each individual could be accepted by all affected. Forst later reformulates this as the "right to justification."
- ***Öffentlichkeit*** **(public sphere)** — Originally from Habermas's *Strukturwandel der Öffentlichkeit* (1962): the social space in which private persons come together as a public to engage in rational-critical debate about matters of common concern. Fraser's feminist critique ("Rethinking the Public Sphere," 1990) is the most influential revision: Habermas's model presupposes a single, bourgeois, exclusionary public and ignores subaltern counterpublics.

### From the Honneth modules (Part II, Modules 7–7A)

- ***Missachtung*** **(disrespect / denial of recognition)** — The negative counterpart to *Anerkennung* in Honneth's theory: the withholding or violation of recognition in any of its three forms. *Missachtung* damages the corresponding dimension of self-relation (self-confidence, self-respect, self-esteem) and motivates social struggle. Honneth's alternative to interest-based or class-based explanations of social conflict.
- ***Soziale Pathologie*** **(social pathology)** — A social arrangement that systematically damages the conditions under which individuals can form an undamaged self-relation. Honneth's central diagnostic category, introduced programmatically in "Pathologien des Sozialen" (1994) and developed throughout his subsequent work. The concept requires further specification: Zurn (second-order disorders), Laitinen & Särkelä (multiple typologies), and Jaeggi (structural rather than psychological reading) all contribute to clarifying what exactly a "social pathology" is and what it is not.
- ***Soziale Freiheit*** **(social freedom)** — Freedom realised *through* social institutions rather than *against* them. Honneth's concept from *Das Recht der Freiheit* (2011): negative freedom (absence of constraint) and reflexive freedom (self-determination) are incomplete; full freedom requires institutions that enable mutual recognition. This is Honneth's Hegelian correction of the liberal tradition.
- ***Normative Rekonstruktion*** **(normative reconstruction)** — Honneth's method, most explicitly articulated in *Das Recht der Freiheit*: reconstructing the rational potential already embedded in existing social institutions rather than constructing normative principles from scratch. The method claims to extract normativity from social reality without collapsing into positivism (merely describing what exists) or idealism (imposing external standards).

### From the Jaeggi modules (Part II, Modules 8–8A)

- ***Lebensform*** **(form of life)** — Jaeggi's central analytical category (*Kritik von Lebensformen*, 2014): the ensemble of social practices through which a group organises fundamental human activities (production, reproduction, collective self-understanding). Forms of life are not merely cultural patterns but problem-solving ensembles that can be rationally evaluated by whether they successfully process the problems they generate. The concept has Wittgensteinian and Hegelian antecedents but Jaeggi gives it a specifically critical-theoretic function.
- ***Krise*** **(crisis)** — In Jaeggi's sense: the point where a form of life's internal contradictions become manifest — the problems it generates exceed its capacity to process them. Distinct from mere dysfunction (a temporary malfunction within a functioning arrangement) and from external disruption (a shock imposed from outside). Crisis reveals that a form of life has *failed by its own standards*, which is what makes Jaeggi's critique immanent.

### From the Forst modules (Part II–III, Modules 10–10A)

- ***Rechtfertigung*** **(justification)** — Forst's candidate for the fundamental normative category: every person has a basic right to justification — a right to be given adequate reasons for the norms, institutions, and power relations that govern their life. Forst argues this is more fundamental than recognition (contra Honneth) because the right to justification specifies *what recognition must consist in* to count as genuine.
- ***Noumenale Macht*** **(noumenal power)** — Forst's concept: power exercised through the control of the "justification space" (*Rechtfertigungsraum*) — that is, the ability to determine which reasons count as valid, which questions can be raised, and which justifications are accepted as adequate. Power is "noumenal" because it operates through the *space of reasons*, not merely through coercion or material incentives.

### From additional modules (Part II–III)

- ***Epistemic injustice*** — Fricker's concept (*Epistemic Injustice*, 2007): injustice done to someone specifically in their capacity as a knower. Two forms: *testimonial injustice* (a speaker receives less credibility than they deserve due to prejudice) and *hermeneutical injustice* (a gap in collective interpretive resources prevents someone from making sense of a socially significant experience). Connects to Honneth's recognition theory (Fricker's injustices can be read as forms of epistemic *Missachtung*) and to the Frankfurt tradition's interest in ideology (hermeneutical injustice is a form of structurally generated misunderstanding).
- ***Demokratische Sittlichkeit*** **(democratic ethical life)** — The attempt (Celikates, Jaeggi, partially Honneth in *Das Recht der Freiheit*) to give *Sittlichkeit* a democratic rather than conservative reading. The challenge: Hegel's *Sittlichkeit* was tied to a specific institutional configuration (constitutional monarchy, patriarchal family, corporatist civil society); can the concept be retained while rejecting those specific institutional commitments?
- ***Teilnahme*** **(participation / partaking)** — Celikates's concept (*Kritik als soziale Praxis*, 2009): the capacity for critique is not a monopoly of theorists but a competence of ordinary social actors ("lay normativity"). Critical theory must reconstruct rather than replace this ordinary critical capacity. The concept challenges the traditional Frankfurt School model of the theorist as the bearer of insight that ordinary agents lack.
- ***Funktionale Differenzierung*** **(functional differentiation)** — Luhmann's concept (if you reach Module 11B): modern society is differentiated into functionally specialised subsystems (law, economy, politics, science, education, art) each operating according to its own binary code. This is the major rival framework to Habermas's system/lifeworld distinction — Luhmann denies that there is a "lifeworld" standing outside the systems, since every perspective is itself a system's self-description. The confrontation between Habermas and Luhmann (the "Habermas-Luhmann debate" of the 1970s–80s) is one of the defining theoretical disputes of post-war German social theory.

### C.2 Style × Tier matrix and time formula

The hour budget per text is computed via:

```
Reading hours = Pages ÷ pp/h(class, language)
Writing hours = min(Reading × α(class, tier), n_chapters × per-Recon ceiling)
Total Hrs    = Reading + Writing
```

Reading speed (pp/h) and writing multiplier (α) depend on the text's **prose-difficulty class**. The class is independent of the syllabus tag — a [P] text can be Dense, Layered, or Transparent depending on its prose, not its importance. Per-text class assignments live in [text_classification.md](text_classification.md).

#### Five prose classes

| Class | Description | Canonical authors |
|---|---|---|
| **Dense** | Resistant prose; re-read needed on most pages | Hegel (RP, Phän), Marx (Kapital, ÖpM), Adorno (DdA, MM, ND), Lukács, Mannheim, Horkheimer 1937, Weber methodological essays |
| **Systematic** | Dense but clearly structured; rewards consolidated reading | Habermas (TKH, EuI), Gadamer, Forst monographs (RauR), Honneth monographs (KuA, RdF, Anerkennung 2018), Khurana, Postone |
| **Layered** | Clear but conceptually layered; needs careful concept-tracking | Honneth essays, Jaeggi, Celikates, Rosa, Stahl, Joas, Renault |
| **Analytic** | Contemporary analytic; short and precise | Forst articles, Fricker, Fraser articles, Zurn, Laitinen-Särkelä, Taylor, Mead, Woodward, Hollis |
| **Transparent** | Clear prose, intended to be understood on first pass | Neuhouser, McCarthy, Outhwaite, Feenberg, Dewey, Polanyi, Streeck, Heinrich, Pippin, Wood |

#### Reading speeds (pp/h)

Three columns per language. **Bottom** = fast end of band (fluent reader, minimal re-read) — used as the default for the execution plan. **Mid** ≈ ~30% slower. **Slow** ≈ ~50% slower (first encounter, heavy re-read).

| Class | DE Bottom | DE Mid | DE Slow | EN Bottom | EN Mid | EN Slow |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Dense | 5 | 3.5 | 2.5 | 6 | 4.5 | 3.5 |
| Systematic | 8 | 5 | 4 | 10 | 7 | 5.5 |
| Layered | 10 | 7.5 | 6 | 13 | 9.5 | 7 |
| Analytic | 15 | 10.5 | 8.5 | 18 | 12.5 | 10.5 |
| Transparent | 18 | 13.5 | 11 | 20 | 16.5 | 13.5 |

#### Writing multiplier α (writing hours per hour of reading)

| Class | Full α | Light α | Orientation | Bonus/Deferred |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Dense | 1.0 | 0.4 | flat 0.25h per text | 0 |
| Systematic | 0.5 | 0.2 | flat 0.25h per text | 0 |
| Layered | 0.4 | 0.15 | flat 0.25h per text | 0 |
| Analytic | 0.3 | 0.12 | flat 0.25h per text | 0 |
| Transparent | 0.3 | 0.12 | flat 0.25h per text | 0 |

#### Per-Recon ceiling (cap)

Writing hours never exceed `n_chapters × per-Recon ceiling`:

- **Full ceiling**: 2.0h per Recon (one chapter or major section)
- **Light ceiling**: 0.75h per Recon

This cap binds when a text has few chapters relative to reading hours (e.g., Marx ÖpM = single essay over ~8h reading; without the cap, α=1.0 would imply 8h writing; with cap, writing = 1 Recon × 2h = 2h).

#### Special rules

**Honneth-monograph decouple.** Honneth monographs (*Kampf um Anerkennung*, *Recht der Freiheit*, *Anerkennung: Eine europäische Ideengeschichte* (2018), *Der arbeitende Souverän*) read at Systematic pp/h but write at Layered α. Rationale: Honneth's monographs are denser to read than essays (Systematic-paced) but the writing-load per chapter is closer to other Layered authors (Jaeggi, Celikates) than to TKH-level systematic prose.

**Aphoristic flag.** Texts flagged aphoristic (currently: Adorno *Minima Moralia* selected aphorisms, Benjamin "Theses on History") stay in Dense pp/h, but `n_chapters` = thematic clusters of ~5–8 aphorisms each, not per-aphorism. This lets the cap bind sensibly on collections that lack natural chapters.

#### What α covers

The writing-time multiplier includes: body draft (500–800w Full / 150–250w Light per chapter) + closing sentence + empirical-presuppositions sub-heading (Full only) + Konzepte updates + re-read time during writing. **C1 questions are NOT in α** — they are separately scheduled at ~30 min per Full-tier text (one set of 2–3 questions per text, filed alongside one Recon, per Practice C1 above).
