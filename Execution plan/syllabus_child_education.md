# Syllabus / Reading List: Child Education & Development (Ages 0–12)

**Two purposes, two ways to read this document.**

1. **Parent track (quick reference) — the primary use.** A curated, age-sequenced, immediately actionable reading list for raising a child, 0–12. This is the original list (Part A below), preserved. If you are reading as a parent and not as a scholar, **read only Part A and the Notes**; ignore everything marked *academic layer*.
2. **Academic layer (optional).** The developmental-science evidence base *and its controversies* (Part B), a bridge to the Sozialphilosophie programme's spine E / Appendix H (Part C), and the live research debates staged with opposing positions (Frontier section). This layer exists for if and when this material feeds an academic contribution — it is not needed to use the list as a parent.

The two layers are kept apart on purpose. The parent track is a *consensus canon* chosen for practical usefulness; the academic layer deliberately introduces the opposing camps the consensus omits.

## Relationship to the Sozialphilosophie programme (spine E)

This is the **developmental-empirical** wing of **spine E** (socialisation / Bildung / social reproduction) in the field map — the "reality-check" layer that spine E names but the main syllabus only gestures at through Appendix H. The division of labour mirrors the MPE / spine-B split:

- **Sozialphilosophie Appendix H** = formation-of-persons *as a normative-philosophical category*: Bildung (Humboldt, Heydorn), the psychoanalytic micro-foundation (Freud, Benjamin), social reproduction (Federici, Bhattacharya), and the flagship wedge — a recognition-theoretic critique of the **human-capital paradigm of childhood**.
- **This document** = the *empirical and practical* substrate: how children actually develop, what the evidence says, what to do on a Tuesday morning.

They meet at one load-bearing node: **Heckman and the toxic-stress / ACE evidence** (Part C here; the flagship's immanent pressure-point there). Heckman's early-childhood-investment programme has empirically rediscovered that early recognition relationships are decisive for formation — while theorising it in a vocabulary (investment, returns, skill-stock) that negates the *Bildung* it stumbled onto. **[→ spine E]** flags the readings here that arm that wedge. The practical parent track (Part A) is upstream of all this and stands on its own.

# PART A — PARENT TRACK (quick reference)

*The original list, preserved. Sorted by importance/relevance for a parent with a 1-year-old, in tiers: start at the top and work down. Books marked ★ are especially relevant right now (age 1–3).*

**Age cutoff rationale:** 0–7 is the developmental core — virtually all major traditions (Montessori, Piaget, Vygotsky/Elkonin, Waldorf) treat ~6–7 as a major transition from sensorimotor/play-dominant development to structured learning. A second section covers 6–12 (the "second plane" in Montessori, concrete-operational stage in Piaget), since the theoretical groundwork for school-age education looks quite different.

## Already read / currently reading
- **Remo Largo** — *Babyjahre* and *Kinderjahre* (developmental milestones, individual variation, "don't compare")
- **Herbert Renz-Polster** — *Kinder verstehen* (evolutionary developmental psychology)
- **Anton Makarenko** — *The Pedagogical Poem* / *The Road to Life* (Soviet collective education, colony for homeless youth)
- **Maria Montessori** — currently reading (presumably *The Absorbent Mind* or a secondary source)

## Tier P1 — Read these first (immediately actionable, ages 0–3)

### ★ Emmi Pikler — *Lasst mir Zeit* (Pflaum Verlag)
Hungarian pediatrician who ran the Lóczy infant home in Budapest. Documented how infants develop all motor skills autonomously if given freedom of movement and attentive (but non-interfering) caregiving. Full of developmental data, photo sequences, and timelines. The core insight — that sitting a baby up before it can get there itself creates postural problems and undermines confidence — is counterintuitive and well-evidenced. Her lighter book *Friedliche Babys — zufriedene Mütter* covers the same philosophy in a more parent-friendly format. Pikler's approach is the natural companion to Montessori for the 0–3 phase.

### ★ Jesper Juul — *Dein kompetentes Kind* (Rowohlt)
Danish family therapist, enormously influential in the German-speaking world. Core thesis: children are born socially competent and cooperative; most "misbehavior" is the child trying to maintain personal integrity within an unequal power relationship. Juul reframes the parent-child dynamic away from obedience/discipline toward mutual respect and "Gleichwürdigkeit" (equal dignity). Very practical, not academic. His later book *Nein aus Liebe* is a short companion piece on setting boundaries without coercion.

### ★ Simone Davies — *The Montessori Toddler* (Workman Publishing)
The modern, practical implementation guide for Montessori at home with 1–3 year olds. Covers prepared environment, daily routines, language development, managing tantrums, and fostering independence. If you're reading Montessori's own writings for the theory, this book is the "how to actually do it on a Tuesday morning" complement. Illustrated, well-organized, no fluff.

### John Bowlby — *Attachment and Loss*, Vol. 1: *Attachment* (Basic Books)
The foundational text on attachment theory. Bowlby synthesized ethology, psychoanalysis, and systems theory to argue that the infant-caregiver bond is a primary biological need (not derived from feeding). This is the theoretical backbone behind Pikler, Renz-Polster, and most modern developmental psychology. Volume 1 is the essential one; Volumes 2 (*Separation*) and 3 (*Loss*) extend the framework but are more clinical. Dense but rigorous — you'll appreciate the empirical grounding. *(For the empirical operationalisation, see Ainsworth in Part B; for the cross-cultural dispute, see the Frontier section.)*

## Tier P2 — Core theoretical framework (ages 0–7)

### Maria Montessori — *The Absorbent Mind* (Holt Paperbacks)
If you haven't read this specific book yet: it's Montessori's theoretical account of how children under 6 learn — unconsciously absorbing their environment in the first three years, then consciously organizing that knowledge from 3–6. The concept of "sensitive periods" (language, order, movement, sensory refinement) comes from here. More philosophical than practical, but it's the foundation everything else in Montessori builds on.

### Maria Montessori — *The Secret of Childhood* (Ballantine)
Montessori's account of how adult misunderstanding of children's needs creates most behavioral problems. Covers the child's need for order, repetition, concentration, and freedom of choice. Pairs with *The Absorbent Mind* — that book is the "what," this one is the "why it goes wrong."

### Alfie Kohn — *Unconditional Parenting* (Atria Books)
A sharp, evidence-based critique of rewards-and-punishment parenting, including its subtle forms (praise as behavioral manipulation, "good job!" as control). Kohn argues that extrinsic motivation crowds out intrinsic motivation, and that children need to feel loved unconditionally — not conditionally on behavior. Draws heavily on Deci & Ryan's self-determination theory. If you liked Renz-Polster's philosophical direction, Kohn takes it further. Can feel polemical at times, but the underlying research is solid. *(For the empirical counterweight on structure/demandingness, see Baumrind in Part B.)*

### Lev Vygotsky — *Mind in Society* (Harvard UP, 1978)
The standard English-language compilation of Vygotsky's key texts. Contains the zone of proximal development (ZPD) — the idea that what a child can do with assistance today, they can do independently tomorrow, and that good teaching targets this zone. Also covers the role of play in development and the relationship between learning and development. Vygotsky died at 37 in 1934; these are edited selections, not a single coherent book. Short but conceptually dense.

### Jean Piaget — *Play, Dreams and Imitation in Childhood* (Routledge)
Piaget's observational study of how children develop symbolic thinking through play and imitation. More accessible than his other major works and directly relevant to the 1–7 age range. His stage theory has been refined since, but the core observation — that children actively construct understanding through interaction with their environment — remains the bedrock of developmental psychology.

### Laurence Steinberg — *The Ten Basic Principles of Good Parenting* (Simon & Schuster, 2004)
**[Added 2026-06-30; the one mainstream-empirical voice in the parent track.]** The accessible, actionable embodiment of the **authoritative** model — warmth *and* structure — by the researcher who extended the authoritative-parenting evidence base into adolescence. Deliberately not romantic-developmentalist: it treats clear expectations, consistency, monitoring, and reasoned firmness as goods, not threats to the child's autonomy, while insisting they be paired with responsiveness and explanation. Read it as the everyday-parenting counterweight that keeps Part A from being single-tradition; pairs with the deeper treatment in Part B4 (Baumrind, Grolnick).

## Tier P3 — Soviet/post-Soviet tradition

### Vasily Sukhomlinsky — *My Heart I Give to Children* (Сердце отдаю детям, 1968)
Ukrainian school principal who spent 22 years at Pavlysh school developing what he called the "school of joy" — nature-based learning, emotional development, fairy tales, and moral education through lived experience rather than instruction. Saw himself as continuing Makarenko's legacy but shifted the emphasis from collective discipline to individual flourishing. Two English translations: Holly Smith (1982, Soviet-era, includes the explicitly Leninist framing) and Alan Cockerill (2016, more accessible, some political content softened). Russian and Ukrainian originals are freely available online. Primarily covers ages 5–10.

### Daniil Elkonin — *The Psychology of Play* (Психология игры)
Vygotsky's student who developed the most systematic theory of play as developmental activity. His key contribution is the periodization of child development: each age has a "leading activity" (emotional communication with caregiver → object manipulation → role play → learning activity), and transitions between stages are driven by contradictions between the child's capabilities and social demands. The English-language entry point is **Zaporozhets & Elkonin (eds.)** — *The Psychology of Preschool Children* (MIT Press), which compiles the Vygotskian school's experimental work on ages 0–7.

### Alexander Zaporozhets — concept of "amplification" (обогащение)
Zaporozhets argued against acceleration — pushing children to the next developmental stage prematurely — and for amplification: enriching the current stage so children fully develop its possibilities before moving on. This is a powerful theoretical counterargument to the "earlier is better" pressure. His work is primarily available in the Zaporozhets & Elkonin compilation above and in Russian-language collections. *(This is the live hinge against Heckman's investment logic — see the Frontier section.)*

### Lev Vygotsky — *Thought and Language* (MIT Press, revised ed. by Kozulin)
Vygotsky's masterwork on the relationship between language and thought in development. More demanding than *Mind in Society* and more relevant once your child enters the language explosion phase (roughly age 2–4). The Kozulin edition is the better translation. If you read Russian, the original *Мышление и речь* is significantly richer than any English version.

## Tier P4 — Extending to school age (6–12)

### Maria Montessori — *The Discovery of the Child* (Ballantine)
Montessori's practical account of the Casa dei Bambini method for ages 3–6, covering the sensorial materials, practical life exercises, and early literacy/numeracy. The bridge between the theoretical *Absorbent Mind* and actual classroom practice.

### Rebeca Wild — *Erziehung zum Sein* (Arbor Verlag)
German-speaking educator who founded the Pesta school in Ecuador, combining Montessori, Pikler, and Piaget into a non-directive educational model. Covers ages 0–12. Especially interesting for its account of how free-play and self-directed learning work in practice through the early school years. Well-known in the German alternative education scene.

### Célestin Freinet — *Education Through Work* (available in German as various Freinet-Pädagogik compilations)
French educator who developed a cooperative, work-based pedagogy for primary schools: class newspapers, printing presses, correspondence between schools, and democratic class meetings. Less theoretical than Montessori or Vygotsky, more focused on practical classroom democracy. Influential in German Reformpädagogik. Most relevant from age 6 onward.

### Janusz Korczak — *How to Love a Child* (Jak kochać dziecko)
Polish-Jewish educator and pediatrician who ran an orphanage in Warsaw and accompanied his children to Treblinka rather than abandon them. His writings on children's rights, dignity, and the adult's obligation to respect the child's inner world are profound and unsentimental. Korczak was a major influence on Sukhomlinsky. Available in English, German (*Wie man ein Kind lieben soll*), and Russian. Covers all ages but is more philosophical than practical.

### Alison Gopnik — *The Gardener and the Carpenter* (Farrar, Straus and Giroux)
Developmental psychologist at UC Berkeley. Her central metaphor: parenting is not carpentry (shaping a child to specification) but gardening (creating conditions for growth). Synthesizes recent research on infant cognition, play, and learning in an accessible way. A good modern complement to the classic theorists above.

## Tier P5 — Deep dives and supplementary

### Daniel Stern — *The Interpersonal World of the Infant* (Basic Books)
How infants experience subjective selfhood and develop a sense of self through interaction. Bridges psychoanalysis and empirical infant research. Dense and clinical, but offers the deepest available account of what's actually happening in a baby's inner world during the first two years. *(Direct sibling to Jessica Benjamin in Appendix H — see Part C.)*

### Adele Faber & Elaine Mazlish — *How to Talk So Kids Will Listen & Listen So Kids Will Talk* (Scribner)
Practical communication techniques based on Haim Ginott's work. Extremely concrete: specific scripts for acknowledging feelings, engaging cooperation, alternatives to punishment, encouraging autonomy, and handling praise. Most useful from about age 2 onward. The German edition (*So sag ich's meinem Kind*) is widely available.

### Peter Gray — *Free to Learn* (Basic Books)
Evolutionary psychologist's argument that children are biologically designed to learn through free play, and that the decline of free play is responsible for rising anxiety and depression in children. Draws on hunter-gatherer societies, the Sudbury Valley School model, and developmental psychology. A good complement to Renz-Polster's evolutionary framing.

### Gerald Hüther — *Jedes Kind ist hoch begabt* (Knaus)
German neuroscientist writing for a popular audience about how the brain develops in early childhood and how environment shapes neural architecture. Accessible, if sometimes oversimplified. His core message — that enthusiasm and intrinsic motivation are the key drivers of brain development, not drill — aligns with most of the authors above. Take the neuroscience claims with appropriate skepticism; the pedagogical conclusions are sound.

## Notes (parent track)

**On reading in original languages**: If you read Russian, Vygotsky (*Мышление и речь*), Sukhomlinsky (*Сердце отдаю детям*), and Elkonin (*Психология игры*) are all richer in the original. English translations of Soviet-era texts tend to either flatten the philosophical nuance or editorialize. Bulgarian editions of these works were widely published during the socialist period and may be findable in used bookshops or online.

**On Bulgarian pedagogy**: The Bulgarian educational tradition during the 20th century drew primarily on Soviet models (Vygotsky, Makarenko, Sukhomlinsky). The main Bulgarian name in educational psychology is Gencho Piryov (Генчо Пирьов), who founded the discipline in Bulgaria, but his works are academic and not translated. Reading the Soviet sources above essentially covers the intellectual foundation of Bulgarian pedagogical thinking.

**What's deliberately not on the parent track**: Waldorf/Steiner (interesting philosophy but the anthroposophical framework requires buy-in that may not suit you), behaviorist approaches (B.F. Skinner etc. — the evidence has moved on), and most of the popular parenting genre (sleep training debates, feeding guides, etc.) which are more practical parenting than education/pedagogy.

# PART B — THE EVIDENCE BASE AND ITS CONTROVERSIES *(academic layer)*

The parent track is a consensus canon — almost every author endorses the same romantic-developmentalist thesis (free play, autonomy, intrinsic motivation, non-interference, anti-acceleration). That consensus is selective. This part introduces the empirical camps the canon omits — the ones most relevant to a reader trained in causal identification, because they exist precisely to discipline the causal inferences the parent-track books make freely.

## B1 — Attachment, made empirical

### Mary Ainsworth et al. — *Patterns of Attachment* (1978)
The empirical operationalisation of Bowlby's theory: the **Strange Situation** procedure and the secure / avoidant / ambivalent classification. You cannot engage attachment theory as *evidence* (rather than as a framework) without Ainsworth — Bowlby (parent track) gives the theory, Ainsworth gives the measurement. Essential before forming any view on the attachment debates in the Frontier section.

### Jerome Kagan — *The Nature of the Child* (1984) / *Galen's Prophecy* (1994)
The temperament challenge: much of what attachment researchers attribute to caregiving may reflect the child's *inborn* temperament (reactivity, inhibition). Kagan is the standing reminder that infant differences are not a blank slate written by parenting. Read as the internal-developmental-psychology check on attachment's environmental claims.

## B2 — The identification correction (the camp the list most needs)

*The parent track is overwhelmingly environmentalist: it assumes parenting style causes child outcomes. The behavioural-genetics and econometric-parenting literatures are the correction — they are about the confound (parents transmit genes* and *environment) that the rest of the list ignores.*

### Judith Rich Harris — *The Nurture Assumption* (1998; rev. 2009)
The frontal challenge: twin and adoption studies show that shared-environment effects (the part of parenting that makes siblings alike) on adult personality and outcomes are surprisingly *small*; genes and the non-shared peer environment dominate. Harris is not an obscurantist — her argument is the most serious empirical case against the premise of the entire parent track. **The single most important omission from a parenting list compiled by an econometrician**, because it *is* the identification problem.

### Robert Plomin — *Blueprint: How DNA Makes Us Who We Are* (2018)
Behavioural genetics for a general reader, by its leading figure. The heritability of cognitive and personality traits, the rise of polygenic scores, and the claim that DNA is the largest *systematic* force in psychological development. Read alongside Harris; take the strong "parenting barely matters" framing as the position to argue *against*, not to adopt.

### Emily Oster — *Cribsheet: A Data-Driven Guide to Better, More Relaxed Parenting* (2019)
An economist applies causal-inference scrutiny (selection, confounding, what RCTs vs. observational studies can show) to the empirical parenting claims — breastfeeding, sleep, discipline. The natural entry point for *your* methodological reflexes; the book that models the epistemic stance the rest of the parent track lacks.

### Bryan Caplan — *Selfish Reasons to Have More Kids* (2011)
Uses the same twin/adoption literature as Harris/Plomin to argue parenting style matters far less for long-run outcomes than parents believe. Provocative and economist-authored; read as the deliberately overstated pole of the "how much does parenting matter?" debate.

## B3 — The science of learning (the opposition to discovery learning)

*Montessori, Piaget, Gray and the constructivist core of the parent track hold that children learn best by self-directed discovery. The cognitive-science-of-instruction literature disputes this directly and has strong evidence on its side, especially for academic content.*

### Kirschner, Sweller & Clark — "Why Minimal Guidance During Instruction Does Not Work" (*Educational Psychologist* 41(2), 2006)
The landmark critique of discovery, problem-based, and inquiry learning, grounded in cognitive-load theory and the architecture of working/long-term memory. The central empirical claim: novices learn better with explicit, guided instruction than with minimal-guidance discovery. The direct antithesis of the parent track's pedagogy.

### Daniel Willingham — *Why Don't Students Like School?* (2009)
Cognitive science of learning for educators: the primacy of background knowledge, the role of practice and memory, and why "learning styles" and pure skills-without-content approaches fail. Lucid and empirically careful.

### E.D. Hirsch — *Cultural Literacy* (1987) / *The Knowledge Deficit* (2006)
The knowledge-rich-curriculum case: comprehension and "critical thinking" depend on a broad base of shared factual knowledge, which child-centred, skills-first pedagogies systematically under-build. The curricular counter-position to Reformpädagogik.

### John Hattie — *Visible Learning* (2009)
The large meta-analytic synthesis of what affects achievement (effect sizes across hundreds of meta-analyses). Methodologically contested (averaging heterogeneous effect sizes), but the standard reference for "what the aggregate evidence says," and it does not favour pure discovery learning. Read critically — its statistics are exactly the kind you can interrogate.

## B4 — Structure, warmth, and parenting styles

### Diana Baumrind — parenting-styles research (1967, 1971, and later syntheses)
The empirical backbone the parent track elides: across decades of work, **authoritative** parenting (high warmth *and* high structure/demandingness) predicts better outcomes than **permissive** (warmth without demands) or **authoritarian** (demands without warmth). This matters because much of the parent track (Juul, Kohn, Faber-Mazlish) argues against demandingness as such; Baumrind's evidence is that structure *plus* warmth beats warmth alone. Read as the corrective, not the refutation. **The decisive nuance** is in Baumrind's later work — "Differentiating between Confrontive and Coercive Kinds of Parental Power-Assertive Disciplinary Practices" (*Human Development*, ~2012; and Baumrind, Larzelere & Owens, *Parenting: Science and Practice*, 2010 — *verify years*): *firmness per se is not harmful — coerciveness is.* Confrontive control (reasoned, firm, consistent, negotiable-within-a-limit) predicts competence; coercive control (arbitrary, punitive, psychologically controlling — shaming, love-withdrawal) predicts the opposite. Most of the parent track rejects exactly the *coercive* kind, which Baumrind also files under authoritarian — so the apparent clash is narrower than it looks (see chat discussion 2026-06-30). *(One genuine break: Baumrind held that mild power-assertion, in some analyses including spanking, was not demonstrably harmful inside authoritative warmth — contested terrain; the meta-analytic consensus, Gershoff, leans against, and every parent-track author rejects it.)*

### Wendy Grolnick — *The Psychology of Parental Control: How Well-Meant Parenting Backfires* (Erlbaum, 2003)
*(Parent-readable, despite living in the academic layer.)* The self-determination-theory refinement of Baumrind, and the most precise text on your exact question: it splits "demandingness" into **structure** (clear expectations, predictable consequences, information, scaffolding — supports the child) versus **control** (pressure, coercion, contingent regard — backfires), pairing both with autonomy-support and involvement. Dissolves the apparent Baumrind-vs-parent-track conflict: everyone endorses *structure*; the dispute was about *control*, under one word.

### The scholarly apparatus — Maccoby & Martin (1983), Darling & Steinberg (1993)
Eleanor Maccoby & John Martin, "Socialization in the context of the family" (*Handbook of Child Psychology*, 1983) — the canonical source for the two-dimensional 2×2 (demandingness × responsiveness) and the fourth, *neglectful*, cell; the dimensional framework usually attributed to "Baumrind" is in fact their reformulation of her three patterns. Nancy Darling & Laurence Steinberg, "Parenting style as context: an integrative model" (*Psychological Bulletin* 113(3), 1993) — distinguishes parenting *style* (the emotional climate) from *practices* (specific behaviours), which sharpens every claim above. Reference-grade; cite, don't necessarily read cover to cover.

# PART C — BRIDGE TO THE SOZIALPHILOSOPHIE PROGRAMME (spine E / Appendix H) *(academic layer)*

These are the readings where the practical/empirical material here docks with the normative-philosophical material in Appendix H. This is the seam along which a Phase-IV contribution on the human-capital-paradigm critique would be written.

### James Heckman & Flavio Cunha — "The Technology of Skill Formation" (*AER* 2007); Heckman, "Skill Formation and the Economics of Investing in Disadvantaged Children" (*Science* 2006) **[→ spine E]**
The economist's foundational entry to early-childhood development: the Perry Preschool and Abecedarian follow-ups, the "Heckman curve" (returns to human-capital investment are highest in early childhood), and the formal skill-formation production function with self-productivity and dynamic complementarity. **This is simultaneously the empirical pillar missing from the parent track and the *target* of the Appendix H flagship wedge** — the recognition-theoretic critique that Heckman has empirically rediscovered the decisiveness of early recognition relationships while theorising it in a vocabulary (investment, returns, skill-stock) that negates the *Bildung* and *Anerkennung* it stumbled onto.

### The toxic-stress / ACE literature — Felitti et al. (*Am. J. Preventive Medicine* 1998); Shonkoff & Phillips, eds., *From Neurons to Neighborhoods* (National Research Council, 2000); Harvard Center on the Developing Child working papers **[→ spine E]**
The Adverse Childhood Experiences study and the developmental-neurobiology of chronic stress. This is the *immanent pressure point* the Appendix H wedge uses: the evidence that adverse early relational environments leave durable biological and developmental marks is generated from *within* the human-capital paradigm, yet points beyond its vocabulary toward the recognition-relational account.

### Heinz-Joachim Heydorn — *Über den Widerspruch von Bildung und Herrschaft* (1970) **[→ spine E]**
The keystone primary text of spine E in the main programme (Appendix H). The dialectic of *Bildung* as emancipatory formation against its subsumption under domination/adaptation (*Halbbildung*). Read this against Heckman to see the two vocabularies — emancipatory formation vs. skill-investment — collide. *(Primary entry is in Appendix H; here it is the bridge pointer.)*

### Honneth (love sphere) and Jessica Benjamin (*The Bonds of Love*) — *cross-reference to Sozialphilosophie 7.1 and Appendix H* **[→ spine E]**
The recognition-theoretic and object-relations account of the earliest caregiving relationship. Daniel Stern (parent track, P5) is the empirical-infant-research sibling of this material; Benjamin and Honneth supply the normative category (recognition) that the human-capital vocabulary lacks. The triangulation — recognition theory × the *Bildung* tradition × the actual developmental/economic evidence on formation — is the contribution space the field map marks as currently unoccupied.

# FRONTIER: LIVE DEBATES *(academic layer)*

The parent track stages no controversies — its authors mostly agree. The field does not. Each debate below is a question with its opposing camps; most of the texts are already listed above. Work any one as a position-map if it grips you. Citation-uncertain items are flagged for verification.

**1. How much does parenting actually cause? (nature/nurture, the identification debate.)**
The entire parent track assumes parenting style shapes outcomes. Harris (*The Nurture Assumption*, B2), Plomin (*Blueprint*), and Caplan say the shared-environment effect is small and genes + peers dominate; the developmentalist canon (Bowlby, Hüther, the attachment tradition) assumes the opposite. The truth is regime-dependent (parenting matters more at the low end, for safety/trauma, and for *values* than for IQ/personality). *This is your home turf — it is a confounding problem, and the twin/adoption design is the identification strategy.*

**2. Discovery vs. direct instruction.**
Montessori, Piaget, Gray, Wild (Part A) vs. Kirschner–Sweller–Clark and Willingham (B3). Does self-directed discovery or explicit guided instruction produce better learning? The evidence tilts toward guided instruction for *academic content* with novices, while the discovery camp has the stronger case for *motivation, play, and the early years* — which is part of why the age-graded structure of this list matters.

**3. Attachment: universal or WEIRD?**
Orthodox attachment theory (Bowlby, Ainsworth, B1) claims a universal secure-base need; cross-cultural critics (Heidi Keller and colleagues — e.g. the universality-claim dispute, *PNAS* 2018 *(verify)*) argue the Strange Situation encodes Western middle-class norms and misclassifies other valid caregiving ecologies (multiple caregivers, proximal vs. distal parenting). Kagan (B1) adds the temperament confound.

**4. The "word gap."**
Hart & Risley, *Meaningful Differences in the Everyday Experience of Young American Children* (1995) — the "30-million-word gap" by SES — vs. the replication-and-critique literature (Sperry, Sperry & Miller, *Child Development* 2019 *(verify)*) disputing both the magnitude and the measurement. An identification-and-measurement debate about SES and early language, squarely in your methodological wheelhouse.

**5. Acceleration vs. amplification ("is earlier better?").**
The hinge between your economics and this canon: Heckman's investment logic (Part C) implies earlier and more is better (high early returns); Zaporozhets's *amplification* (Part A, P3), Gopnik, and Gray argue for enriching the *current* stage rather than rushing the next, and warn that academic acceleration in early childhood backfires. The list already half-contains this debate — it is the most productive one for you to actually work out, because both sides speak your language (returns vs. developmental timing).

**6. Smartphones, social media, and the youth mental-health crisis.**
Not relevant at 0–3 but the loudest current debate for the school years: Jonathan Haidt, *The Anxious Generation* (2024), argues a causal link between smartphone/social-media adoption and rising adolescent anxiety/depression; critics (Candice Odgers, Andrew Przybylski) argue the evidence is correlational and the effect sizes small. Peter Gray's play-decline thesis (Part A) is the adjacent, complementary claim. Park until the relevant age, but it is the debate most likely to matter by the time your son reaches it.
